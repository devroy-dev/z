# War, Security & Just War

*Subject-knowledge coach codex. Authored under the subject-knowledge variant: real named content; every Rule is a defensible fact; the normative verdict of a contested debate is taught in the Worked example (both sides shown), never keyed. The subject reasons from principles, doctrines, and near-consensus lessons rather than fast-moving specific conflicts.*

## Scope

This subject asks when the use of force is justified, how it may be waged, and how catastrophe is prevented — the intersection of ethics (may we kill, and whom?) and strategy (what actually keeps the peace?). It covers the just-war tradition and its rivals, the vocabulary of deterrence and security, the strategic and historical record, the live debates, and the recurring myths. It excludes the verdict of those debates — the learner reconstructs each position's strongest case and weighs magnitude, probability, and reversibility, rather than being told whether a given war or weapon is justified.

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** define a term; attribute a doctrine or work to its thinker; state the criteria or structure of a doctrine; identify the standard objection to a position; identify what a debate turns on; correct a common myth.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~35% recall (criteria, definitions, attributions), ~45% application (apply a criterion, state a mechanism, name the objection), ~20% multi-step (weigh magnitude × probability × reversibility; distinguish ethics from strategy).
- **In scope:** the just-war criteria and doctrines; each position's claim, strongest case, and objection; the strategic and historical record; the myths and corrections.
- **Out of scope:** the verdict of a contested debate. No item keys "was this intervention justified" or "should we ban this weapon." Casualty figures for specific conflicts are treated as contested estimates.
- **Classic traps:** conflating legality and morality; "deterrence works / must fail"; "intervention always works / always backfires"; misusing *in bello* proportionality as "equal force"; treating "just cause" as a trump; treating the ticking-bomb hypothetical as a realistic guide.

## §1 The three levels of just-war analysis

The structural spine of the whole tradition.

**Rule:** *Jus ad bellum* is the justice *of resorting to war* (when it is permissible to fight at all); *jus in bello* is justice *in the conduct of war* (how force may be used once fighting begins); *jus post bellum* is the ethics of *ending* war and building the peace.

**Worked example:** These levels are distinct and frequently confused: a war can be justly begun but unjustly waged, or unjustly begun but conducted within the *in bello* rules. Keeping them separate is the first discipline of the tradition — the questions "may you fight?" and "how may you fight?" and "how do you end it?" have different criteria and different answers.

**Trap:** Collapsing the levels — assuming a just cause (*ad bellum*) licenses any conduct (*in bello*), or that lawful conduct redeems an unjust war.

**Key facts:** *ad bellum* = may you fight · *in bello* = how you fight · *post bellum* = ending and rebuilding · the three are distinct.

## §2 The ad bellum criteria

The full set that must *all* hold for resort to war to be just.

**Rule:** The *ad bellum* criteria are just cause (typically self-defence or defence of others against grave wrong), legitimate authority (a competent public authority, not private actors), right intention (the aim must actually *be* the just cause), proportionality (expected good outweighs expected harm of the whole war), last resort (peaceful options exhausted or clearly futile), and reasonable prospect of success.

**Worked example:** A war is just only if the *whole set* holds. "The cause is just" is a fraction of the work: even a genuine just cause fails if the war is not a last resort, is disproportionate, or has no reasonable prospect of success. "Reasonable prospect" and "proportionality" require predicting the unpredictable, which is why the criteria are elastic and any side can claim to meet them.

**Trap:** Treating "just cause" as a trump — meeting *one* criterion does not make a war just; a debater who stops at "the cause is just" has done a fraction of the work.

**Key facts:** just cause + legitimate authority + right intention + proportionality + last resort + reasonable prospect · all must hold · just cause alone is insufficient.

## §3 The in bello criteria and double effect

How force may be used once fighting has begun.

**Rule:** The *in bello* criteria are discrimination / non-combatant immunity (target only combatants) and proportionality (force in a *given operation* must not cause harm *excessive relative to the concrete military advantage anticipated* — not "equal force"); the doctrine of double effect permits a foreseen-but-unintended harm only where the act is not wrong, the bad effect is not the *means* to the good, it is not *intended*, and it is proportionate.

**Worked example:** Double effect distinguishes deliberately targeting civilians (forbidden) from a lawful strike that foreseeably kills some (potentially permissible) — but only under all four strict conditions, so it is not a blanket excuse. *In bello* proportionality is the most-misused term in the domain: it means harm not *excessive relative to military advantage*, not equal casualties or equal force.

**Trap:** Misusing *in bello* proportionality as "equal force" or "the same number of casualties," and invoking double effect as a catch-all that automatically permits foreseen civilian deaths.

**Key facts:** discrimination = target combatants only · proportionality = harm not excessive relative to military advantage · double effect = four strict conditions, not a blanket excuse.

## §4 The moral equality of combatants and the security vocabulary

The traditional-vs-revisionist dispute and the strategic terms.

**Rule:** The moral equality of combatants (the traditional, Walzerian view) holds that soldiers on both sides have an equal *in bello* right to fight and be spared when *hors de combat*, regardless of whether their cause is just, because ordinary soldiers are not responsible for their government's decision; revisionists (McMahan) deny this.

**Worked example:** The strategic vocabulary sits alongside the ethics: deterrence prevents action by threatening unacceptable retaliation (needs credibility and capability); second-strike capability / MAD makes nuclear war irrational when both sides can retaliate after absorbing a first strike; counterforce (targeting forces) vs. countervalue (targeting cities) carries large moral and strategic consequences; the stability–instability paradox (nuclear parity deters all-out war but may embolden lower-level conflict); and Schelling's "threat that leaves something to chance" (deliberately introducing escalation risk to make a threat credible).

**Trap:** Treating soldiers' and civilians' deaths as morally identical — non-combatant immunity is central, and the moral-equality dispute (Walzer vs. McMahan) is *live*, not settled.

**Key facts:** moral equality of combatants = Walzer (traditional) vs. McMahan (revisionist) · deterrence = credibility + capability · MAD/second-strike · counterforce vs. countervalue · stability–instability paradox · Schelling's brinkmanship.

## §5 R2P, IHL, and the framing traditions

The doctrine of protection, the legal layer, and the definitional problems.

**Rule:** The Responsibility to Protect (R2P; ICISS report, 2001; endorsed at the 2005 UN World Summit) holds that sovereignty entails a responsibility to protect populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity, and the international community should act when a state manifestly fails — later framed as three pillars (the state's responsibility; international assistance; timely, decisive collective response).

**Worked example:** International humanitarian law (IHL — the Geneva and Hague traditions) codifies much of *jus in bello*: protection of civilians, prisoners, and the wounded, and prohibition of certain weapons and tactics. Legal and moral categories overlap but are not identical. Terrorism has no single agreed definition — the analytical question is whether it is defined by the *actor*, the *target*, or the *method* (deliberate violence against civilians for political ends) — and asymmetric/irregular warfare strains the whole combatant/civilian framework.

**Trap:** Conflating legality and morality — "it was legal, so it was just" and "it was illegal, so it was wrong" are both fallacies; IHL and the just-war tradition overlap but are not identical.

**Key facts:** R2P = ICISS 2001, endorsed 2005, three pillars · IHL = Geneva/Hague, codifies *in bello* · terrorism has no agreed definition (actor/target/method) · legal ≠ moral.

## §6 Position: the just-war tradition in full

**Rule:** The just-war tradition holds war is sometimes morally permissible but only under strict conditions, and even a just war must be fought justly — sitting *between* pacifism and realism.

**Worked example:** The lineage: Augustine (war as tragic necessity against grave wrong); Aquinas (three conditions — legitimate authority, just cause, right intention); the School of Salamanca (Vitoria, Suárez — the rights of non-Christian peoples and limits of conquest); Grotius (*On the Law of War and Peace*, 1625 — secularised into international law); Walzer (*Just and Unjust Wars*, 1977 — the canonical modern restatement, with a controversial "supreme emergency" exception). **Strongest case:** it takes *both* the horror of war *and* the reality of grave evils seriously, offering a disciplined framework rather than "anything goes" or "nothing goes," and it maps onto international law. **Objections:** the criteria are elastic (any side can claim just cause and last resort), and "reasonable prospect" and "proportionality" require predicting the unpredictable.

**Trap:** Cherry-picking one criterion; the tradition requires the *whole set*, and its own weakness is that the criteria are elastic.

**Key facts:** Augustine → Aquinas → Salamanca → Grotius (1625) → Walzer (1977) · strongest: disciplined middle path, maps to law · objection: elastic criteria, requires predicting the unpredictable.

## §7 Position: revisionist just-war theory

**Rule:** Revisionist just-war theory (Jeff McMahan, *Killing in War*, 2009) denies the moral equality of combatants, arguing killing in war is justified on the same basis as ordinary self-defence — *liability* — so soldiers fighting for an *unjust* cause are not moral equals of those resisting them, even if both obey the *in bello* rules.

**Worked example:** This collapses the neat firewall between *ad bellum* and *in bello*: on the revisionist view, whether you may kill depends on the justice of your cause, not merely on wearing a uniform. **Strongest case:** it tracks the intuition that aggressors and their victims are not moral equals and takes individual responsibility seriously. **Objection:** it is hard to operationalise (ordinary soldiers rarely know if their cause is just and cannot adjudicate it mid-war), and Walzerians argue the traditional equality is a vital *convention* that protects everyone by keeping the *in bello* rules symmetrical and enforceable.

**Trap:** Treating the moral-equality dispute as settled; the Walzer/McMahan clash is live, and each side has a serious case (workability vs. responsibility).

**Key facts:** McMahan, 2009 · killing = liability, not uniform · collapses the *ad bellum*/*in bello* firewall · strongest: aggressors ≠ victims · objection: soldiers can't judge their cause; equality is a protective convention.

## §8 Position: pacifism

**Rule:** Pacifism holds war is never (or virtually never) justified — either absolute (killing is intrinsically wrong) or contingent (modern war's scale and uncertainty make it almost never pay) — and includes principled *nonviolence* as a method of struggle (Gandhi's *satyagraha*, King).

**Worked example:** **Strongest case:** war's costs are so vast and outcomes so uncertain that the presumption against it should be near-absolute, and empirical work (Chenoweth & Stephan, *Why Civil Resistance Works*, 2011) finds non-violent campaigns have historically succeeded *more* often than violent ones. **Objections:** it appears to abandon victims of aggression and genocide ("let evil triumph rather than fight" is a hard bullet to bite); contingent pacifism collapses toward just-war theory once it admits *any* defensive force; and nonviolence's success record depends on conditions (a regime with some restraint, media, internal divisions) that do not always hold.

**Trap:** "Pacifism = passivity" — principled pacifism includes active non-violent *resistance* (Gandhi, King; the Chenoweth–Stephan evidence), though its success is conditional.

**Key facts:** absolute vs. contingent · nonviolence = active resistance (Gandhi, King) · Chenoweth & Stephan (2011): nonviolence often works better · objection: abandons atrocity victims; success is conditional.

## §9 Positions: realism and deterrence theory

**Rule:** Realism holds that in an anarchic world states act on necessity and interest, so moralising about war is naïve; deterrence theory holds the credible threat of unacceptable retaliation prevents war, and nuclear weapons may paradoxically keep the peace among great powers.

**Worked example:** *Realism* — **strongest:** predicts state behaviour and warns against moralised crusades that ignore consequences; **objection:** it cannot explain or condemn atrocity as *wrong*, only as imprudent. *Deterrence* — the lineage runs Brodie (*The Absolute Weapon*, 1946 — the bomb's purpose is to *avert* war), Schelling (credibility, commitment, brinkmanship, "the threat that leaves something to chance"), and Kahn (escalation); **strongest case:** the absence of direct great-power war since 1945; **objections:** deterrence requires *near-perfect reliability forever* — one miscalculation, accident, or irrational actor is catastrophic — and the Cold War's near-misses show it ran partly on luck.

**Trap:** Presenting deterrence as fail-safe *or* as a bluff that must fail; it held since 1945 *and* nearly failed on identifiable occasions.

**Key facts:** realism = necessity governs; can't condemn atrocity as wrong · deterrence = Brodie/Schelling/Kahn · strongest: long peace · objection: needs perfect reliability forever, ran on luck.

## §10 The strategic and historical record

**Rule:** The "long peace" (no direct great-power war since 1945, attributed partly to nuclear deterrence — Gaddis) is qualified by near-misses — the Cuban Missile Crisis (1962), the 1983 Able Archer scare, and Stanislav Petrov (1983) — which show deterrence "held, with luck," not that it is fail-safe.

**Worked example:** The record's near-consensus lessons: humanitarian intervention is *mixed* — non-intervention amid atrocity (Rwanda, 1994) is widely judged a grave moral failure, *and* interventions with chaotic aftermaths worsened outcomes, so success hinges on the specific case and a genuine plan for the peace (*jus post bellum*). R2P (2001, endorsed 2005) has been invoked and contested, with critics charging it became a pretext for regime change. On negotiating with armed groups, the distinction that matters is *tactical* engagement vs. *strategic legitimation*. On autonomous weapons, the concerns are the accountability gap (Sparrow, "Killer Robots," 2007), the erosion of meaningful human control, and a lowered threshold for force.

**Trap:** "Humanitarian intervention always works / always backfires" — the record is genuinely mixed; Rwanda (non-intervention as failure) and chaotic-aftermath interventions must *both* be on the table.

**Key facts:** long peace since 1945 (Gaddis) · Cuba 1962, Able Archer 1983, Petrov 1983 = "held on luck" · Rwanda 1994 = non-intervention failure · R2P 2001/2005 contested · Sparrow 2007 on autonomous weapons.

## §11 The recurring dilemmas: terrorism, torture, and the nuclear order

**Rule:** The negotiate-with-terrorists dilemma turns on tactical engagement vs. strategic legitimation; the ticking-time-bomb hypothetical is the standard pump for consequentialist-vs-absolutist ethics, but assumes certainties (guilt, the bomb, torture's efficacy) that never co-exist in reality.

**Worked example:** States face a genuine trade-off on terrorism: refusing to negotiate avoids incentivising hostage-taking, but *some* conflicts have ended only through talks that brought armed groups into politics — so the distinction is between crisis-specific, non-legitimising *tactical* engagement and recognising a group as a political actor (*strategic legitimation*). The ticking-bomb critique (Bufacchi, Arrigo) is that the scenario is *unrealistic* and that building institutions around it corrupts them; torture's reliability as intelligence is deeply doubted. The nuclear order (NPT regime, no-first-use debates, disarmament vs. modernisation) is a perennial backdrop.

**Trap:** Treating the ticking-bomb scenario as a realistic real-world guide — it smuggles in certainties that never co-exist, and institutionalising the exception corrupts the state.

**Key facts:** terrorism = tactical engagement vs. strategic legitimation · ticking bomb assumes impossible certainties (Bufacchi, Arrigo) · torture's reliability doubted · NPT regime strained.

## §12 Debate: humanitarian intervention without Security Council authorization

**Rule:** This question turns on legality vs. morality (does Council paralysis defeat legitimacy, or is unauthorised force a dangerous precedent?) and on the reasonable-prospect-of-success criterion applied to *this* intervention.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case: when a state is slaughtering its people and the Council is paralysed by a veto, a duty to prevent genocide can override the legal formality — sovereignty is not a licence for mass murder (R2P's moral core). The **con** case: bypassing the Council destroys the only agreed authority for force, sets a precedent powerful states abuse as a pretext for regime change, and interventions frequently worsen outcomes — "we must do *something*" is not a plan. Reward explicit weighing of the specific case's prospects and aftermath.

**Trap:** Assuming the intervention's own harms away; the reasonable-prospect and *jus post bellum* questions must be applied to the specific case, not waved aside.

**Key facts:** crux = legality vs. morality + reasonable prospect · R2P moral core vs. precedent/regime-change abuse · weigh the specific aftermath.

## §13 Debate: has nuclear deterrence made the world safer

**Rule:** This is the archetypal magnitude × probability debate — weigh a *low probability of unbounded catastrophe* against a *demonstrated record of peace*.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **safer** case: decades without great-power war under the nuclear shadow, and the certainty of catastrophic retaliation makes major aggression irrational as conventional deterrence never did. The **not-safer** case: "on balance safer" ignores tail risk — deterrence must work *every single time forever*, the Cold War's near-misses show it nearly failed on luck, and proliferation multiplies the fingers on triggers. The near-miss cases (Cuba, Petrov, Able Archer) are decisive for the con side; the long peace and the irrationality-of-aggression mechanism for the pro side.

**Trap:** "Deterrence is proven safe" or "proven to fail" — both over-read the record; the honest weighing is low-probability/unbounded-catastrophe against a real peace record.

**Key facts:** crux = magnitude × probability · long peace vs. tail risk + near-misses + proliferation · Cuba/Petrov/Able Archer.

## §14 Debate: should states never negotiate with terrorist organizations

**Rule:** This question turns on distinguishing *tactical* engagement from *strategic legitimation*, reasoning about the specific group and conflict rather than "always" or "never."

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **never** case: negotiation rewards violence, incentivises the next atrocity or hostage-taking, and legitimises groups that reject the rules of politics — a firm no-negotiation norm removes the payoff. The **sometimes** case: absolutism forecloses the talks that have actually *ended* some insurgencies and civil wars, refusing to talk can prolong violence, and the real question is *when and how*, not *never*.

**Trap:** Defending "always" or "never" instead of drawing the tactical/strategic-legitimation line for the specific group.

**Key facts:** crux = tactical engagement vs. strategic legitimation · removes the payoff vs. some wars end only in talks · reason about the specific group.

## §15 Debate: should lethal autonomous weapons be banned

**Rule:** This question turns on weighing claimed discrimination/precision gains against accountability and escalation costs, and testing whether a ban is enforceable.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **ban** case: removing meaningful human control over life-and-death crosses a moral line, creates an accountability gap (no one is answerable for a machine's killing — Sparrow), and lowers the threshold for war, so a preemptive ban (like the chemical/biological weapons norms) is prudent before proliferation. The **no-ban** case: autonomy could *reduce* civilian harm through precision and speed and protect one's own personnel, a ban is unverifiable and would cede the technology to those who ignore it, and it is better to regulate *meaningful human control*. Reward engagement with what "meaningful human control" actually requires.

**Trap:** Sloganeering about "killer robots" instead of engaging the specific mechanism (what meaningful human control requires) and enforceability.

**Key facts:** crux = precision gains vs. accountability/escalation + enforceability · accountability gap (Sparrow) vs. precision/verification · define meaningful human control.

## §16 Debate: do soldiers fighting an unjust cause do no wrong if they obey the laws of war

**Rule:** This question (the moral-equality-of-combatants debate) turns on the *workability* problem (can soldiers really judge their cause?) against the *responsibility* problem (does obedience erase moral agency?).

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **traditional (Walzer)** case: ordinary soldiers do not choose the war and cannot adjudicate its justice, so holding them individually liable is unfair and unworkable, and symmetrical *in bello* rules protect everyone by staying neutral on cause. The **revisionist (McMahan)** case: killing is justified by liability, not by wearing a uniform, so those advancing an unjust cause are not moral equals of those resisting them, and the convenient "equality" launders aggression. Reward the more consistently defended position, not the intuition the adjudicator shares.

**Trap:** Rewarding the intuition one personally shares; the debate is between two serious cases (workability vs. responsibility).

**Key facts:** crux = workability vs. responsibility · symmetrical rules protect everyone vs. liability not uniform · Walzer vs. McMahan.

## §17 Debate: is torture never justified, even to prevent an imminent mass-casualty attack

**Rule:** This question (the ticking-bomb debate) turns on weighing the absolutist/deontological line and the *institutional* argument (what permitting it does to the state) against the extreme-hypothetical consequentialist challenge.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **never** case: torture is an absolute wrong that dehumanises both victim and perpetrator, the ticking-bomb scenario is a fantasy assuming certainties that never exist, and institutionalising an exception corrupts the state and produces unreliable intelligence and routine abuse. The **rare-exception** case: if one genuinely faced certain knowledge and thousands of lives, an absolute prohibition would itself be a moral failure — the deontological line cannot survive a sufficiently extreme case. Reward the never-side that attacks the *realism* of the hypothetical and the exception-side that meets the institutional-corruption point.

**Trap:** Resting the argument on the fantasy — treating the ticking-bomb as a straightforward real-world guide rather than confronting its unrealism and the institutional-corruption critique.

**Key facts:** crux = absolutism + institutional corruption vs. extreme-hypothetical consequentialism · fantasy certainties · unreliable intelligence · a prohibition that fails the extreme case.

## §18 The domain's most error-prone claims

**Rule:** The domain's signature move is probabilistic risk-weighing — magnitude × probability × reversibility — and its two signature traps are the bloodless "surgical fix" optimist and the "all war is wrong" absolutist, each asserting an extreme instead of weighing.

**Worked example:** Walk the recurring myths. *Conflating legality and morality* — the tradition and IHL overlap but are not identical. *"Deterrence works / must fail"* — both over-read; it held since 1945 and nearly failed on identifiable occasions. *"Intervention always works / always backfires"* — the record is mixed (Rwanda; chaotic aftermaths). *Misusing "proportionality"* — *in bello* it means harm not excessive relative to military advantage, not equal force or casualties. *"Just cause" as a trump* — all criteria must hold, not one. *"Pacifism = passivity"* — it includes active non-violent resistance. *The ticking bomb as realistic* — it smuggles in impossible certainties. And inventing precise casualty figures for contested conflicts is a red flag; reason from the ethical structure.

**Trap:** Invoking double effect as a blanket excuse — foreseen civilian deaths are *not* automatically permissible; the harm must not be the *means* to the good and must be proportionate.

**Key facts:** signature move = magnitude × probability × reversibility · two traps = surgical-fix optimist + all-war-is-wrong absolutist · legality ≠ morality · proportionality ≠ equal force · just cause is not a trump · ticking bomb is a fantasy · casualty figures are contested.
