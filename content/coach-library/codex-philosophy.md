# Political Philosophy & Ethics

*Subject-knowledge coach codex. Authored under the subject-knowledge variant: real named content; every Rule is a defensible fact — a definition, attribution, or the correct description of a framework; the normative verdict of a contested debate is taught in the Worked example (both sides shown), never keyed. This subject is the least empirical of the ten: what is testable is whether a framework is described and applied correctly, not which value is right.*

## Scope

This subject supplies the moral and political-philosophical frameworks that underpin value debates: what makes an action right, what a just society owes its members, when the collective may override the individual, and what we owe to those absent from the room. It covers the ethical theories and theories of justice, the canonical thinkers and thought-experiments, the live debates, and the recurring myths. It excludes the verdict of those debates — the learner learns to describe and apply each framework consistently, not to memorise which framework wins.

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** define a concept; attribute a framework, work, or thought-experiment to its thinker; state the mechanism/structure of a framework; identify the standard objection to it; identify what a debate turns on; correct a common myth or misdescription.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~35% recall (definitions, attributions), ~45% application (describe a framework correctly, name its standard objection, match a thinker to a concept), ~20% multi-step (apply a framework to a case; identify opportunistic framework-switching).
- **In scope:** correct descriptions of each framework, its strongest case, and its standard objection; the canonical thinkers and thought-experiments; the myths and corrections.
- **Out of scope:** the verdict of a contested value debate. No item keys "is Rawls right" or "is equality of outcome better." "Fact-checking" here means checking whether a framework is described correctly.
- **Classic traps:** framework-label without content; opportunistic framework-switching; "utilitarianism just means the greatest good for the greatest number"; misstating the difference principle as equal outcomes; strawmanning libertarianism as selfishness; treating a thought-experiment as settling anything.

## §1 The three ethical frameworks

The foundational trio: outcomes, duties, character.

**Rule:** Consequentialism holds rightness depends only on outcomes (utilitarianism is the leading form — maximise aggregate welfare; Bentham, refined by Mill); deontology holds rightness depends on conformity to duties (Kant: act only on universalisable maxims; treat persons as ends, never merely as means); virtue ethics holds the right action is what a virtuous person would do (Aristotle: cultivate virtues and flourishing, *eudaimonia*).

**Worked example:** Faced with a hard choice, the three frameworks ask different questions: consequentialism, "which act produces the best outcome?"; deontology, "which act respects my duties and treats persons as ends?"; virtue ethics, "what would a person of good character do?" Utilitarianism splits into *act* (evaluate each act) and *rule* (evaluate acts by the rules that, generally followed, maximise welfare); Kant's *categorical imperative* and *humanity formula* anchor deontology; Aristotle's "golden mean" and *phronesis* (practical wisdom) anchor virtue ethics.

**Trap:** Naming a framework as a badge ("utilitarian," "Kantian") without deploying its actual machinery — aggregation and its objections; universalisability and ends-in-themselves.

**Key facts:** consequentialism = outcomes (Bentham/Mill); act vs. rule · deontology = duties (Kant); categorical imperative + ends-not-means · virtue ethics = character (Aristotle); the mean, *eudaimonia*, *phronesis*.

## §2 The core objections to consequentialism

The two objections that recur whenever welfare is aggregated.

**Rule:** The separateness of persons (Rawls, Nozick) is that aggregating welfare across people treats humanity as one super-person and can sacrifice individuals for the sum; the demandingness objection (Bernard Williams's "integrity" objection) is that consequentialism may require enormous, unrelenting sacrifice, leaving no room for personal projects.

**Worked example:** The separateness objection bites whenever a side aggregates welfare — utilitarianism can in principle justify sacrificing an innocent for a greater sum, ignoring that each person's life is distinct and their own. The demandingness objection bites at the other end: if you must *always* maximise the good, morality becomes self-effacing, with no space for the commitments that give a life meaning. Rule utilitarianism answers some objections by evaluating rules rather than acts, at the cost of either collapsing back toward act utilitarianism or hardening into rule-worship.

**Trap:** Ignoring the separateness of persons whenever a side is aggregating welfare across people — it is the decisive standard objection there.

**Key facts:** separateness of persons = Rawls/Nozick (no super-person) · demandingness = Williams's integrity objection · rule utilitarianism answers some, at a cost.

## §3 Liberty and paternalism

The vocabulary of when the state may override a person's choices.

**Rule:** Berlin ("Two Concepts of Liberty," 1958) distinguishes negative liberty (freedom *from* interference) from positive liberty (freedom *to* be one's own master); Mill's harm principle (*On Liberty*, 1859) permits restricting a competent adult's liberty only to prevent harm *to others*, not for their own good.

**Worked example:** Paternalism overrides a person's choices for their own good — *soft* paternalism intervenes only where choice is non-voluntary (poor information, incapacity, addiction), *hard* paternalism overrides even informed voluntary choices, and *libertarian paternalism* / "nudging" (Thaler & Sunstein) steers choices while preserving formal freedom. Berlin warned that positive liberty can be twisted to justify coercion "for your own good" — forcing people "toward their real selves."

**Trap:** Equivocating between negative and positive liberty (freedom-*from* vs. entitlement-*to*) when a debate about "freedom" is really about two different things.

**Key facts:** negative vs. positive liberty (Berlin, 1958) · harm principle = Mill (1859) · soft vs. hard paternalism · nudging = Thaler & Sunstein · Berlin's warning about positive liberty.

## §4 Justice devices and hard problems

Rawls's device, Dworkin's trumps, and Parfit's puzzle.

**Rule:** The veil of ignorance / original position (Rawls) holds that principles of justice are those we would choose for society not knowing our own place in it; rights as trumps (Dworkin) override ordinary welfare-maximising calculation; the non-identity problem (Parfit) is that policies affecting *which* people come to exist make it hard to say we "harmed" future people.

**Worked example:** Behind the veil, not knowing your class, talents, or gender, you choose principles you could live with in *any* position — which reframes self-interested claims as questions of fairness. Rights-as-trumps blocks trading away a right for a small aggregate gain. The non-identity problem complicates intergenerational ethics: since different choices produce different individuals, it is hard to say a specific future person was "harmed." The is/ought gap (Hume) — that one cannot straightforwardly derive an *ought* from an *is* — is frequently invoked, and often mis-invoked.

**Trap:** Mis-invoking the naturalistic fallacy / is-ought gap — not every move from facts to values is fallacious; check the invocation is apt.

**Key facts:** veil of ignorance = Rawls · rights as trumps = Dworkin · non-identity problem = Parfit · is/ought gap = Hume, often mis-invoked.

## §5 Framework: utilitarianism / consequentialism in full

**Rule:** Utilitarianism claims the right act or policy maximises aggregate well-being — the best net consequences.

**Worked example:** **Strongest case:** it is impartial (everyone's welfare counts equally, which grounded historic reforms — Bentham on animals, prisoners, women), practical (weigh costs and benefits), and reformist (it condemns cruelties defended by tradition). **Objections:** (1) it can justify sacrificing an innocent for the greater good (the separateness of persons); (2) it is extremely demanding; (3) welfare is hard to measure and compare across people; (4) it can license repugnant conclusions if the numbers align. Rule utilitarianism answers some by evaluating rules, at the cost of collapsing toward act utilitarianism or hardening into rule-worship.

**Trap:** "Utilitarianism just means the greatest good for the greatest number" — an oversimplification hiding the hard parts: aggregation, the separateness of persons, and the act/rule distinction.

**Key facts:** maximise aggregate welfare · strongest: impartial, practical, reformist · objections: separateness, demandingness, measurement, repugnant conclusions · rule vs. act.

## §6 Framework: deontology (Kantian) in full

**Rule:** Kantian deontology claims morality is a matter of duties and universalisable principles, with persons possessing dignity and never to be used *merely* as means, whatever the outcome.

**Worked example:** **Strongest case:** it protects the individual against being sacrificed for aggregate gain, grounds inviolable rights, and captures the intuition that some acts (torturing an innocent) are wrong *even if* they would produce good results. **Objections:** (1) rigidity — it seems to forbid lying even to a murderer at the door (Kant's own notorious example); (2) duties can conflict with no clear tie-breaker; (3) it can look coldly indifferent to consequences (*fiat justitia, ruat caelum*).

**Trap:** Treating "Kant forbids lying even to save a life" as a knockdown — it is a contested *implication*, and modern Kantians have responses; using it as an obvious reductio is lazy.

**Key facts:** duties + universalisability; ends not means · strongest: protects the individual, grounds rights · objections: rigidity, conflicting duties, coldness · the murderer-at-the-door case is contested.

## §7 Framework: virtue ethics in full

**Rule:** Virtue ethics claims we should focus on *being* a good person — cultivating courage, justice, temperance, and practical wisdom (*phronesis*) — rather than on a decision-procedure for acts.

**Worked example:** **Strongest case:** it matches how moral life is actually lived — character, habituation, judgment — and avoids both the rigidity of rules and the coldness of calculation. **Objections:** (1) it under-specifies what to *do* in a hard case ("do what the virtuous person would do" can be unhelpful); (2) virtues can conflict; (3) what counts as a virtue may be culturally relative. The modern revival runs through Anscombe and MacIntyre's *After Virtue*.

**Trap:** Expecting virtue ethics to yield a clean decision-procedure; its strength is character, and "do what the virtuous person would do" is deliberately not an algorithm.

**Key facts:** character over acts/outcomes · Aristotle; revival = Anscombe, MacIntyre · strongest: matches lived moral life · objection: under-specifies the hard case.

## §8 Framework: theories of justice — Rawls and Nozick

**Rule:** Rawls (*A Theory of Justice*, 1971) argues the original position yields equal basic liberties plus the *difference principle* — inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged; Nozick (*Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, 1974) argues a distribution is just if it arose through just acquisition and voluntary transfer, whatever the resulting pattern.

**Worked example:** *Rawls* — **strongest:** a principled account of *when* inequality is acceptable, grounded in what impartial choosers would pick; **objections:** why maximise the *minimum* (strong risk-aversion assumed), plus Nozick's liberty critique and communitarian critiques of the "unencumbered self." *Nozick* — the **Wilt Chamberlain argument:** any *patterned* theory (like Rawls's) is continually upset by voluntary choices, so preserving the pattern requires constant interference with liberty; only a minimal state is justified, and redistributive taxation is "on a par with forced labor"; **objections:** the theory of just acquisition is under-specified, real holdings are often tainted by force and fraud, and it can license severe deprivation.

**Trap:** "Rawls proves we should maximise equality" — the difference principle *permits* inequalities that benefit the worst-off; it is not a mandate for equal outcomes.

**Key facts:** Rawls = veil + difference principle (1971) · Nozick = entitlement + minimal state + Wilt Chamberlain (1974) · difference principle permits inequality · Nozick's acquisition theory under-specified.

## §9 Framework: capabilities, luck egalitarianism, and communitarianism

**Rule:** The capabilities approach (Sen, Nussbaum) holds justice concerns people's real *freedoms to do and be* what they value, not just resources; luck egalitarianism holds inequalities from *unchosen* bad luck are unjust while those from *choices* are not; communitarianism (MacIntyre, Sandel, Taylor, Walzer) holds the abstract choosing individual is a fiction and justice must be rooted in communities.

**Worked example:** *Capabilities* — people convert resources into functioning at different rates (a disabled person needs more to achieve the same mobility), so real freedom is the right metric; **objection:** which capabilities, and who lists them? *Luck egalitarianism* — correct for the lottery of birth and talent, not for choices; **objection:** it can be harshly moralistic (abandoning the "imprudent") and struggles to draw the choice/luck line. *Communitarianism* — Walzer's "spheres of justice" holds different goods are justly distributed by different principles; **objection:** it risks relativism and can shelter oppressive traditions.

**Trap:** Using "resources/income" metrics where they miss real freedom (disability, conversion factors) — the capabilities approach is the sharpest correction there.

**Key facts:** capabilities = real freedoms (Sen, Nussbaum) · luck egalitarianism = luck vs. choice · communitarianism = constituted selves, spheres of justice (Walzer) · each with its objection.

## §10 Canonical thinkers and thought-experiments

**Rule:** The canonical thought-experiments are reasoning tools that *test* principles, not proofs that *settle* them — the trolley problem (Foot; Thomson) pumps the consequentialist/deontological divide, and Singer's drowning child pumps duties to distant strangers.

**Worked example:** The trolley problem (divert a trolley to kill one and save five? push a person off a bridge to do the same?) probes whether it matters *how* the harm is brought about — but intuition-pumps test principles, they do not settle them, and can mislead. Singer's drowning child argues that if we can prevent something bad without sacrificing anything of comparable importance, we ought to, and physical distance is morally irrelevant. The social-contract lineage runs Hobbes (*Leviathan*, 1651; order over the state of nature), Locke (natural rights, government by consent), Rousseau (the general will).

**Trap:** Treating the trolley problem (or any thought-experiment) as settling anything — a round that becomes trolley-problem ping-pong without extracting the underlying principle is shallow.

**Key facts:** trolley problem = Foot/Thomson (consequentialist/deontological) · drowning child = Singer (beneficence) · pumps test, don't prove · contract lineage = Hobbes/Locke/Rousseau.

## §11 Debate: equality of outcome vs equality of opportunity

**Rule:** This question turns on *defining the equality metric* (welfare? resources? capabilities? real opportunity?) and defending it against the standard objection.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **outcome** case: formal equality of opportunity is hollow when starting points are radically unequal — background, wealth, and unchosen luck shape outcomes so heavily that only attention to results delivers real fairness (Rawlsian and luck-egalitarian intuitions). The **opportunity** case: equalising outcomes erases desert, agency, and incentive, punishes effort and choice, and requires continual coercive interference with liberty (Nozick's Wilt Chamberlain point). Strong debaters often converge on *fair* equality of opportunity plus a floor.

**Trap:** Arguing without defining the equality metric, or ignoring the standard objection to your side (incentive/liberty cost for outcome-egalitarians; "hollow formal equality" for opportunity-egalitarians).

**Key facts:** crux = define the equality metric · hollow formal equality vs. incentive/liberty cost · common convergence: fair opportunity + a floor.

## §12 Debate: may the state prevent competent adults from harming only themselves

**Rule:** This question turns on locating the precise condition that licenses intervention (incapacity, non-voluntariness, externalities, internalities) rather than arguing blanket liberty or blanket protection.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **anti-paternalism** case: sovereign adults own their lives, the harm principle bars coercion for one's "own good," and the power to protect people from themselves is the power to impose the majority's values on private choices. The **paternalism** case: "purely self-regarding" harm is rarer than it looks (third-party and social costs, dependents), and addiction, misinformation, and cognitive bias undermine the "competent, informed choice" premise, so soft paternalism intervenes only where autonomy is already compromised. Penalise a paternalist who cannot limit the principle (it would justify banning anything risky) and an anti-paternalist who pretends no choice has spillovers.

**Trap:** Arguing blanket liberty or blanket protection instead of naming the precise condition (incapacity, non-voluntariness, externality, internality) that licenses intervention.

**Key facts:** crux = the precise licensing condition · sovereignty/harm-principle vs. compromised autonomy + spillovers · soft vs. hard paternalism.

## §13 Debate: a Rawlsian society vs a libertarian one

**Rule:** This question turns on internal consistency and engagement with the *best* version of the rival — does the Rawlsian answer the liberty objection, does the libertarian answer tainted-acquisition and severe-deprivation?

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **Rawlsian** case: behind the veil, rational people would insure against ending up worst-off, so a just society improves the position of the least advantaged, not just the aggregate or the lucky. The **libertarian** case: justice concerns how holdings *arose* (just acquisition and voluntary transfer), not whether they match a pattern, and enforcing Rawlsian distribution requires continually overriding free choices and treats talents as collective property. Reward internal consistency, not the society the adjudicator personally prefers.

**Trap:** Rewarding whichever society one personally prefers, or caricaturing the rival ("libertarianism = selfishness"; "Rawls = equal outcomes") instead of engaging its best version.

**Key facts:** crux = consistency + engaging the best rival · veil/difference principle vs. just acquisition/liberty · Rawls vs. the liberty objection; Nozick vs. tainted acquisition.

## §14 Debate: do future generations have rights that override the present

**Rule:** This question turns on the non-identity problem and on *how* to weigh present against future (discounting, thresholds, a duty to *sustain* rather than a right held by specific future persons).

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case: we routinely bind the future (debt, climate, nuclear waste), impartiality across time is as compelling as across space, and discounting future people's interests to near-zero is arbitrary. The **con** case: the non-identity problem complicates "rights" (our choices determine *who* exists), rights are usually held by actual identifiable persons, and present people have real, weighty claims too. A debater who has never heard of the non-identity problem is under-equipped here.

**Trap:** Ignoring the non-identity problem — it is the central complication for any intergenerational "rights" claim.

**Key facts:** crux = non-identity problem + how to weigh present/future · we already bind the future vs. rights need identifiable holders · duty to sustain vs. rights held by specific future persons.

## §15 Debate: do we owe as much to distant strangers as to those close to us

**Rule:** This question turns on weighing impartial beneficence against special obligations and demandingness.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro (Singer)** case: if we can prevent something bad without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought to, and physical distance is morally irrelevant (the drowning-child logic scaled up). The **con** case: special obligations (to family, community, compatriots) are real and constitutive of the moral life, and a duty this demanding is self-effacing and ignores the legitimate partiality that gives life meaning (the integrity objection). Reward whoever engages the strongest version of the other rather than caricaturing it.

**Trap:** Caricaturing either Singer's rigour or the partiality reply instead of engaging its strongest form.

**Key facts:** crux = impartial beneficence vs. special obligations + demandingness · drowning child (Singer) vs. integrity/partiality (Williams).

## §16 The domain's most error-prone claims

**Rule:** Because this domain is conceptual, its central sin is *opportunistic framework-switching* — invoking rights when consequences are inconvenient and consequences when rights are inconvenient — and consistency is the coin of the realm.

**Worked example:** Walk the recurring misdescriptions. *Framework-label without content* — naming "utilitarian" or "Kantian" as a badge without the machinery. *"Utilitarianism just means the greatest good for the greatest number"* — hides aggregation, the separateness of persons, act/rule. *"Kant forbids lying even to save a life" as a knockdown* — a contested implication with modern responses. *"Rawls proves we should maximise equality"* — the difference principle *permits* inequalities benefiting the worst-off. *"Libertarianism = selfishness / anarchy"* — Nozick's is a rigorous rights-based position (self-ownership, just transfer), not greed. *Treating the trolley problem as settling anything* — pumps test principles, they don't prove them. And confusing negative and positive liberty, or mis-invoking the naturalistic fallacy, are recurring slips.

**Trap:** Asserting a value as "self-evident" — the domain's job is to *defend* values, not assert them; "obviously X is good" with no argument is exactly what should lose.

**Key facts:** opportunistic framework-switching = the cardinal sin · consistency is the coin of the realm · the difference principle permits inequality · thought-experiments test, don't prove · defend values, don't assert them.
