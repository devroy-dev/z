# Geopolitics & the Changing World Order

*Subject-knowledge coach codex. Authored under the subject-knowledge variant: real named content; every Rule is a defensible fact; the normative verdict of a contested debate is taught in the Worked example (both sides shown), never keyed.*

## Scope

This subject is power among states in a world with no overarching authority. It covers the vocabulary of power and order, the competing theories of international relations, the factual record from the Peloponnesian War to the multipolar present, the live debates, and the recurring myths. It excludes the verdict of those debates — the learner reconstructs each theory's strongest case and names what a question turns on, rather than being told which polarity is safer or whether the liberal order is worth defending.

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** define a term; attribute a theory or concept to its thinker; state the mechanism a theory proposes; identify the standard objection to a school; identify what a debate turns on; correct a common myth.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~35% recall (definitions, attributions, dates), ~45% application (state the mechanism, match a theory to its claim, name the objection), ~20% multi-step (identify which logic a case activates; distinguish framing from fact).
- **In scope:** definitions; each school's claim, mechanism, strongest case, and objection; the dated record; the myths and corrections.
- **Out of scope:** the verdict of a contested debate. No item keys "is multipolarity more dangerous" or "is the liberal order worth defending."
- **Classic traps:** treating the Thucydides Trap or the Westphalian origin as settled; overstating the democratic peace from dyadic to monadic; "deterrence always works / never works"; "sanctions work / never work"; treating one IR school as obviously correct.

## §1 Anarchy, self-help, and the security dilemma

The structural premise of the field and the spiral it generates.

**Rule:** Anarchy in IR means the absence of a central authority above states (not chaos); under self-help each state must ultimately provide its own security, and the security dilemma (John Herz; developed by Robert Jervis, 1978) is that measures one state takes to feel safe make others feel threatened, prompting countermeasures.

**Worked example:** Because there is no world government to enforce agreements, a state arms or allies for defence — but a neighbour cannot read intentions, only capabilities, so it arms too, and both end less secure though both preferred peace. Jervis distinguished a *spiral model* (arming provokes) from a *deterrence model* (weakness provokes); knowing which situation you are in is the crux, and getting it wrong is how wars start.

**Trap:** Assuming without argument that a rival is either an aggressor to be deterred or a frightened state to be reassured — the whole difficulty (Jervis) is telling which you face.

**Key facts:** anarchy = no central authority, not chaos · self-help · security dilemma = Herz/Jervis (1978) · spiral vs. deterrence model.

## §2 Balance of power and polarity

How order is argued to arise from the distribution of power.

**Rule:** Balance of power holds that states align to prevent any one power dominating — balancing (joining against the strong) vs. bandwagoning (joining the strong); polarity is the number of great powers (unipolar, bipolar, multipolar), each argued to have different stability properties.

**Worked example:** In multipolar systems two characteristic failure modes appear: *buck-passing* (hoping someone else checks the threat) and *chain-ganging* (being dragged into an ally's war). Whether more poles means more danger is contested — Waltz argued *bipolarity* was most stable (two players, clear balancing, fewer miscalculations), so "unipolar = safe" is not obvious.

**Trap:** Debating polarity without supplying the *mechanism* by which a given structure causes war or peace (miscalculation, overreach, balancing failure) — gesturing at "balance" or "primacy" is not an argument.

**Key facts:** balancing vs. bandwagoning · unipolar/bipolar/multipolar · buck-passing + chain-ganging = multipolar failure modes · Waltz: bipolarity most stable.

## §3 Power, hegemony, and transition

The forms of power and the theories of hegemonic order and its challenge.

**Rule:** Joseph Nye distinguishes hard power (coercion), soft power (attraction), and smart power (combining them); hegemonic stability theory (Kindleberger, Gilpin) holds that open order is easier to sustain when one dominant power underwrites it; power-transition theory (Organski) holds war is likeliest when a *dissatisfied* rising power nears parity with the ruling power.

**Worked example:** The pop-culture version of power transition is Graham Allison's "Thucydides Trap" (*Destined for War*, 2017). Its logic: an anxious incumbent and a dissatisfied riser near parity is the classic recipe for war. Note this describes a *risk*, not a destiny — the sample of historical cases is selective and the causation contested.

**Trap:** Citing the Thucydides Trap as an iron law. It is a useful heuristic describing a risk, not proof of inevitability; catch debaters who treat it as destiny.

**Key facts:** Nye = hard/soft/smart power · hegemonic stability = Kindleberger/Gilpin · power transition = Organski · Thucydides Trap = Allison (2017), a risk not a law.

## §4 Deterrence and the nuclear paradoxes

The strategic vocabulary of preventing war by threat.

**Rule:** Deterrence prevents an action by threatening unacceptable retaliation and requires credibility and capability; second-strike capability (retaliating after absorbing a first strike) underpins mutually assured destruction (MAD), while the stability–instability paradox holds that nuclear parity makes all-out war less likely but *limited* conflict more likely.

**Worked example:** Extended deterrence (protecting allies under one's nuclear umbrella) raises a credibility problem: will A really risk its own cities for B? Schelling (*Arms and Influence*, 1966) is foundational; the stability–instability paradox explains why nuclear rivals may still skirmish at low levels, each believing escalation will be capped. Alliance dilemmas (Snyder) pair *entrapment* (dragged into an ally's war) against *abandonment* (left exposed) — the two fears that haunt every alliance.

**Trap:** "Deterrence always works" (it held through the Cold War partly on luck) or "never works" (which ignores the great-power long peace) — both over-read the record.

**Key facts:** credibility + capability · second-strike/MAD · stability–instability paradox · extended deterrence credibility problem · entrapment vs. abandonment (Snyder) · Schelling foundational.

## §5 Sovereignty and the ordering principles

The units of the system and the rival principles of order.

**Rule:** Sovereignty is supreme authority within territory and formal equality among states; two rival ordering principles compete — spheres of influence (great powers dominating their neighbourhoods) vs. universal rules (one set of rules applied to all) — with the post-1945 "liberal international order" the leading instance of the latter.

**Worked example:** The liberal international order is the system of open trade, multilateral institutions, and aspirationally liberal norms underwritten by US power — the thing now widely argued to be fraying. Its "Westphalian" sovereignty pedigree is itself contested (Osiander): sovereignty evolved over centuries rather than springing cleanly from 1648.

**Trap:** The Westphalian myth — asserting 1648 cleanly created modern sovereignty. It is partly a 20th-century retrofit; sovereignty evolved gradually.

**Key facts:** sovereignty = territorial supremacy + formal equality · spheres vs. universal rules · liberal order = open trade + institutions + US power · Westphalian origin contested.

## §6 School: realism

**Rule:** Realism claims states are the main actors, pursuing power and security in an anarchic self-help system where morality and institutions are thin constraints.

**Worked example:** The variants: *classical realism* (Morgenthau, *Politics Among Nations*, 1948; Thucydides) roots competition in human nature ("the strong do what they can"); *neorealism* (Waltz, 1979) roots it in the *structure* of anarchy and argues bipolarity is most stable; *offensive realism* (Mearsheimer, 2001) says great powers seek regional hegemony, making US–China rivalry structurally likely; *defensive realism* says states seek *enough* security; *neoclassical realism* adds domestic variables. **Strongest case:** it explains recurring balancing and rivalry across very different cultures and eras. **Standard objection:** it under-predicts cooperation and the democratic peace, treats preferences as fixed, and did not predict the peaceful end of the Cold War.

**Trap:** "Realism is just true" — it captures part of reality but under-predicts durable cooperation and change.

**Key facts:** Morgenthau (classical), Waltz (neorealism/bipolarity), Mearsheimer (offensive), defensive/neoclassical variants · strongest: recurring rivalry · objection: missed cooperation and the Cold War's end.

## §7 School: liberalism / liberal institutionalism

**Rule:** Liberalism claims cooperation is achievable because institutions, trade interdependence, and domestic politics shape state behaviour beyond raw power.

**Worked example:** The strands (Doyle): *institutional liberalism* (Keohane, *After Hegemony*, 1984) — institutions reduce transaction costs, provide information, and raise the cost of defection; *commercial liberalism* — trade interdependence raises the cost of war; *republican liberalism / democratic peace* (Doyle, building on Kant) — democracies very rarely fight *each other*. **Strongest case:** the density of postwar institutions and the near-absence of war among mature democracies. **Standard objection:** institutions may reflect rather than constrain power (Mearsheimer's "False Promise," 1994–95); great powers ignore rules when core interests demand.

**Trap:** "Institutions solve everything" — they raise the cost of defection but great powers still defect when core interests conflict; institutions are conditional.

**Key facts:** Keohane (institutionalism), Doyle (democratic peace) · institutions cut transaction costs · trade raises war's cost · strongest: postwar institutions + democratic peace · objection: institutions may just reflect power.

## §8 School: constructivism

**Rule:** Constructivism claims interests and identities are socially constructed, not fixed by structure — Wendt's "Anarchy Is What States Make of It" (1992): the same anarchy can produce a Hobbesian, Lockean, or Kantian culture depending on shared ideas.

**Worked example:** Where realism sees a fixed structural logic, constructivism explains change realism cannot — the end of the Cold War *without* war, the transformation of enemies into partners, the spread of norms against conquest and chemical weapons. **Strongest case:** these changes and norm shifts. **Standard objection:** it is strong on *how ideas matter* but weaker on prediction, and can under-weight material power.

**Trap:** Using constructivism to dismiss material power entirely; its strength is explaining change, not predicting it.

**Key facts:** Wendt (1992) · identities/interests socially constructed · trump card = peaceful Cold War end · objection: weak on prediction, under-weights material power.

## §9 Schools: the English School, Marxist/critical, and the professional move

**Rule:** The English School (Hedley Bull, *The Anarchical Society*, 1977) holds there is an "international society" of states bound by shared rules even under anarchy; Marxist/world-systems and critical theories hold the order reflects dominant classes and core economies.

**Worked example:** The English School is a middle path between pure realism's "international system" and cosmopolitan liberalism's "world community" — sovereignty, diplomacy, international law, and the balance of power function as shared institutions. Feminist and postcolonial IR argue mainstream theory naturalises particular (gendered, Western) vantage points. The professional move is rarely to declare for one school but to identify *which logic a specific case activates* — a structural rivalry (realism), an institutionalised cooperation problem (liberalism), or a norm shift (constructivism).

**Trap:** Treating one IR school as obviously correct; each captures part of reality, and dismissing the others wholesale is over-reaching.

**Key facts:** English School = Bull (1977), international society · Marxist/critical = order serves the strong · professional move = identify which logic the case activates.

## §10 The classical and 19th-century record

**Rule:** The Peloponnesian War and Melian Dialogue (5th c. BCE) are the founding realist text; the Congress of Vienna (1815) and the Concert of Europe produced an unusual stretch without general war — the classic case for managed multipolar balance.

**Worked example:** Thucydides' account originates the "rising power vs. ruling power" and "might makes right" motifs. Bismarck's later-19th-century alliance system is the case study in how skilful balancing preserves peace — and how its collapse unleashes war. The Peace of Westphalia (1648) is conventionally cited as the origin of the sovereign-state system, though "Westphalian sovereignty" is partly a later myth.

**Trap:** Citing Vienna/Concert as proof multipolarity is safe while ignoring that its collapse produced 1914; the same era illustrates both managed balance and its failure.

**Key facts:** Melian Dialogue = realism's origin · Vienna 1815, Concert of Europe · Bismarck's alliance system · Westphalia 1648 (contested origin).

## §11 The World Wars and the interwar period

**Rule:** The origins of the First World War (1914) are the central battleground for overdetermination vs. contingency and the classic case of chain-ganging; the interwar period and the League of Nations (1919–1939) are multipolarity's cautionary tale.

**Worked example:** Was the 1914 system so primed — alliance blocs, arms races, mobilisation timetables — that war was near-inevitable, or did the assassination and specific choices genuinely tip it? The interwar years of failed collective security and appeasement are heavily cited by multipolarity's critics as evidence of its instability.

**Trap:** Treating 1914 as cleanly caused by the assassination *or* cleanly overdetermined; it is the standard case where both must be weighed.

**Key facts:** WWI 1914 = overdetermination-vs-contingency + chain-ganging · League 1919–1939 · interwar = failed collective security + appeasement.

## §12 The Cold War and its near-misses

**Rule:** The Cold War (c. 1947–1991) was US–Soviet bipolarity under nuclear deterrence; John Lewis Gaddis called the absence of great-power war the "long peace," which the near-misses (Cuban Missile Crisis 1962; Able Archer 1983; the Petrov incident 1983) qualify as "held, partly on luck."

**Worked example:** Containment (Kennan's "Long Telegram," 1946) framed US strategy. The Cuban Missile Crisis is the definitive case for deterrence, brinkmanship, and the stability–instability paradox at once; Petrov (a Soviet officer who chose *not* to report a false missile-warning as a real attack) and Able Archer (a NATO exercise mistaken by some Soviet leaders for a possible first strike) are the standard evidence that the peace ran partly on luck.

**Trap:** Presenting deterrence as fail-safe; the near-misses show it held *with* documented close calls, not that it is guaranteed.

**Key facts:** c. 1947–1991 · Kennan containment (1946) · "long peace" (Gaddis) · Cuba 1962, Able Archer 1983, Petrov 1983 = "held, on luck."

## §13 The unipolar moment and its challenges

**Rule:** With the USSR's collapse (1989–1991) the US became sole superpower — Krauthammer's "unipolar moment" — while the end of the Cold War *without* great-power war is constructivism's trump card against pure structural realism, which largely failed to predict it.

**Worked example:** Wohlforth argued unipolarity could be stable and durable; Fukuyama's "The End of History?" (1989) argued liberal democracy had won the *ideological* contest (routinely strawmanned — see below). Then 9/11 (2001) redirected US strategy toward counter-terrorism and costly interventions, China's rise (WTO accession 2001, rapid economic and military expansion) produced a peer competitor, and the 2008 financial crisis accelerated talk of relative decline.

**Trap:** Fukuyama's "end of history" strawman — he did not claim events or conflict would stop; he argued liberal democracy had won as the endpoint of political *legitimacy*.

**Key facts:** unipolar moment = Krauthammer · Cold War end = constructivism's trump · Wohlforth (durable unipolarity), Fukuyama (ideological, not literal, "end") · 9/11 2001, China's rise, 2008 crisis.

## §14 The multipolar turn and institutional strain

**Rule:** Russia's revisionism (Georgia 2008, Crimea 2014, the full Ukraine invasion 2022) is the sharpest recent challenge to the norm against territorial conquest; institutional strain (the WTO Appellate Body paralysed from December 2019; recurrent UN Security Council deadlock) marks a fraying rules-based order.

**Worked example:** BRICS expansion, middle-power hedging, and Global South "non-alignment 2.0" mark a system no longer cleanly unipolar. On sanctions, the scholarly record (Hufbauer and colleagues) suggests they achieve their stated aims perhaps around a third of the time, with wide variation and serious methodological disputes — so neither "sanctions work" nor "sanctions never work" is defensible as stated.

**Trap:** Asserting "US decline," "the liberal order is collapsing," or "the world is multipolar now" as settled fact — these are contested *interpretations* to be argued, not data.

**Key facts:** Crimea 2014, Ukraine 2022 = conquest-norm challenge · WTO Appellate Body paralysis 2019 · sanctions ~⅓ success (contested) · multipolar turn = framing, not fact.

## §15 Debate: is a multipolar world more dangerous than a unipolar one

**Rule:** This question turns on the mechanism by which a given polarity causes war or peace, and often hides a definitional move about *which* alternative pole-structure is the comparison.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **more-dangerous** case: more great powers means more dyads that can miscalculate, more shifting alliances (interwar chain-ganging and buck-passing), and no single underwriter of order; history's multipolar eras produced the world wars. The **less-dangerous** case: unipolarity breeds unchecked hegemonic overreach and resentment; balanced multipolarity restrains any single aggressor (the Concert of Europe), and Waltz argued *bipolarity*, not unipolarity, was most stable — so "unipolar = safe" is not obvious.

**Trap:** Gesturing at "balance" or "primacy" without a causal mechanism; the debate often smuggles in an undefined comparison structure.

**Key facts:** crux = mechanism of war/peace per polarity · miscalculation/chain-ganging vs. overreach · Waltz favoured bipolarity · hidden definitional move.

## §16 Debate: do institutions constrain the weak more than the strong

**Rule:** This question turns on whether institutions are conditional — effective where great-power interests partly align, weak where they sharply conflict.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **constrain-the-weak** case: great powers wield vetoes, ignore adverse rulings, and shape rules to their advantage, binding small states while the powerful defect at will (Mearsheimer; world-systems). The **constrain-the-strong** case: institutions raise the *cost* of great-power defection (reputational, coordination), give small states voice and information, and have restrained even powerful states on many second-order issues (Keohane). The strong line concedes conditionality and specifies *which* institution and *which* issue.

**Trap:** Generalising; the defensible position specifies the institution and the issue rather than claiming institutions uniformly bind one side.

**Key facts:** crux = institutions are conditional · vetoes and rule-shaping vs. raised defection costs · specify which institution/issue.

## §17 Debate: are the US and China destined for conflict (the Thucydides Trap)

**Rule:** This question turns on weighing structural power-transition pressure against nuclear deterrence and economic interdependence.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **destined** case: a dissatisfied riser nearing parity with an anxious incumbent is the classic recipe for war; structural pressure outweighs good intentions. The **not-destined** case: nuclear deterrence, deep interdependence, and the absence of a WWI-type territorial casus belli make major war very costly and avoidable, and Allison's cases are selectively chosen — the "trap" is a heuristic, not a law.

**Trap:** Treating the Thucydides Trap as deterministic *or* dismissing structural risk entirely; both over-read.

**Key facts:** crux = structural pressure vs. deterrence + interdependence · Allison's cases selective · a risk, not a destiny.

## §18 Debate: as a middle power, formally align or hedge

**Rule:** This question turns on the specific state's geography, threats, and economic ties, weighed through the entrapment/abandonment trade-off.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **align** case: alignment buys credible security guarantees and market access; hedging leaves a state exposed and friendless in a crisis (the abandonment fear). The **hedge** case: hedging preserves autonomy, avoids being dragged into others' wars (the entrapment fear), and maximises leverage by keeping options open (India's strategic autonomy; ASEAN centrality). Judge against the specific state's actual position, not a generic template.

**Trap:** Reasoning from a generic template rather than the specific state's geography, threats, and ties.

**Key facts:** crux = the specific state + entrapment/abandonment · guarantees vs. autonomy · India/ASEAN as hedging exemplars.

## §19 Debate: is the rules-based liberal order worth defending

**Rule:** This question turns on separating the *descriptive* question (how rules-based was it really?) from the *normative* one (worth defending versus the realistic alternative?).

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **defend** case: it underwrote the longest great-power peace and the largest rise in prosperity in history, and its erosion invites spheres-of-influence bullying of the weak. The **skeptical** case: the "order" was always selective — it constrained the weak while its architect broke its own rules — so defending it can mean defending hegemony dressed as principle, and a genuinely multipolar order may be fairer. Reward whoever names the concrete alternative order rather than debating an abstraction.

**Trap:** Debating an abstraction; the sharp version names the concrete alternative order being compared.

**Key facts:** crux = descriptive vs. normative · longest peace + prosperity vs. selective/hegemonic · name the alternative order.

## §20 The domain's most error-prone claims

**Rule:** The domain's two most-cited and most-overstated items are the Thucydides Trap (a risk, not an iron law) and the Westphalian origin of sovereignty (partly a later retrofit, not a tidy 1648 origin).

**Worked example:** Walk the recurring myths. *"Democracies never go to war"* — the robust finding is *dyadic* (mature democracies rarely fight *each other*); the *monadic* claim (democracies are generally more peaceful) is weaker, and "democracy" definitions do heavy lifting. *"Deterrence always works / is a bluth that must fail"* — both over-read; it held since 1945 *and* nearly failed on identifiable occasions (Cuba, Petrov, Able Archer). *"Sanctions work / never work"* — the record is mixed and case-dependent (targeted vs. comprehensive; against what goal). *"Fukuyama said nothing would ever happen again"* — a misreading of a claim about *ideological* legitimacy. And keep power (a state *can* enforce a rule) distinct from legitimacy (whether the rule is *accepted*).

**Trap:** Asserting contested framings — "US decline," "the liberal order is collapsing," "the world is multipolar now" — as settled facts rather than interpretations to be argued.

**Key facts:** Thucydides Trap + Westphalian myth = most overstated · democratic peace is dyadic not monadic · deterrence "held with near-misses" · sanctions ~⅓ (contested) · power ≠ legitimacy · framings are not facts.
