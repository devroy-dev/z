# Global Economy: Trade, Development & Growth

*Subject-knowledge coach codex. Authored under the subject-knowledge variant of the coach authoring standard: real named content (neutrality waived); every Rule is a defensible fact — a definition, attribution, mechanism, a school's stated position, a dated event, or the crux a contested question turns on; the normative verdict of a contested debate is taught in the Worked example (both sides shown) but is never the basis of a quiz key.*

## Scope

This subject is how nations grow rich, why some stay poor, and what policy can do about it. It covers the core vocabulary of trade and development, the competing theoretical schools, the factual record of the great growth episodes and failures, the live policy debates, and the recurring myths that circulate as settled fact. It deliberately excludes the normative resolution of those debates: the learner is taught to reconstruct the strongest case on each side and to name what a question turns on — not to memorise a verdict.

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** define a term; attribute a claim, work, or hypothesis to its thinker or school; state the mechanism by which a policy changes prices, incentives, credit, or investment; identify the standard objection to a school's position; identify what a contested question *turns on* (its crux or decisive sub-question); correct a common myth.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~35% recall (definitions, attributions, dates), ~45% application (state the mechanism, match a school to its claim, name the standard objection), ~20% multi-step (identify the crux of a debate; disaggregate a growth episode into its drivers).
- **In scope:** definitions; each school's stated claim, mechanism, strongest case, and standard objection; the dated factual record and the mechanisms behind each episode; the recurring myths and the defensible correction to each.
- **Out of scope:** the verdict of a contested debate. No quiz item has "which side is right" as its key. *Whether* free trade beat aid, or inequality is worth it, is taught, never graded.
- **Classic traps:** treating correlation as causation (the field's central hazard); over-attributing the post-1990 poverty decline to "free trade"; stating estimate-laden poverty aggregates as exact; reading China as clean proof of either free markets or state control; conflating trade openness with financial (capital-account) openness.

## §1 Comparative advantage and the distribution of gains

The foundational case for trade: even a country that is worse at producing everything still gains by specialising where its costs are *relatively* lowest.

**Rule:** Comparative advantage (David Ricardo, *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*, 1817): two countries both gain by specialising in the good each produces at the lowest *opportunity* cost and trading, even if one holds an absolute disadvantage in both goods.

**Worked example:** Country A produces a unit of cloth in 2 hours and a unit of wine in 4; Country B produces cloth in 6 hours and wine in 3. B is absolutely worse at both. But A gives up less wine to make cloth than B does, and B gives up less cloth to make wine than A does — so if A specialises in cloth, B in wine, and they trade, total output of both goods rises and each ends better off than in self-sufficiency. The gain comes from *relative* cost, not absolute skill. Note the distributional footnote: trade raises *aggregate* welfare but creates winners and losers — the Stolper–Samuelson logic implies it can lower the return to a country's *scarce* factor (e.g. unskilled labour in a rich country), which is why "trade is good on net" and "trade hurt these specific workers" are both true.

**Trap:** Confusing comparative advantage with Adam Smith's earlier *absolute* advantage (*The Wealth of Nations*, 1776) — assuming the country better at making both has nothing to gain from trade.

**Key facts:** Ricardo, 1817 · relative/opportunity cost, not absolute · both parties gain · Smith's absolute advantage, 1776 · Stolper–Samuelson: gains are unevenly distributed.

## §2 Infant industry, ISI, EOI, and export discipline

The core development-policy vocabulary: how a late-comer builds industry, and the one mechanism that separated the successes from the failures.

**Rule:** The infant-industry argument (Alexander Hamilton, *Report on Manufactures*, 1791; Friedrich List, *The National System of Political Economy*, 1841) holds that nascent industries need *temporary* protection to reach the scale, learning, and productivity to compete; export discipline is the mechanism that conditions that protection on becoming globally competitive.

**Worked example:** Two ways to industrialise behind that logic. **Import-substitution industrialisation (ISI)** produces domestically what was formerly imported, behind protective barriers (Latin America, India, mid-20th century). **Export-oriented industrialisation (EOI)** produces for world markets and uses export competition to force efficiency (the East Asian path). The decisive difference is *export discipline*: East Asian state support was conditioned on export performance, so a subsidised firm had to win in world markets or lose its support — a built-in market test that killed off white-elephant industries. ISI without that discipline coddled inefficient firms indefinitely.

**Trap:** Treating ISI and EOI as equally viable protection strategies. The historical record separates them precisely on export discipline — protection *with* a competitiveness test (Korea) versus protection *without* one (Latin American ISI).

**Key facts:** Hamilton 1791, List 1841 · ISI = inward, EOI = outward · export discipline = subsidy conditioned on export performance · the mechanism that prevented ISI's waste.

## §3 Growth fundamentals: productivity, convergence, and the middle-income trap

What actually drives long-run growth, and why poor countries don't automatically catch up.

**Rule:** Total factor productivity (TFP) — the "Solow residual," the part of growth *not* explained by adding capital or labour — is the ultimate driver of long-run growth; convergence (poorer economies catching up) occurs *conditionally* but not unconditionally.

**Worked example:** Add more machines and workers and output rises, but with diminishing returns; sustained growth in income *per head* comes from doing more with the same inputs — technology and efficiency, captured as TFP. The Solow model predicts poorer economies should grow faster and converge on rich ones. They do — but only *conditional* on savings, human capital, and institutions. Unconditional catch-up fails: many poor economies do not close the gap at all, and some stall at middle income (the "middle-income trap"), squeezed between low-wage producers below and high-tech innovators above. The failure of automatic convergence is a central puzzle of the field.

**Trap:** Assuming convergence is automatic — that being poor is itself a growth advantage. It is only an advantage conditional on the fundamentals; the middle-income trap is a useful heuristic, though its status as a distinct "trap" is debated.

**Key facts:** TFP = Solow residual · long-run growth is a TFP story · convergence is conditional, not unconditional · middle-income trap = stall between low-wage and high-tech competitors.

## §4 The policy vocabulary: Washington Consensus, developmental state, institutions

Three labels that dominate the field — and are routinely strawmanned.

**Rule:** The Washington Consensus (John Williamson, 1989) is a list of ten market-oriented reforms (fiscal discipline, redirected public spending, tax reform, financial and trade liberalisation, competitive exchange rates, privatisation, deregulation, secure property rights) — not "privatise everything overnight."

**Worked example:** Set three concepts against each other. The **Washington Consensus** names market-oriented reforms; Williamson complained it was caricatured as "neoliberalism" and used as an epithet. The **developmental state** (Chalmers Johnson, *MITI and the Japanese Miracle*, 1982) actively guides industrialisation through directed credit, targeted subsidies, and export discipline. **Extractive vs. inclusive institutions** (Acemoglu–Robinson) locate the deep cause of prosperity in whether institutions secure broad property rights and open opportunity (inclusive) or concentrate power and rents (extractive). Each label points to a different lever: prices, the state, or institutions.

**Trap:** Using "Washington Consensus," "neoliberal," or "structural adjustment" as epithets rather than descriptions. Williamson's original list included fiscal discipline and property rights; the harsh *implementation* in some countries is a separate matter from the *label*.

**Key facts:** Williamson coined it, 1989, ten reforms · developmental state = Johnson, 1982 · inclusive vs. extractive institutions = Acemoglu–Robinson · the terms are frequently caricatured.

## §5 Structural constraints: dependency, terms of trade, and the resource curse

The pessimistic side of the vocabulary — why some starting positions may trap an economy.

**Rule:** The Prebisch–Singer hypothesis holds that the terms of trade move against primary-commodity producers over time; the resource curse holds that resource wealth can *retard* development through Dutch disease, rent-seeking, and unaccountable rentier states.

**Worked example:** Dependency theory (Raúl Prebisch and the Latin American structuralists) argues the global economy channels surplus from a commodity-exporting "periphery" to an industrial "core," with the terms of trade deteriorating for primary producers. The resource-curse mechanism is concrete: a resource boom inflates the exchange rate, which crowds out manufacturing (Dutch disease); resource rents fuel corruption and rent-seeking; and a state that lives off resource revenue taxes its citizens less, so it is less accountable to them. Both ideas explain why raw endowments can be a handicap rather than a boon.

**Trap:** Taking the *strong* dependency thesis as proven. The East Asian rise from periphery to core directly falsifies it — poor economies *can* industrialise and climb; use Prebisch–Singer carefully, for the commodity-dependence case, not as a law.

**Key facts:** Prebisch–Singer = terms of trade against primary producers · Dutch disease = boom inflates currency, crowds out manufacturing · rentier state = resource revenue weakens accountability · East Asia falsifies the strong version.

## §6 The evidence turn: RCTs and the randomista movement

A methodological shift from grand theory to tested interventions.

**Rule:** The randomised controlled trial (RCT) movement (Abhijit Banerjee, Esther Duflo, Michael Kremer; 2019 economics Nobel) tests *specific* development interventions experimentally rather than arguing grand growth theory.

**Worked example:** Instead of debating "what causes growth" in the abstract, the randomista approach asks a narrow, answerable question — does *this* deworming programme, cash transfer, or schooling tweak actually improve outcomes? — and tests it with a treatment and control group, as in medicine. The payoff is credible micro-level evidence of "what works." The limit is scope: RCTs answer micro questions and do not settle *macro* strategy (whether a country should industrialise via manufacturing or services).

**Trap:** Treating RCT findings as macro growth strategy. A tested cash-transfer result says nothing about whether an economy should pursue export manufacturing; catch the over-extension from micro to macro.

**Key facts:** Banerjee–Duflo–Kremer, 2019 Nobel · experimental method borrowed from medicine · strong on micro "what works" · silent on macro growth strategy.

## §7 School: classical / neoclassical free-trade liberalism

**Rule:** Free-trade liberalism (rooted in Smith and Ricardo, modernised in the Washington Consensus) claims that open markets, secure property, and undistorted prices allocate resources efficiently and that state distortions misallocate capital and breed rent-seeking.

**Worked example:** The mechanism runs through prices and competition: competition disciplines firms; imports lower consumer prices; exports exploit comparative advantage; foreign investment transfers technology and capital. Its **strongest case** is that the post-1990 growth and poverty collapse coincided with a wave of liberalisation, while command economies and heavily protected ISI regimes largely stagnated. Its **standard objection**: essentially every successful late developer — Britain, the US, Germany, Japan, Korea — used protection and industrial policy *before* opening and only liberalised later, so premature liberalisation can destroy infant industries and lock a country into commodity exports.

**Trap:** Reading the post-1990 correlation (liberalisation + growth) as clean proof of causation. The reforms that accompanied opening (domestic institutional change) may have done the real work.

**Key facts:** Smith, Ricardo, Washington Consensus · mechanism = competition, cheap imports, comparative advantage, FDI · strongest case = post-1990 record · objection = today's rich protected first.

## §8 School: developmental-state / structuralist

**Rule:** The developmental-state school (Chalmers Johnson; Alice Amsden, *Asia's Next Giant*, 1989; Robert Wade, *Governing the Market*, 1990; Ha-Joon Chang, *Kicking Away the Ladder*, 2002) claims late development requires a capable, autonomous state that disciplines capital toward strategic sectors and forces firms to become export-competitive.

**Worked example:** The mechanism is directed credit, a protected home market to build scale, and subsidies *conditioned on export performance* — the discipline that prevented ISI's waste. Its **strongest case**: Korea and Taiwan went from poor to rich in a generation using exactly these tools, and today's rich countries did the same when catching up, then "kicked away the ladder" by preaching free trade to those behind (Chang's central point). Its **standard objection**: this depended on unusual *state capacity* and Cold War geopolitics (US aid and market access); most states attempting directed industrial policy produced captured rents and white elephants, not Koreas.

**Trap:** Generalising from Korea to "any country should run industrial policy." The hidden variable is state capacity — whether *this* state can execute without capture.

**Key facts:** Johnson, Amsden, Wade, Chang · mechanism = directed credit + export discipline · strongest case = Korea/Taiwan in a generation · objection = requires rare state capacity.

## §9 School: institutionalist

**Rule:** The institutionalist school (Douglass North; Daron Acemoglu, Simon Johnson, James Robinson, *Why Nations Fail*, 2012; 2024 Nobel) claims the deep cause of prosperity is inclusive institutions, and that policies matter only insofar as institutions let good ones stick.

**Worked example:** The mechanism: secure property rights and constraints on elites create the incentive to invest and innovate; extractive institutions do the opposite. Its **strongest case** rests on natural experiments — divided Korea (same geography and culture, different institutions, divergent outcomes) and the "reversal of fortune" in former colonies, identified via the 2001 settler-mortality instrument. Its **standard objection**: it risks circularity (what *makes* institutions inclusive?) and struggles to explain rapid growth under manifestly non-inclusive institutions — above all China.

**Trap:** Treating "inclusive institutions" as a complete explanation. The China case is the standing anomaly, and the definition can slide toward circularity.

**Key facts:** North; Acemoglu–Johnson–Robinson, 2012, 2024 Nobel · mechanism = property rights + elite constraints · trump card = divided Korea · anomaly = China.

## §10 School: Keynesian and macro-stabilisation traditions

**Rule:** The Keynesian tradition claims demand matters, markets can settle at high-unemployment equilibria, and the state has a role in counter-cyclical stabilisation and in managing the balance of payments and exchange rate during development.

**Worked example:** Where the classical view trusts markets to clear, the Keynesian view holds an economy can get stuck below full employment, so the state should support demand in downturns rather than cut into them. Its **strongest case**: the postwar "embedded liberalism" era paired open trade with active domestic management and delivered strong, broadly shared growth, and abrupt austerity in crises can deepen them. Its **standard objection**: persistent deficits, inflation, and politicised macro management can destabilise developing economies (the monetarist/orthodox critique).

**Trap:** Confusing counter-cyclical stabilisation with permanent deficit spending. The defensible claim is about managing the *cycle*, not running perpetual deficits.

**Key facts:** demand-side · high-unemployment equilibria possible · embedded liberalism = open trade + domestic management · objection = deficits/inflation risk.

## §11 School: neoliberal / market-fundamentalist and Austrian critiques

**Rule:** The Austrian critique (Hayek) holds that central planning cannot solve the "knowledge problem" — the dispersed information that only prices aggregate — so top-down industrial policy is doomed to misallocate.

**Worked example:** The monetarist/orthodox strand says: control inflation, keep budgets disciplined, free prices, and let markets work, because intervention distorts and invites capture. The Austrian strand goes deeper: no planner can gather the dispersed, tacit knowledge that market prices summarise, so directed allocation must misfire. Its **strongest case**: the failures of command economies and hyperinflationary populism. Its **standard objection**: it conflates *all* state action with central planning — the developmental state used markets *and* direction — and pure laissez-faire has few unambiguous development successes.

**Trap:** Sliding from "central planning fails" to "all state direction fails." The knowledge-problem argument targets comprehensive planning, not every targeted intervention.

**Key facts:** Hayek's knowledge problem · prices aggregate dispersed information · strongest case = command-economy failure · objection = conflates direction with planning.

## §12 School: dependency and world-systems

**Rule:** Dependency and world-systems theory (Prebisch; Andre Gunder Frank; Immanuel Wallerstein) claims underdevelopment is not a starting condition but a *product* of a global system that channels surplus from periphery to core.

**Worked example:** The mechanism: commodity dependence and unequal terms of trade trap the periphery in supplying cheap raw materials to an industrial core that captures the value-added. Its **strongest case**: commodity-dependent economies have often seen volatile revenues and deteriorating terms of trade, and the resource curse is real. Its **standard objection**: the East Asian rise from periphery to core falsifies the *strong* version — poor economies *can* climb — and its policy prescription (delinking, ISI) generally underperformed EOI.

**Trap:** Taking the strong "escape is impossible" version as the school's defensible core. The usable claim is about commodity-dependence risk, not an iron law of entrapment.

**Key facts:** Prebisch, Frank, Wallerstein · core/periphery surplus transfer · strongest case = commodity volatility, resource curse · falsified (strong version) by East Asia.

## §13 School: new structural economics

**Rule:** New structural economics (Justin Yifu Lin) claims governments should facilitate industries aligned with a country's *evolving* comparative advantage, rather than leap to industries it cannot yet sustain.

**Worked example:** This is the attempted reconciliation of market and state: the state has a role, but a disciplined one — helping firms move into sectors just beyond the current frontier of the country's factor endowments, not commanding a jump to advanced industries the economy cannot support. It pairs, in the modern field, with the randomista turn (§6): test interventions rather than theorise. Its **significance for the learner**: it is the cutting-edge synthesis position — "facilitate the next rung," not "pick a distant winner."

**Trap:** Reading it as either pure laissez-faire or classic pick-a-winner industrial policy. Its distinctive claim is *comparative-advantage-following* facilitation — a middle path.

**Key facts:** Justin Yifu Lin · facilitate the evolving comparative advantage · rungs, not leaps · the modern market-state synthesis.

## §14 The mature synthesis: state capacity and sequencing

**Rule:** The view most professional economists converge toward is that institutions and state capacity are the deep variables, while trade and industrial policy are tools whose success depends on that capacity and on sequencing.

**Worked example:** Almost nobody serious defends pure autarky or pure laissez-faire for a developing economy. The real disagreements are narrower: *how much* state, *which* sectors, and *in what order*. Sequencing is often decisive — the same policy that helps when applied in the right order (build capacity, then open) can wreck an economy when applied prematurely. This synthesis is why the schools above are best understood as emphasising different variables rather than as flatly contradicting one another.

**Trap:** Framing the field as a binary "state vs. market." The mature position is about degree, sector, and sequence — not a side.

**Key facts:** deep variables = institutions + state capacity · trade/industrial policy = context-dependent tools · sequencing often decides outcomes · the binary framing is the amateur's error.

## §15 The post-1990 collapse in extreme poverty

**Rule:** From around 1990 to the late 2010s the share of the world in extreme poverty fell dramatically — by most World Bank accounts from well over a third of humanity to under a tenth — but the reduction was concentrated in China, with substantial further contributions from India and East Asia.

**Worked example:** The absolute number in extreme poverty fell from roughly 1.9 billion toward the high hundreds of millions before the pandemic. The analytically important point is *attribution*: because the bulk came from China and India, the honest question is never "did global capitalism reduce poverty" but "what specifically drove the Chinese and Indian episodes — trade openness, domestic institutional reform, state-led investment, or all three in sequence?" The number is real; the *cause* is the whole debate.

**Trap:** Stating the aggregates as exact. They are estimates tied to a poverty line and a base year and move with data revisions — present them as "roughly" or "on the order of," never as settled figures.

**Key facts:** ~1990 to late 2010s · from >⅓ to <1/10 of humanity · ~1.9 billion falling toward high hundreds of millions · concentrated in China + India · figures are line-dependent estimates.

## §16 The Marshall Plan and postwar Europe

**Rule:** From 1948, US aid and the restoration of open trade under the Bretton Woods framework helped drive Western Europe's postwar recovery — the classic case for aid-plus-openness *when institutions and human capital already exist*.

**Worked example:** Europe recovered fast because aid and reopened trade landed on economies that already had the institutions, skills, and industrial know-how to absorb them productively. That is exactly why the episode is a caution against over-generalising: many later aid recipients lacked those preconditions, so the same aid-plus-openness formula did not reproduce the same result.

**Trap:** Reading the Marshall Plan as proof that aid works everywhere. Its lesson is conditional — aid is productive *where the absorptive institutions and human capital already exist*.

**Key facts:** from 1948 · Bretton Woods framework · aid + reopened trade · worked because preconditions existed · not a general template.

## §17 Japan and the East Asian tigers

**Rule:** Postwar Japan industrialised under heavy state coordination (the MITI model), and from the 1960s the "tigers" — Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong — converged rapidly toward rich-world incomes within a generation.

**Worked example:** The tigers are not one model. Korea and Taiwan are the developmental-state exemplars (directed credit, export discipline, protected-then-opened sectors); Hong Kong is the free-market exemplar; Singapore blends heavy state direction with openness. The World Bank's *The East Asian Miracle* (1993) tried to reconcile these and became a battleground — free-traders read it as vindicating openness, statists as vindicating industrial policy. The single episode supports multiple readings because the four cases genuinely differ.

**Trap:** Citing "East Asia" as if it were one policy story. The region contains both the statist (Korea/Taiwan) and the free-market (Hong Kong) exemplars.

**Key facts:** Japan = MITI model · tigers = Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Hong Kong · rich in a generation · *The East Asian Miracle*, World Bank, 1993 · read differently by both camps.

## §18 China's reform era (from 1978)

**Rule:** Deng Xiaoping's reforms began in 1978 and combined agricultural decollectivisation, special economic zones, gradual experimental liberalisation, and WTO accession in 2001 — under continuous heavy state direction throughout.

**Worked example:** Trace the mechanisms in sequence: the **Household Responsibility System** decollectivised agriculture and unleashed rural productivity; **Special Economic Zones** (Shenzhen designated 1980) attracted foreign investment and export manufacturing; **township-and-village enterprises** and gradual, experimental liberalisation ("crossing the river by feeling the stones") spread reform; **WTO accession in 2001** integrated China into world trade and turbocharged exports. This is *the* contested case: free-traders cite the opening, statists cite the state-owned banks and industrial policy, institutionalists struggle because growth occurred under a one-party state without "inclusive" institutions in the Acemoglu–Robinson sense. All three schools read the same episode differently — which is why it dominates the domain.

**Trap:** Claiming China cleanly proves either free markets *or* state control. The defensible reading is "openness + heavy state direction + gradualism, in sequence" — anyone who reads it as clean proof of one side is over-reading.

**Key facts:** reforms from 1978 · Household Responsibility System · Shenzhen SEZ 1980 · WTO accession 2001 · contested by all three schools.

## §19 India's liberalisation (from 1991)

**Rule:** A balance-of-payments crisis in 1991 triggered IMF-supported reforms under Finance Minister Manmohan Singh — dismantling the "Licence Raj," cutting tariffs, devaluing the currency, and opening to foreign investment — after which growth accelerated markedly.

**Worked example:** The "Licence Raj" was a pervasive industrial-licensing system that required government permission for most business decisions; dismantling it removed a heavy regulatory drag, and growth accelerated. India's distinctive feature is a **services-led** rather than manufacturing-led path (IT, business processing), which is the source of a live debate over whether it "skipped" the labour-absorbing manufacturing stage that lifted East Asia's masses out of poverty (see §24).

**Trap:** Treating India's path as a smaller copy of East Asia's. It is structurally different — services-led, not manufacturing-led — which is precisely what makes it debated.

**Key facts:** 1991 balance-of-payments crisis · Manmohan Singh · Licence Raj dismantled · tariff cuts + devaluation + FDI opening · services-led path.

## §20 The cautionary failures

**Rule:** The domain's cautionary cases — Latin American ISI and the 1980s "lost decade," sub-Saharan structural adjustment, the 1997 Asian financial crisis, and the resource curse — each isolate a specific mechanism of failure.

**Worked example:** Four distinct lessons. **Latin American ISI** produced some industrialisation but also inefficiency and chronic balance-of-payments crises, ending in the 1980s debt crisis and a lost decade — the case against protection *without* export discipline. **Structural adjustment** in sub-Saharan Africa (IMF/World Bank austerity, liberalisation, privatisation, 1980s–90s) is widely judged to have produced disappointing growth and real social costs, fuelling the "post-Washington Consensus." The **1997 Asian crisis** (triggered by the collapse of the Thai baht's peg in July 1997 and rapid capital flight) drove Joseph Stiglitz's critique that IMF-imposed austerity deepened the crisis — its lesson is that *financial openness is not the same as trade openness, and sequencing matters*. The **resource curse** shows resource-rich states underperforming resource-poor peers via Dutch disease and rentier politics.

**Trap:** Conflating the 1997 crisis's lesson about *capital-account* (financial) openness with trade openness. They are different; the 1997 lesson is about capital flows, not trade in goods.

**Key facts:** Latin American ISI → 1980s lost decade · SSA structural adjustment → post-Washington Consensus · Asian crisis July 1997, Thai baht, Stiglitz critique · financial ≠ trade openness.

## §21 The China shock in rich countries

**Rule:** Autor, Dorn, and Hanson ("The China Syndrome," 2013) showed that the surge of Chinese imports after 2001 caused concentrated, persistent job and wage losses in exposed manufacturing communities, even as it lowered consumer prices broadly.

**Worked example:** The finding reshaped the profession's view of trade's costs. Trade's aggregate gains are real — cheaper goods for everyone — but the *distributional* costs turned out larger, more concentrated, and more persistent than the textbook "workers will reallocate to new jobs" story assumed. Exposed communities did not smoothly recover; the damage clustered and lingered. This is the empirical backbone of the modern, serious protectionist case, and it complicates any glib "free trade helps everyone."

**Trap:** Using the China shock to claim trade has *no* aggregate benefit, or ignoring it to claim adjustment is painless. The defensible reading is real aggregate gains *plus* real, concentrated, persistent local losses.

**Key facts:** Autor–Dorn–Hanson, 2013 · post-2001 import surge · concentrated, persistent local job/wage losses · broad consumer-price gains · backbone of serious protectionism.

## §22 Debate: free trade versus foreign aid for poverty reduction

**Rule:** This question turns on causation and definition — what counts as "free trade," and whether it *caused* the great poverty declines or merely accompanied domestic reform.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro-trade** case: the great declines (China, India, East Asia) were driven by integration into world markets and domestic market reform, not aid; trade creates self-sustaining growth while aid's macro growth record is weak and can fund dependency. The **pro-aid** case: the trade that reduced poverty was *managed and sequenced*, not textbook free trade, and aid delivered decisive gains in *health* (vaccination, disease control) that trade did not, building the human capital growth requires. The crux for a thinker to resolve: disaggregate the China/India episodes rather than treat "trade" as one undifferentiated force, and separate aid's weak *growth* record from its strong *health* record.

**Trap:** Attributing the whole decline to "free trade." This over-reads a story that was really domestic reform plus selective openness; and it conflates aid's growth record with its health record.

**Key facts:** crux = causation + definition of "free trade" · disaggregate China/India · aid: weak on growth, strong on health · "managed and sequenced," not textbook.

## §23 Debate: is industrial policy necessary for late development

**Rule:** This question turns on state capacity — whether the *specific* country can execute industrial policy without capture, not merely whether it worked *somewhere*.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case: every country that industrialised late used it (Britain, US, Germany, Japan, Korea, China); markets alone underinvest in the learning, scale, and coordination new industries require; export discipline solved the waste problem that sank ISI. The **con** case: industrial policy requires state capacity and insulation from capture that most developing states lack; for every Korea there are many costly failures; picking winners invites rent-seeking, so the safer general prescription is good institutions, macro stability, and openness. The decisive comparison is export discipline (Korea) versus its absence (Latin American ISI), and the decisive question is whether *this* state can execute.

**Trap:** Arguing from "it worked in Korea" to "any country should do it." The sophisticated line on both sides concedes state capacity is the hidden variable.

**Key facts:** crux = state capacity of the specific country · Korea vs. Latin American ISI = the decisive contrast · export discipline is the mechanism · "worked somewhere" ≠ "will work here."

## §24 Debate: export manufacturing versus a services-led path

**Rule:** This question (the "India question") turns on whether manufacturing can still absorb mass low-skill labour in an automated world.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **manufacturing** case: manufacturing absorbs large numbers of low-skill workers, drives productivity growth, and is historically how mass populations left poverty (East Asia); a services-led path risks leaving hundreds of millions of low-skilled workers behind. The **services** case: automation is eroding manufacturing's job-absorption everywhere (premature deindustrialisation); services (IT, business processing) can leapfrog and may be where a given economy has genuine comparative advantage; forcing a manufacturing path against factor endowments wastes resources. The crux to resolve: is the East Asian labour-absorbing ladder still there to climb, or has automation removed the rungs?

**Trap:** Assuming the East Asian ladder is still available. The decisive sub-question is whether manufacturing can still absorb mass labour *now*, not whether it did historically.

**Key facts:** crux = manufacturing's labour-absorption under automation · premature deindustrialisation · services leapfrog vs. mass low-skill employment · the India case.

## §25 Debate: is inequality a necessary price of growth

**Rule:** This question turns on the *mechanism* linking inequality to growth — the positive channels (incentives, savings) versus the negative ones (human capital, demand, political capture).

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case: incentives require unequal rewards; concentrated savings finance investment; the Kuznets pattern suggests early-development inequality is transitional. The **con** case: high inequality can *retard* growth — under-investment in the poor's human capital, weak aggregate demand, and political capture that blocks reform. The Kuznets curve itself is contested: Piketty (*Capital in the Twenty-First Century*, 2013) argues inequality re-rose in rich countries, breaking the tidy curve. The crux is which mechanism dominates in a given context, not a universal answer.

**Trap:** Citing the Kuznets curve as a law. It is a contested hypothesis; Piketty's challenge is the standard rebuttal.

**Key facts:** crux = which mechanism dominates · positive: incentives, savings · negative: human capital, demand, capture · Kuznets curve contested, Piketty 2013.

## §26 Debate: unilateral tariff abolition

**Rule:** This question turns on weighing immediate consumer and efficiency gains against strategic leverage and concentrated adjustment costs.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case (the classic economist's argument): tariffs tax your own consumers and inputs, so unilateral free trade captures the consumer and efficiency gains immediately, without waiting for reciprocity. The **con** case: reciprocity is leverage; unilateral opening forfeits bargaining power, can gut strategic or nascent industries, and ignores the concentrated adjustment costs (the China shock) that a sequenced approach could cushion. The crux is whether the immediate aggregate gain outweighs the forfeited leverage and the distributional shock.

**Trap:** Arguing only from the textbook aggregate. The defensible debate engages the distributional and sequencing realities, not just the net consumer gain.

**Key facts:** crux = consumer/efficiency gain vs. leverage + adjustment cost · unilateral = immediate gain, no reciprocity wait · con = leverage, strategic industries, China-shock costs.

## §27 The domain's most error-prone claims

**Rule:** In this domain, correlation is routinely mistaken for causation — openness, reform, and growth move together, and asserting that any one caused the others ignores the identification problem, which is the field's central analytical hazard.

**Worked example:** Walk the recurring myths and their corrections. *"Free trade lifted a billion out of poverty"* — over-attribution; the bulk was China and India, driven substantially by *domestic* reform (decollectivisation, dismantling the Licence Raj) alongside selective opening. *"Today's rich countries got rich through free trade"* — historically false as a general claim; most protected and subsidised during catch-up and liberalised later (Chang) — though ISI *without* export discipline generally failed, so neither "protection always works" nor "openness always works" holds. *"China proves free markets work" / "China proves state control works"* — genuinely contested evidence; the honest reading is "openness + heavy state direction + gradualism, in sequence." *"Aid doesn't work"* — too broad; weak on growth, strong on health. *"Just exploit your natural resources"* — ignores the resource curse and Dutch disease. The **lump-of-labour fallacy** assumes a fixed number of jobs so imports/automation permanently destroy employment; aggregate employment is not fixed, but (per the China shock) transition costs are concentrated and persistent — so neither the naive "jobs are fixed" nor the naive "workers just reallocate painlessly" story is right.

**Trap:** False precision on poverty aggregates, and conflating trade openness with financial (capital-account) openness — the 1997 Asian crisis is about capital flows, not trade in goods.

**Key facts:** correlation ≠ causation (the master error) · over-attribution of poverty decline to trade · China is contested evidence · aid: weak growth / strong health · resource curse · lump-of-labour fallacy · trade openness ≠ financial openness · Kuznets curve contested.
