# Democracy, Governance & Political Institutions

*Subject-knowledge coach codex. Authored under the subject-knowledge variant: real named content; every Rule is a defensible fact; the normative verdict of a contested debate is taught in the Worked example (both sides shown), never keyed.*

## Scope

This subject is the rules that convert citizens' preferences into collective decisions and hold power accountable: electoral systems, majority-minority balance, the vertical distribution of power, the money and media shaping competition, and how democracies decay. It covers the vocabulary, the competing theories of democracy and institutional design, the empirical record, the live debates, and the recurring myths. It excludes the verdict of those debates — the learner reconstructs each position's strongest case and names the trade-off a design makes, rather than being told which electoral system is best.

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** define a term; attribute a law, theorem, or framework to its thinker; state the mechanism or tendency a model predicts; identify the standard objection to a design; identify what a debate turns on; correct a common myth.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~35% recall (definitions, attributions), ~45% application (state the tendency, match a theory to its claim, name the objection), ~20% multi-step (surface the trade-off a design makes; distinguish input from output legitimacy).
- **In scope:** definitions; each theory's claim, mechanism, strongest case, and objection; the empirical record; the myths and corrections.
- **Out of scope:** the verdict of a contested debate. No item keys "is PR better than FPTP" or "should voting be compulsory."
- **Classic traps:** "more democracy is always better" and its mirror "experts always know best"; treating Duverger's law or the median-voter theorem as iron laws; "compulsory voting definitely changes who wins"; using "populism" as a slur for disliked policy; "money buys elections."

## §1 Electoral systems: FPTP, PR, and mixed

The core menu and its characteristic trade-off.

**Rule:** First-past-the-post (FPTP) uses single-member districts where the plurality winner takes the seat, tending toward disproportional results but decisive single-party government; proportional representation (PR) allocates seats in proportion to votes, tending toward more representative multi-party coalitions.

**Worked example:** FPTP can manufacture a governing majority from a minority of votes and squeeze out third parties, but produces one accountable winner; PR makes nearly every vote count and includes minorities, but blurs accountability (who is responsible when everyone governs together?). Mixed systems (mixed-member proportional, as in Germany and New Zealand) combine district and proportional elements; ranked/preferential and runoff systems seek majority winners and reduce vote-splitting.

**Trap:** "PR causes instability" / "FPTP is undemocratic" — both overstatements; the honest claim is about *tendencies* and *trade-offs*, since many PR democracies are stable and FPTP is a legitimate if disproportional design.

**Key facts:** FPTP = single-member, plurality, disproportional but decisive · PR = proportional, representative but coalition-prone · mixed = MMP (Germany, NZ) · ranked/runoff seek majority winners.

## §2 Duverger's law and the median-voter theorem

Two workhorse models — powerful tendencies, not laws.

**Rule:** Duverger's law is the empirical tendency for FPTP to produce two dominant parties and PR to sustain many; the median-voter theorem holds that, under simplifying assumptions (single issue-dimension, two parties, full turnout), parties converge on the median voter's preference.

**Worked example:** Duverger holds as a strong regularity with exceptions — regional parties survive under FPTP. The median-voter theorem's assumptions often fail: multi-dimensional politics, abstention, and primaries pulling candidates to the extremes all break the tidy convergence. Both are useful models whose limits matter as much as their content.

**Trap:** Treating either as an iron law — Duverger is a strong tendency with exceptions, and the median-voter theorem's convergence collapses when its assumptions fail.

**Key facts:** Duverger = FPTP → two parties, PR → many (tendency) · median-voter = convergence under one dimension/two parties/full turnout · both have well-known limits.

## §3 Majoritarian vs consensus democracy

Lijphart's framework for how power is concentrated or dispersed.

**Rule:** Arend Lijphart distinguishes *majoritarian* democracy (FPTP, single-party government, centralised power — concentrating power in the winners) from *consensus* democracy (PR, coalitions, federalism, power-sharing — dispersing it), and argued consensus democracies perform at least as well and are "kinder, gentler."

**Worked example:** Majoritarian systems concentrate power like a hammer driving a nail; consensus systems disperse it like a mirror reflecting the whole crowd. Consociationalism (grand coalitions, mutual vetoes, proportionality, segmental autonomy) is the consensus toolkit for holding *deeply divided* societies together. Lijphart's performance finding is influential but debated (correlation-vs-causation and case-selection critiques apply).

**Trap:** Presenting Lijphart's performance finding as settled; it is influential but subject to correlation-vs-causation and case-selection critiques.

**Key facts:** majoritarian = concentrate power · consensus = disperse power · Lijphart's "kinder, gentler" finding (debated) · consociationalism for divided societies.

## §4 Arrow, public choice, and the limits of "the will of the people"

The technical results that complicate aggregation.

**Rule:** Arrow's impossibility theorem shows no ranked voting system can perfectly satisfy a small set of reasonable fairness criteria simultaneously; public choice (Buchanan, Tullock) applies economics to politics, predicting rational ignorance, rent-seeking, and concentrated-interest capture of dispersed majorities.

**Worked example:** Arrow's result — plus the paradox of voting (majority preferences can be intransitive) — means "the will of the people" is not always well-defined, and agenda-setting power (who decides what is voted on, and in what order) can determine outcomes. Public choice explains why organised minorities often beat dispersed majorities: the few with concentrated interests out-organise the many with diffuse ones.

**Trap:** Invoking an unproblematic "will of the people" — Arrow and the paradox of voting show aggregation is genuinely hard and may not yield a coherent majority preference at all.

**Key facts:** Arrow = no perfect ranked system · paradox of voting = intransitive majorities · agenda-setting matters · public choice = rational ignorance + rent-seeking + capture.

## §5 Populism, backsliding, and the legitimacy distinction

The vocabulary of decay and a useful framing throughout.

**Rule:** Populism is a "thin" ideology (Cas Mudde) dividing society into a pure "people" versus a corrupt "elite" and claiming to embody the singular popular will (it can attach to left or right); democratic backsliding (Levitsky & Ziblatt, *How Democracies Die*, 2018) is the incremental erosion of democracy by *elected* leaders capturing the "referees," not sudden coup.

**Worked example:** Backsliding works through "executive aggrandizement" — capturing courts, election administration, and media, and tilting the playing field — rather than tanks in the street. A useful framing throughout: distinguish *input legitimacy* (decisions are legitimate because the right people decided them, through fair procedures) from *output legitimacy* (legitimate because they produce good results). Many democracy debates are disputes about which kind should win when they conflict.

**Trap:** Using "populism" as a synonym for "policies I dislike," "right-wing," or "left-wing" — it is a specific structural logic (people vs. corrupt elite, claim to the singular popular will).

**Key facts:** populism = thin ideology, people vs. elite (Mudde) · backsliding = incremental, elected leaders capture referees (Levitsky & Ziblatt, 2018) · input vs. output legitimacy.

## §6 Federalism and accountability chains

The vertical distribution of power and how power is checked.

**Rule:** Federalism constitutionally divides authority between national and subnational governments — justified by subsidiarity, policy experimentation ("laboratories of democracy"), and containing conflict in diverse societies — while accountability runs *vertically* (elections) and *horizontally* (courts, free press, audit and election bodies, legislatures checking the executive).

**Worked example:** Federalism's rationales: fit policy to local preferences, let regions experiment, disperse power as a check on tyranny, and give minorities self-rule. Its risks: coordination failure, a regulatory "race to the bottom," and enabling *local* majorities to oppress *local* minorities. Horizontal accountability — the "referees" — is precisely what backsliding leaders capture.

**Trap:** Treating federalism as unambiguously good for minorities; it can *entrench* local majorities oppressing local minorities, depending on how diversity is distributed.

**Key facts:** federalism = divided authority, subsidiarity, laboratories · risks: race to the bottom, local-majority oppression · vertical (elections) vs. horizontal (courts/press/audit) accountability.

## §7 School: electoral-system design in full

**Rule:** The majoritarian case prizes decisive, accountable, stable single-party government; the proportional case prizes representativeness and inclusion; the synthesis requires *defining "good governance" first*, then choosing the system that best delivers *that* metric.

**Worked example:** *Majoritarian/FPTP* — **strongest:** avoids coalition horse-trading and unstable minority governments, with strong constituency links; **objection:** systematically disproportional, can manufacture a majority from a vote minority, squeezes out third parties. *Proportional/PR* — **strongest:** higher representativeness and often higher turnout, better minority inclusion; **objection:** coalitions can be unstable or captured by small pivotal parties, and accountability blurs. Arrow's theorem is the reminder that no system is flawless on every axis.

**Trap:** Asserting one system is simply superior without a defined yardstick — the debate is won by whoever defines "good governance" (representativeness vs. stability vs. accountability vs. inclusion) first.

**Key facts:** majoritarian = decisive/accountable · PR = representative/inclusive · define the metric first · Arrow = no flawless system.

## §8 School: theories of democracy in full

**Rule:** Minimalist/competitive democracy (Schumpeter) treats democracy as a *method* for choosing and removing leaders; participatory/deliberative democracy (Habermas) requires informed public deliberation, not just voting; pluralist theories (Dahl's polyarchy) see power dispersed, while elite/public-choice critics see concentrated interests dominating.

**Worked example:** *Minimalist* — **strongest:** modest and achievable, foregrounding what democracies most reliably deliver (removing bad rulers without violence); **objection:** sets the bar too low, ignoring participation. *Deliberative* — **strongest:** legitimacy from reasoned debate, not raw preference aggregation; **objection:** hard to scale, and real publics are often inattentive (the public-choice "rational ignorance" point). *Epistemic/technocratic skepticism* — some decisions require expertise voters lack, so insulating them (independent central banks, expert regulators) can improve outcomes at a legitimacy cost; **objection:** the governed still bear the consequences, and "technical" choices often mask value judgments.

**Trap:** Assuming "more participation is always better" or "experts always know best" without engaging the actual trade-off a design makes.

**Key facts:** minimalist = method (Schumpeter) · deliberative = reasoned debate (Habermas) · pluralist (Dahl) vs. elite/public-choice · technocracy = competence at a legitimacy cost.

## §9 Schools: populism and federalism in full

**Rule:** On populism, the *corrective* view reads surges as signals of real grievances elites ignored, while the *corrosive* view holds its anti-pluralist logic delegitimises opposition and independent institutions; on federalism, the *federalist* case is fit-to-local-preference and dispersed power, the *centralist* case is uniform standards and protection against local tyranny.

**Worked example:** The mature line on populism distinguishes populist *demands* (frequently legitimate) from populist *methods* (frequently anti-pluralist) — a surge can be a legitimate symptom whose methods still corrode the institutions democracy depends on. On federalism: the federalist case (local control, experimentation, self-rule for minorities) meets the centralist case (uniform national standards, stronger redistribution, protection of individuals against local tyranny), each with a mirror objection (fragmentation and local-majority oppression vs. distant one-size-fits-all rule).

**Trap:** Treating populism as all-signal or all-solvent; the sophisticated line separates its (often legitimate) demands from its (often anti-pluralist) methods.

**Key facts:** populism corrective vs. corrosive; demands vs. methods · federalism = local fit/experimentation vs. uniform standards/anti-local-tyranny.

## §10 The empirical record

**Rule:** Duverger's law holds as a tendency not a law; compulsory voting reliably raises turnout (Australia) but its effect on *outcomes* is contested; 21st-century backsliding has typically been incremental and legal-seeming rather than by coup.

**Worked example:** Additional well-established patterns: Lijphart's consensus-performance finding is influential but debated; campaign finance correlates with success, but whether money *causes* wins or flows to likely winners is genuinely hard to disentangle; referendums have a mixed record (they can legitimise big decisions but also oversimplify trade-offs into binaries and be captured by well-funded campaigns); and confident causal claims about "social media destroying democracy" outrun the settled evidence. Arrow's theorem and the paradox of voting remind us the "majority preference" can be intransitive and agenda-setting can decide outcomes.

**Trap:** "Compulsory voting definitely changes who wins" — turnout rises but the partisan/outcome effect is *contested*, since the lean of the additional voters varies.

**Key facts:** Duverger = tendency · compulsory voting raises turnout, outcome effect contested (Australia) · backsliding = incremental, not coups · money correlates but causation muddy · referendums = mixed record.

## §11 Debate: does PR produce better governance than FPTP

**Rule:** This question turns on *defining "better governance"* (representativeness vs. stability vs. accountability vs. inclusion) and then routing the electoral mechanics to that metric.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **PR** case: representation matches votes, almost no vote is wasted, minorities gain voice, turnout is often higher, and coalitions force compromise. The **FPTP** case: decisive single-party government is accountable and stable, voters can cleanly reward or punish a governing party, and PR empowers small pivotal parties into paralysis or perpetual bargaining. Whoever picks the yardstick first controls the debate; asserting one system is simply superior without one is under-arguing.

**Trap:** Arguing without a yardstick — the whole debate hinges on which metric of "good governance" is chosen.

**Key facts:** crux = define the governance metric · representativeness vs. stability/accountability · no vote wasted vs. clean accountability.

## §12 Debate: should voting be compulsory

**Rule:** This question turns on weighing the legitimacy/representativeness gain against the liberty cost and the empirical uncertainty about whether outcomes change.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **compulsory** case: full participation makes government reflect the whole electorate, counters the class/age skew of voluntary turnout, and strengthens legitimacy. The **voluntary** case: coercing a political act infringes liberty, may add uninformed or resentful votes, and buys uncertain benefit since the outcome effects are unproven. Penalise a compulsory-side that asserts outcomes definitely improve.

**Trap:** Asserting outcomes definitely improve — the outcome effect is unproven, so the liberty cost buys an uncertain benefit.

**Key facts:** crux = legitimacy gain vs. liberty cost vs. empirical uncertainty · turnout rises · outcome effect unproven.

## §13 Debate: is populism a symptom of democratic failure or a disease of democracy

**Rule:** This question turns on distinguishing populist *demands* (often a legitimate symptom) from populist *methods* (often a genuine disease).

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **symptom** case: populist surges are downstream of real, unaddressed grievances and elite unresponsiveness, so treating populism as a pathogen ignores the failures that produced it. The **disease** case: whatever its causes, populism's anti-pluralist logic — one true people, illegitimate opposition, captured referees — actively corrodes the institutions democracy depends on, and the "symptom" framing excuses the damage. The sophisticated line makes the demands/methods distinction rather than treating populism as all-signal or all-solvent.

**Trap:** Treating populism as entirely signal (excusing the anti-pluralist damage) or entirely solvent (ignoring the real grievances).

**Key facts:** crux = demands (symptom) vs. methods (disease) · real grievances vs. corroded institutions.

## §14 Debate: do federal systems govern diverse societies better

**Rule:** This question turns on the specific society's cleavages — territorially concentrated minorities favour federalism; dispersed ones may not.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **federal** case: self-rule for regions and minorities contains conflict, fits policy to local preferences, and disperses power, and many stable diverse democracies are federal. The **central** case: federalism can entrench local majorities oppressing local minorities, block national redistribution, and fragment responses to shared problems, while centralisation can better guarantee uniform rights. Reason from the actual pattern of diversity, not a generic template.

**Trap:** Reasoning from a generic template rather than the specific society's pattern of diversity.

**Key facts:** crux = the society's cleavages · concentrated minorities favour federalism · self-rule vs. local-majority oppression/fragmentation.

## §15 Debate: should some decisions be insulated from majorities

**Rule:** This question turns on weighing competence (output legitimacy) against legitimacy (input legitimacy), and specifying *which* decisions — genuinely technical vs. value-laden — belong on which side.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **insulate** case: expertise and time-consistency (independent central banks) produce better outcomes on complex technical questions than short-term electoral pressure. The **against** case: insulation is a legitimacy deficit, "technical" choices often smuggle in value judgments, and the governed deserve a say in decisions that bind them. The strong position specifies which decisions are genuinely technical and which are value-laden.

**Trap:** Failing to distinguish genuinely technical decisions from value-laden ones dressed as technical.

**Key facts:** crux = competence (output) vs. legitimacy (input) · central banks as exemplar · specify technical vs. value-laden.

## §16 The domain's most error-prone claims

**Rule:** The domain's two signature traps are "more democracy is always better" and its mirror "experts always know best" — each carries real costs the other side surfaces.

**Worked example:** Walk the recurring myths. *"More democracy is always better"* — direct democracy, maximal participation, and pure majoritarianism each carry costs (uninformed decisions, minority oppression, instability). *"Experts always know best"* — technocratic insulation carries legitimacy costs and can disguise value choices as neutral. *"Compulsory voting definitely changes who wins"* — the outcome effect is contested. *"PR causes instability" / "FPTP is undemocratic"* — both overstate; the honest claim is about tendencies and trade-offs. *"Populism = any policy I dislike"* — it is a specific structural logic. *"Democracies die in coups"* — the 21st-century pattern is mostly incremental, legal-seeming backsliding. *"Money buys elections"* — spending correlates with winning, but money also flows to likely winners, so neither "money decides" nor "money doesn't matter" is defensible.

**Trap:** Treating "the will of the people" as a single coherent thing — Arrow and the paradox of voting show aggregation is genuinely hard and agenda-setting matters.

**Key facts:** "more democracy always better" + "experts always know best" = the two signature traps · Duverger/median-voter = tendencies not laws · compulsory-voting outcome contested · populism = precise logic · backsliding = incremental · money correlates, causation muddy.
