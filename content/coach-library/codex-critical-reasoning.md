# Critical Reasoning

*Coach house skill codex. Authored to the v2 standard for skill codices: substance not voice; globally and culturally neutral (argument topics on universal themes only — no real policies, parties, countries, or named people); every rule and every worked example has exactly one defensible answer; § is the citation unit; section order is the study plan.*

## Scope

This skill is the analysis of a short argument: finding its conclusion and assumptions, and judging what strengthens it, weakens it, follows from it, or is wrong with it. It covers argument anatomy, assumption identification, strengthen and weaken, flaw detection, inference, method and role analysis, parallel reasoning, and evaluation. It deliberately excludes formal symbolic logic (that is Logical Reasoning) and any real-world subject knowledge; every argument is self-contained and judged on its reasoning alone.

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** a short argument followed by a stem — find the conclusion, the necessary assumption, what strengthens/weakens it, the flaw, what must follow, the method or the role of a statement, the parallel argument, or the best evaluating question.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~30% recall/recognition (locating premise vs conclusion, naming a flaw type), ~50% application (selecting the strengthener, weakener, or assumption), ~20% multi-step (parallel reasoning, evaluate-the-argument).
- **In scope:** premise/conclusion/assumption structure; the negation test; strengthen; weaken; flaw types; inference; method and role; parallel reasoning; evaluation.
- **Out of scope:** the factual truth of an argument's content; formal validity notation (Logical Reasoning); rhetoric or style critique; any external knowledge.
- **Classic traps:** picking an answer that restates a premise (adds nothing); attacking the conclusion's truth instead of the reasoning; choosing a too-strong statement as the "necessary" assumption; matching a parallel argument by topic instead of structure.

## §1 Anatomy of an argument

Every argument breaks into the claim being made and the support offered for it, plus the unstated links it relies on.

**Rule:** An argument has premises (the stated support), a conclusion (the claim the premises are offered to support), and assumptions (unstated premises the conclusion requires).

**Worked example:** "The new process is faster than the old one, so the plant should adopt it." Method: ask what is being claimed (the recommendation "the plant should adopt it" — the conclusion) and what supports it ("the new process is faster" — the premise). Then ask what unstated link is needed: that being faster is a sufficient reason to adopt, with no overriding drawback (cost, quality) — the assumption. Answer: premise = it is faster; conclusion = adopt it; assumption = speed outweighs any downside.

**Trap:** Mistaking a premise for the conclusion — the conclusion is the claim the *other* statements are marshalled to support, not merely the last sentence.

**Key facts:** premise = stated support · conclusion = the supported claim · assumption = required unstated premise · the conclusion is what the rest is offered to prove.

## §2 Finding the conclusion

The conclusion is identified by function, not position — it is the point everything else is there to establish.

**Rule:** The conclusion is the statement the other statements support; test a candidate by inserting "therefore" before it and checking that the rest reads as its reasons.

**Worked example:** "Output has risen every month this year. The new equipment was installed in January. So the equipment is responsible for the higher output." Method: apply the "therefore" test to each sentence. "Therefore output has risen" — no, that is offered as evidence. "Therefore the equipment is responsible" — yes, the other two sentences read as reasons for it. Answer: the conclusion is **"the equipment is responsible for the higher output."**

**Trap:** Picking the first or most striking sentence; the conclusion is whichever claim the others are given to support, which may appear anywhere in the passage.

**Key facts:** conclusion = the claim the rest supports · use the "therefore" test · position does not determine it · evidence sentences are premises, not the conclusion.

## §3 The necessary assumption and the negation test

A necessary assumption is a link the argument cannot do without — and negating it collapses the argument.

**Rule:** A necessary assumption is one the argument requires to hold; if you negate it and the conclusion no longer follows, it is necessary (the negation test).

**Worked example:** "Plants in room A grew taller than those in room B; room A had more light; therefore the extra light caused the difference." Method: a necessary assumption is that the rooms did not differ in another growth-relevant factor (such as water). Apply the negation test: negate it — "room A also had more water" — and the light-caused-it conclusion collapses, because water could explain the difference. Since negating it destroys the argument, it is necessary. Answer: the argument assumes **the rooms were alike in other growth factors** (no alternative cause).

**Trap:** Choosing a statement stronger than needed (e.g. "light is the only thing that affects growth"); a necessary assumption is the *minimum* the argument requires, and the negation test is what identifies it.

**Key facts:** necessary assumption = required for the conclusion · negation test: negate it → if the argument fails, it is necessary · choose the minimum needed, not the strongest possible · here: no alternative cause differed.

## §4 Strengthen

A strengthener raises the probability that the conclusion is true, most often by ruling out an alternative explanation.

**Rule:** A strengthener makes the conclusion more likely — commonly by closing off an alternative cause or supplying a missing link — without merely repeating a premise.

**Worked example:** Same argument: the extra light caused room A's taller plants. Method: the conclusion is threatened by other possible causes, so a statement that removes them strengthens it. "The two rooms were identical in water, temperature, and soil" rules out the main alternatives, leaving light as the remaining difference. Answer: the strengthener is **"the rooms were identical in all other growth factors."**

**Trap:** Selecting a statement that just restates the premise ("room A had more light") — it adds no new support; a strengthener must supply *new* grounds, typically by eliminating an alternative.

**Key facts:** strengthener = raises the conclusion's probability · ruling out an alternative cause is the strongest move · restating a premise adds nothing · it need not prove the conclusion, only support it.

## §5 Weaken

A weakener lowers the probability that the conclusion is true, most often by introducing an alternative cause the argument overlooked.

**Rule:** A weakener makes the conclusion less likely — commonly by supplying an alternative explanation or breaking a link the argument depends on.

**Worked example:** Same argument. Method: the causal conclusion is vulnerable to a rival cause, so introducing one weakens it. "Room A also received considerably more water than room B" offers an alternative explanation for the taller plants, undercutting the claim that *light* was responsible. Answer: the weakener is **"room A also had more water."**

**Trap:** Choosing an irrelevant difference (e.g. the rooms had different wall colours) that does not bear on plant growth — a weakener must plausibly affect the outcome in question.

**Key facts:** weakener = lowers the conclusion's probability · an alternative cause is the strongest weakener · the difference must be relevant to the outcome · weakening need not disprove, only reduce support.

## §6 Flaw in the reasoning

A flaw question asks you to name the *type* of reasoning error, not to dispute the conclusion's truth.

**Rule:** A flaw is a defect in how the premises support the conclusion — such as treating correlation as causation, generalising from an unrepresentative sample, or attacking the source instead of the claim.

**Worked example:** "Everyone I surveyed at the training centre exercises daily, so most people exercise daily." Method: identify how the support fails to establish the conclusion. The sample (people at a training centre) is not representative of "most people," who were never sampled — a generalisation from an unrepresentative group. Answer: the flaw is **drawing a general conclusion from an unrepresentative sample.**

**Trap:** Arguing that the conclusion is false; a flaw answer names the *reasoning* error, and the conclusion could even be true while the argument for it remains flawed.

**Key facts:** flaw = a defect in the premise-to-conclusion link · common types: correlation→causation, unrepresentative sample, attacking the source · name the error, do not dispute the conclusion · a flawed argument can still have a true conclusion.

## §7 Inference and what must follow

An inference question wants what the statements *guarantee*, not what is merely plausible.

**Rule:** A valid inference is a statement that must be true given the premises; the correct answer is the one the premises deductively guarantee, not one they only make likely.

**Worked example:** "All items in box 1 are red. Some items in box 1 are heavy. Therefore, some red items are heavy." Method: the heavy items are in box 1, and everything in box 1 is red, so those heavy items are red — hence some red items are heavy, which *must* be true. Compare a merely plausible option like "most red items are heavy," which the premises do not guarantee. Answer: the supported inference is **"some red items are heavy."**

**Trap:** Selecting a likely-sounding option that the premises do not force; an inference must be guaranteed, so anything the premises leave open is wrong.

**Key facts:** inference = must be true given the premises · guaranteed, not merely likely · "some box-1 items are heavy + all box-1 are red" → "some red items are heavy" · reject options the premises leave open.

## §8 Method of reasoning and the role of a statement

These questions ask *how* the argument works or *what job* a particular sentence does within it.

**Rule:** A method question describes the argument's strategy; a role question identifies a specified sentence's function — premise, conclusion, an example, or a consideration raised in order to be rejected.

**Worked example:** "Some claim the longer route is faster because it avoids the junction. But timing shows it takes longer overall. So the shorter route is the quicker choice." Method: identify the function of the first sentence. It states a view ("the longer route is faster") that the author then rebuts with timing evidence — so it is a consideration raised in order to be rejected, not the author's own position. Answer: the first sentence's role is **an opposing claim the author introduces in order to argue against it.**

**Trap:** Treating a stated counter-view as the author's own conclusion — a sentence the author raises only to reject serves the opposite function to a premise or conclusion.

**Key facts:** method = the argument's overall strategy · role = one sentence's function · a raised-then-rejected view is not the author's position · distinguish premise, conclusion, example, and counter-consideration.

## §9 Parallel reasoning

The correct parallel argument shares the target's logical structure, whatever its subject matter.

**Rule:** A parallel argument matches the original in logical form — the pattern of premises and conclusion — not in topic, tone, or wording.

**Worked example:** Original: "If the valve is open, the tank drains. The tank did not drain. So the valve was not open." Method: extract the form — "if P then Q; not Q; therefore not P" (a valid contrapositive pattern). The parallel is any argument with that same form, e.g. "If the seed is viable, it sprouts. The seed did not sprout. So it was not viable." A candidate on the same topic but with the form "if P then Q; P; therefore Q" would *not* parallel it. Answer: the parallel is the argument sharing the **"if P then Q; not Q; so not P"** structure.

**Trap:** Matching on subject matter (another argument about valves and tanks) rather than on logical structure — parallelism is about form alone.

**Key facts:** parallel = same logical form · ignore topic, tone, and wording · match the pattern of premises and conclusion · "if P then Q; not Q; so not P" pairs only with the same structure.

## §10 Evaluate the argument

To evaluate an argument, find the question whose answer would most change how well the conclusion is supported.

**Rule:** The best evaluating question is the one whose two possible answers would swing the argument in opposite directions — strengthening it under one answer and weakening it under the other.

**Worked example:** Argument: the extra light in room A caused its taller plants. Method: look for the question whose answer decides the conclusion. "Did the two rooms differ in water supply?" is such a question: if *no*, the alternative-cause objection falls away and the argument is strengthened; if *yes*, an alternative cause appears and it is weakened. A question like "What colour were the walls?" fails this test, since neither answer bears on growth. Answer: the evaluating question is **"did the rooms differ in another growth factor such as water?"**

**Trap:** Choosing a question whose answer leaves the conclusion unchanged; a useful evaluating question must have answers that pull the argument in opposite directions.

**Key facts:** best evaluate-question = its answers swing the argument opposite ways · it targets the argument's key assumption · irrelevant questions do not change the conclusion · here: whether another growth factor differed.
