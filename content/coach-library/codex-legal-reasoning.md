# Legal Reasoning (jurisdiction-neutral)

*Coach house skill codex. Authored to the v2 standard for skill codices: substance not voice; globally and culturally neutral; every rule and every worked example has exactly one defensible conclusion; § is the citation unit; section order is the study plan.*

**Hard rule of this codex:** the legal rule or principle needed is **always supplied in the question**. This subject teaches the *application skill* only — never legal knowledge. Every principle below is **invented and neutral** ("Principle: a person who…"); no real statute, jurisdiction, or named case is ever cited. This is what keeps the skill global and legally safe.

## Scope

This skill is applying a *given* rule or principle to a set of facts to reach the single conclusion the rule dictates. It covers reading a rule precisely, applying it to facts, working from a supplied principle or precedent, identifying material facts, spotting the issue, distinguishing, and resolving competing principles. It deliberately excludes all substantive law and legal knowledge — the rule you need is always provided — and excludes moral opinion about whether the rule is good.

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** a supplied principle (or precedent) plus a fact pattern, with a stem asking for the single best conclusion, the material fact, the issue, or whether a precedent applies.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~30% recall/recognition (reading a rule's elements correctly), ~50% application (applying a rule or principle to facts), ~20% multi-step (analogy, distinguishing, competing principles).
- **In scope:** reading a rule's elements, conditions, and exceptions; applying a supplied rule to facts; principle-application; identifying material facts; analogical reasoning from a supplied precedent; issue-spotting; distinguishing; reasoning under competing principles.
- **Out of scope:** any real law, statute, jurisdiction, or case; whether a rule is just or wise; facts not supplied in the question.
- **Classic traps:** importing outside legal knowledge the supplied rule does not contain; ignoring a stated exception; treating a vivid but immaterial fact as decisive; distinguishing a precedent on an immaterial difference.

## §1 What legal reasoning tests

The subject tests whether you can apply a rule you are given — not whether you know any law.

**Rule:** A legal-reasoning question supplies the rule you need; your task is to apply it exactly to the facts, using no outside legal knowledge.

**Worked example:** *Principle: anyone who enters land without the occupier's permission commits trespass.* Facts: X walks through an open gate into a fenced garden, believing it to be a public park. Method: apply the principle's terms — entry occurred, and it was without the occupier's permission — so trespass is committed. The principle contains no exception for honest mistake, so X's belief does not matter, however tempting outside knowledge of "mistake defences" might be. Answer: **X has committed trespass.**

**Trap:** Importing an outside rule the principle does not state (such as "an honest mistake is a defence") — apply only the supplied rule.

**Key facts:** the rule is always supplied · apply it, do not add to it · no outside legal knowledge · X entered without permission → trespass, belief is irrelevant here.

## §2 Reading a rule precisely

A rule is made of elements, conditions, and exceptions; the conclusion follows only when every element is met and no exception applies.

**Rule:** Break a supplied rule into its elements (all of which must be satisfied), its conditions, and its exceptions; the outcome follows only if every element holds and no exception is triggered.

**Worked example:** *Principle: a person is liable for damage if (a) they lit a fire, (b) on their own land, and (c) the fire spread to a neighbour's land — unless (d) an unforeseeable natural event caused the spread.* Facts: P lit a fire on P's own land; a sudden, unforeseeable windstorm then carried it to Q's land. Method: check each element — (a) P lit a fire ✓, (b) on P's own land ✓, (c) it spread to Q's land ✓ — so the base rule is met. But test the exception: (d) an unforeseeable natural event (the freak windstorm) caused the spread ✓, so the exception applies. Answer: **P is not liable**, because the exception is triggered.

**Trap:** Confirming the elements and stopping there, ignoring a stated exception that reverses the outcome.

**Key facts:** elements = all must be met · check conditions and exceptions too · an exception can reverse a met rule · here (a)(b)(c) met but exception (d) applies → not liable.

## §3 Applying a supplied rule to facts

When every element of a rule is satisfied and no exception applies, the conclusion is fixed.

**Rule:** If the facts satisfy every element of the supplied rule and trigger no exception, the rule's stated consequence follows.

**Worked example:** *Principle: a seller must refund a buyer if the goods were defective at the time of sale.* Facts: a buyer purchases an item that was already broken when it was sold. Method: the single element is "defective at the time of sale," and the facts state the item was already broken when sold, so the element is met and there is no exception. Answer: **the seller must refund the buyer.**

**Trap:** Adding a condition the rule does not contain (such as "only if the buyer reports it within a week") — apply the rule exactly as supplied.

**Key facts:** all elements met + no exception → the stated consequence · "defective at the time of sale" is met · do not invent extra conditions · the outcome is whatever the rule states.

## §4 Principle-application questions

A principle-application question gives a principle and facts and asks for the conclusion the principle produces.

**Rule:** Apply the supplied principle to the facts by checking which of its branches the facts fall under, and read off the branch's consequence.

**Worked example:** *Principle: a person who finds lost property must return it to the owner if the owner can be identified; otherwise the finder may keep it.* Facts: the finder discovers a wallet containing the owner's name and address. Method: the principle has two branches — owner identifiable (must return) or not identifiable (may keep). The wallet contains the owner's name and address, so the owner *can* be identified, placing the facts in the first branch. Answer: **the finder must return the wallet.**

**Trap:** Choosing the "may keep it" branch by overlooking the identifiability condition that the facts clearly satisfy.

**Key facts:** match the facts to the principle's branch · identifiable owner → must return · unidentifiable → may keep · the name and address make the owner identifiable → return.

## §5 Identifying the material facts

Material facts are those that decide whether an element of the rule is met; other facts, however vivid, do not change the outcome.

**Rule:** A material fact is one that determines whether an element of the supplied rule is satisfied; an immaterial fact does not affect the outcome, however prominent it seems.

**Worked example:** *Principle: anyone who enters land without the occupier's permission commits trespass.* Facts: at dusk, wearing a bright red coat, R climbs over a locked gate into a garden after the occupier refused entry. Method: test which facts bear on the rule's elements — "entry" and "without permission." The refusal of entry (no permission) and the climbing in (entry) are *material*; the time of day and the red coat do not affect whether the elements are met, so they are *immaterial*. Answer: the material fact is **that the occupier had refused permission** (making the entry unpermitted).

**Trap:** Treating a vivid but immaterial detail (the red coat, the time of day) as decisive — only facts that determine an element are material.

**Key facts:** material fact = decides whether an element is met · immaterial facts do not change the outcome · refusal of permission is material; the coat is not · strip the facts to those that touch the rule's elements.

## §6 Analogical reasoning from a supplied precedent

A supplied precedent applies to new facts when their *material* facts match; matching on immaterial similarities does not count.

**Rule:** Apply a supplied precedent to new facts by matching the *material* facts; if the material facts align, the precedent's outcome follows.

**Worked example:** *Precedent: a café was held liable when a customer slipped on a spill that staff knew about but had not cleaned up.* New facts: in a shop, staff knew of a leak but left it, and a customer slipped on it. Method: identify the precedent's material facts — a hazard the staff *knew about* and *failed to address*, causing a slip. The new facts match on exactly these material points (known hazard + failure to act + a slip); that both are businesses is an immaterial similarity but the material ones align. Answer: **the shop is liable by analogy to the precedent.**

**Trap:** Matching on an immaterial similarity ("both are businesses") or ignoring the material match — the analogy holds on the facts that drove the precedent's outcome.

**Key facts:** apply a precedent by matching material facts · here: known hazard + failure to act + slip · immaterial similarities (both businesses) do not carry the analogy · aligned material facts → same outcome.

## §7 Spotting the issue

The issue is the precise element of the supplied rule whose satisfaction is genuinely in doubt on the facts.

**Rule:** The issue is the exact element of the rule that the facts leave in genuine dispute — the one point on which the outcome turns.

**Worked example:** *Principle: anyone who enters land without the occupier's permission commits trespass.* Facts: it is agreed that Y walked into the garden; the parties dispute whether the occupier had given Y permission to do so. Method: check each element against the facts — "entry" is agreed (not in doubt), but "without permission" is contested (Y says permission was given, the occupier denies it). The element in genuine dispute is permission. Answer: the issue is **whether the occupier gave Y permission to enter.**

**Trap:** Stating a broad topic ("this is about property rights") instead of the precise contested element — the issue is the specific point the outcome turns on.

**Key facts:** issue = the element genuinely in dispute · undisputed elements are not the issue · here: entry is agreed, permission is contested → permission is the issue · name the precise point, not the general topic.

## §8 Distinguishing and qualifying

A precedent is distinguished by finding a *material* difference in the facts that makes its rule inapplicable.

**Rule:** To distinguish a supplied precedent, identify a material difference between its facts and the new facts — a difference that bears on the element the precedent turned on — so its outcome does not carry over.

**Worked example:** *Precedent: an occupier was liable for a slip on a hazard the staff knew about but did not address.* New facts: a customer slipped on a spill that had occurred seconds earlier, which no staff member knew about or could reasonably have discovered. Method: the precedent turned on the staff *knowing* of the hazard; the new facts differ materially on exactly that point — here there was no knowledge and no reasonable chance to discover it. That material difference makes the precedent inapplicable. Answer: **the precedent is distinguished, and the occupier is not liable on these facts.**

**Trap:** Distinguishing on an immaterial difference (a different type of shop, a different day) — only a difference touching the element the precedent turned on will distinguish it.

**Key facts:** distinguish by a *material* difference · the difference must touch the element the precedent turned on · no knowledge of the hazard vs. known hazard = material · immaterial differences do not distinguish.

## §9 Reasoning under competing principles

When two supplied principles point to opposite outcomes, apply the one whose stated scope or specificity governs the facts.

**Rule:** When two supplied principles conflict on the facts, apply the one that more specifically governs the situation (or that the question states takes priority); the more specific principle controls over the more general.

**Worked example:** *Principle 1: an occupier may exclude any person from their land. Principle 2: a person may enter another's land without permission to save a life in immediate danger.* Facts: X enters Y's land without permission to pull a child out of a burning building. Method: both principles bear on the facts and point opposite ways — Principle 1 would make the entry wrongful, Principle 2 would permit it. Principle 2 speaks specifically to the emergency (saving a life in immediate danger), which is exactly the situation here, so the more specific principle governs. Answer: **X's entry is justified** under the more specific life-saving principle.

**Trap:** Mechanically applying the general principle (exclusion) and ignoring the specific principle that squarely fits the emergency facts.

**Key facts:** conflicting principles → the more specific one controls · match each principle's scope to the facts · the life-saving principle specifically covers the emergency · a stated priority in the question also decides.
