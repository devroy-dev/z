## Scope

This skill is standard-English syntax and usage — the rules that make a sentence correct and clear. It covers sentence structure, agreement, tense consistency, pronoun use, modifiers, prepositions and idiom, articles, parallelism, punctuation, and commonly confused words. It deliberately excludes creative style, rhetoric, and vocabulary breadth (that is Reading Comprehension), and any content requiring outside knowledge; every example is a self-contained sentence.

## §1 Sentence structure

A complete sentence needs a subject and a finite verb forming an independent clause; a subordinate clause alone is a fragment.

**Rule:** A grammatical sentence contains at least one independent clause — a subject paired with a finite (tensed) verb — that can stand on its own.

**Worked example:** "Because the machine stopped." Method: this clause opens with the subordinator "because," which makes it dependent, and it has no independent clause to complete the thought — so it is a fragment. Attach a main clause: "The line halted because the machine stopped." Now "the line halted" is an independent clause (subject "the line," finite verb "halted"). Answer: the corrected sentence is **"The line halted because the machine stopped."**

**Trap:** Treating a subordinate clause (opening with because, although, when, if, that) as a full sentence — it needs an independent clause to attach to.

**Key facts:** a sentence needs an independent clause · independent = subject + finite verb, standing alone · subordinators (because, although, when) make a clause dependent · a dependent clause alone is a fragment.

## §2 Subject–verb agreement

A verb agrees in number with its grammatical subject, no matter what words come between them.

**Rule:** A verb agrees with its subject in number; intervening phrases (of…, with…, as well as…) do not change the subject's number.

**Worked example:** "The box of tools ___ heavy." (is / are) Method: find the true subject — "box" (singular); "of tools" is a prepositional phrase modifying it, not the subject. A singular subject takes the singular verb "is." Answer: **"The box of tools is heavy."** (The plural "tools" sits next to the verb but is not the subject.)

**Trap:** Agreeing the verb with the nearest noun ("tools … are") instead of the head noun of the subject ("box … is").

**Key facts:** verb agrees with the subject's number · phrases after "of/with/as well as" do not change it · "the box of tools is" (singular) · note: in British usage a collective noun (team, committee) may take a plural verb when the members act individually — a point of British/American divergence.

## §3 Tense and aspect consistency

Keep the tense consistent within a sentence or passage unless the time frame genuinely shifts.

**Rule:** Verbs in a connected sentence should stay in one tense unless a real change in time requires a shift.

**Worked example:** "She finished the report and submits it." Method: "finished" is past and "submits" is present, but both actions belong to the same past sequence, so the tenses should match. Correct the second verb to past: "She finished the report and submitted it." Answer: **"She finished the report and submitted it."**

**Trap:** Shifting from past to present (or vice versa) mid-sentence with no change in the actual time frame.

**Key facts:** keep one tense unless the time frame changes · past + past for a single past sequence · "finished … submitted" · a genuine time shift (a past habit and a present result) can justify a change.

## §4 Pronoun reference and agreement

A pronoun must point unmistakably to one antecedent and match it in number.

**Rule:** A pronoun must refer to exactly one clear antecedent; if two nouns could be the referent, the sentence must be rewritten to remove the ambiguity.

**Worked example:** "When the manager met the director, she was nervous." Method: "she" could refer to the manager or the director — the reference is ambiguous. Rewrite so the pronoun has one possible antecedent: "The manager was nervous when she met the director." Now "she" can only be the manager. Answer: **"The manager was nervous when she met the director."**

**Trap:** Leaving a pronoun that could attach to two earlier nouns; ambiguous reference is corrected by rewriting, not by guessing the intended noun.

**Key facts:** a pronoun needs one clear antecedent · two possible referents = ambiguous, so rewrite · match number: a singular antecedent takes a singular pronoun · singular "they" is accepted in contemporary Commonwealth usage for an indefinite antecedent (anyone, each person).

## §5 Modifiers: misplaced and dangling

A modifier must sit beside the word it describes, and an opening modifier must have a logical subject to attach to.

**Rule:** Place a modifier next to the word it modifies; an introductory modifying phrase must logically describe the subject of the main clause, or it dangles.

**Worked example:** "Walking to the station, the rain started." Method: the opening phrase "walking to the station" should describe the subject of the main clause, but the stated subject is "the rain," which cannot walk — a dangling modifier. Supply the logical subject: "Walking to the station, I felt the rain start" (or "As I walked to the station, the rain started"). Answer: **"As I walked to the station, the rain started."**

**Trap:** Beginning with an "-ing" phrase whose doer is not the subject of the following clause — the phrase then attaches to the wrong thing.

**Key facts:** a modifier belongs next to what it modifies · an intro phrase must describe the main-clause subject · "walking to the station" needs a person, not "the rain," as the subject · fix by supplying the logical doer.

## §6 Prepositions and idiom

Standard English fixes particular prepositions to particular verbs and adjectives; the correct pairing is idiomatic, not deducible.

**Rule:** Certain words take a fixed preposition by idiom; use the established pairing (for example, "comply with," "different from," "capable of").

**Worked example:** "All staff must comply ___ the rules." Method: the verb "comply" idiomatically takes "with," not "to" or "for." Answer: **"All staff must comply with the rules."** (Similarly "different from" is standard; "capable of," "responsible for," "consist of" follow fixed pairings.)

**Trap:** Substituting a plausible but non-standard preposition ("comply to," "different than" in formal British usage) — the correct particle is set by idiom and must be learned per word.

**Key facts:** verb/adjective + preposition pairings are fixed by idiom · "comply with," "different from," "capable of," "responsible for" · the pairing is not deducible from meaning · learn the standard particle for each word.

## §7 Articles and determiners

Articles are chosen by the sound and the definiteness of the noun that follows, not by spelling alone.

**Rule:** Use "a/an" for a non-specific singular countable noun ("an" before a vowel *sound*), "the" for a specific or already-known referent, and no article for general plurals and uncountables.

**Worked example:** "She is ___ honest worker." (a / an) Method: choose by the initial *sound*, not the letter. "Honest" begins with a silent "h," so it starts with a vowel sound, taking "an." Answer: **"She is an honest worker."** (By contrast "a university" takes "a," because "university" begins with a consonant *sound*, /juː/, despite the vowel letter.)

**Trap:** Choosing the article by spelling (seeing "h" and picking "a") instead of by the sound the word actually begins with.

**Key facts:** "a/an" = non-specific singular countable; "the" = specific/known · "an" before a vowel *sound* · "an honest," "a university" (by sound) · no article for general plurals and uncountables.

## §8 Parallelism

Items joined in a list or by coordination must share the same grammatical form.

**Rule:** Elements in a series or a coordinated structure must be grammatically parallel — all nouns, all "-ing" forms, or all infinitives, consistently.

**Worked example:** "The role involves planning, to organise, and execution." Method: the three items mix a gerund ("planning"), an infinitive ("to organise"), and a noun ("execution"). Make them one consistent form; using gerunds throughout gives "planning, organising, and executing." Answer: **"The role involves planning, organising, and executing."**

**Trap:** Mixing forms in a list (a gerund, then an infinitive, then a noun) — every item after "involves" must take the same grammatical shape.

**Key facts:** series items must share one grammatical form · all gerunds, all infinitives, or all nouns · "planning, organising, and executing" · parallelism applies to items joined by and/or and by correlative pairs (both…and, not only…but also).

## §9 Punctuation

Two independent clauses cannot be joined by a comma alone; that is a comma splice.

**Rule:** Join two independent clauses with a comma *plus* a coordinating conjunction (and, but, so…), or with a semicolon, or write them as separate sentences — a comma alone is a comma splice.

**Worked example:** "The test was hard, everyone passed." Method: "the test was hard" and "everyone passed" are both independent clauses, joined by only a comma — a comma splice. Repair it in a standard way: add a conjunction ("The test was hard, but everyone passed") or use a semicolon ("The test was hard; everyone passed"). Answer: **"The test was hard, but everyone passed."**

**Trap:** Splicing two full clauses with a comma; the comma needs a coordinating conjunction after it, or must become a semicolon or full stop.

**Key facts:** two independent clauses need comma + conjunction, a semicolon, or a full stop · comma alone = comma splice · "The test was hard, but everyone passed" · the serial (Oxford) comma is optional in Commonwealth usage — be consistent within a text.

## §10 Commonly confused words

Certain word pairs are distinguished by their grammatical function; the correct choice follows from that function.

**Rule:** Distinguish confusable pairs by function — "its" is possessive while "it's" means "it is"; "fewer" counts countable nouns while "less" measures uncountable ones.

**Worked example:** "The company lost ___ licence." (its / it's) Method: the blank needs a possessive ("the licence belonging to the company"), and the possessive form is "its" (no apostrophe); "it's" would expand to "it is," which does not fit. Answer: **"The company lost its licence."** (Note the British spelling: "licence" is the noun, "license" the verb; also "fewer errors" for countable, "less time" for uncountable.)

**Trap:** Writing "it's" for the possessive — the apostrophe form always means "it is" (or "it has"), never "belonging to it."

**Key facts:** its = possessive; it's = it is/has · fewer = countable, less = uncountable · British: "licence" (noun) vs "license" (verb) · their/there/they're and affect (verb)/effect (noun) follow the same function test.
