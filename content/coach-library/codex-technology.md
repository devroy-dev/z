# Technology & Society Governance

*Subject-knowledge coach codex. Authored under the subject-knowledge variant: real named content; every Rule is a defensible fact; the normative verdict of a contested debate is taught in the Worked example (both sides shown), never keyed. Focus is governance and social effects; deep technical detail is out of scope — reason from mechanisms and evidence.*

## Scope

This subject asks who should control powerful technologies, who bears the cost when they cause harm, and how to regulate fast-moving innovation without freezing its benefits — spanning AI, data and surveillance, platform market power, automation and labour, digital speech, and the contest between an open internet and digital sovereignty. It covers the vocabulary, the competing regulatory, economic, and philosophical frameworks, the factual record, the live debates, and the recurring myths. It excludes the verdict of those debates — the learner names the specific mechanism, the harmed party, and the innovation trade-off, rather than being told which regulation is right.

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** define a term; attribute a concept or work to its thinker; state the mechanism (network effect, externality, chilling effect, task displacement); identify the standard objection to a framework; identify what a debate turns on; correct a common myth.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~35% recall (definitions, attributions), ~45% application (state the mechanism, match a framework to its claim, name the objection), ~20% multi-step (specify the harm mechanism and harmed party; weigh against the innovation cost).
- **In scope:** definitions; each framework's claim, mechanism, strongest case, and objection; the factual record; the myths and corrections.
- **Out of scope:** the verdict of a contested debate. No item keys "should platforms be broken up" or "will AI kill us." Current products and figures move fast and are treated as illustrative.
- **Classic traps:** the lump-of-labour fallacy *and* its mirror ("painless reallocation"); the Luddite caricature; "this time is different" asserted not argued; "platforms are neutral" / "AI is objective"; "big = bad" / "big = fine" without a harm mechanism; asserting AI existential risk as settled in either direction.

## §1 The Collingridge dilemma and the precaution-vs-permission axis

The master problem of technology governance and the two regulatory defaults.

**Rule:** The Collingridge dilemma (David Collingridge, *The Social Control of Technology*, 1980) is that early in a technology's life it is easy to shape but its effects are unknown, and by the time the effects are clear the technology is entrenched and hard to change; the precautionary principle shifts the burden onto innovators to show safety, while permissionless innovation (Adam Thierer) presumes innovation is allowed and addresses harms as they arise.

**Worked example:** The dilemma is a timing trap: regulate too early and you may freeze benefits and entrench incumbents; too late and harms are locked in and hard to reverse. The precautionary principle says act to prevent serious or irreversible harm even under uncertainty; permissionless innovation says most predicted harms never materialise and ex-ante restriction forfeits gains. Every "regulate now vs. later" clash is structured by this axis.

**Trap:** Misusing the precautionary principle as "ban anything uncertain" — the honest version weighs *both* directions of risk, including the harms of *not* innovating (foregone medicines, safety, growth).

**Key facts:** Collingridge dilemma = early shapeable/unknown vs. late known/entrenched (1980) · precautionary = burden on innovator · permissionless = Thierer, harms addressed as they arise.

## §2 Externalities, network effects, and platform power

The economic mechanisms behind platform dominance.

**Rule:** An externality is a cost imposed on third parties not reflected in a transaction (an internality is a cost imposed on one's own future self); network effects make a product more valuable as more people use it, driving "winner-take-most" dynamics, and data moats plus switching costs entrench that dominance.

**Worked example:** The mechanism chain: direct network effects (more users, more value to each) and indirect effects (a large user base attracts complementors) produce durable scale; two-sided markets (users and advertisers) let pricing on one side subsidise the other; accumulated data improves products in ways rivals can't match; and lock-in (your data, contacts, and habits live in the platform) makes leaving costly, blunting competition even absent formal monopoly. This is why "just let competitors enter" understates the barrier.

**Trap:** Ignoring that platform dominance rests on *real* mechanisms — network effects, data advantages, switching costs — so "just let rivals compete" is naïve, while "break them up" must reckon with the consumer benefits of scale.

**Key facts:** externality vs. internality · network effects (direct + indirect) · data moat + switching costs = lock-in · two-sided markets · dominance rests on real mechanisms.

## §3 Antitrust standards and liability regimes

The two antitrust philosophies and the liability choices.

**Rule:** The consumer-welfare standard (Robert Bork, *The Antitrust Paradox*, 1978) treats conduct as anticompetitive only if it harms consumers (typically via higher prices or reduced output); the neo-Brandeisian / structuralist view (Lina Khan, "Amazon's Antitrust Paradox," 2017; Tim Wu, *The Curse of Bigness*, 2018) holds concentration of power is itself a harm even absent price rises.

**Worked example:** On liability, *strict liability* holds an actor liable for harm regardless of fault (forcing internalisation and prevention), while *negligence/fault-based* liability applies only to failing a standard of care; *intermediary liability* asks whether a platform is responsible for user content, with *safe harbors* (immunity if conditions are met) shaping the whole internet. A cross-cutting choice is *ex ante* regulation (up-front rules) vs. *ex post* liability (courts after the fact), each with a characteristic failure (over-caution/capture; under-deterrence/unattributable harm).

**Trap:** "Big = bad" (structuralist reflex) or "big = fine" (Chicago reflex) without specifying the harm mechanism — concentration can bring real consumer benefits *and* real harms.

**Key facts:** consumer-welfare = Bork (1978), price/output harm · neo-Brandeisian = Khan/Wu, concentration is harm · strict vs. fault liability · safe harbors · ex ante vs. ex post.

## §4 Privacy, surveillance, and the base-rate problem

The conceptions of privacy and the analytical hazard of mass surveillance.

**Rule:** Privacy is theorised as control over information (Westin, 1967), contextual integrity (information flows should match the norms of the context — Nissenbaum), or dignity vs. property, with surveillance capitalism (Zuboff, 2019) naming the model of extracting behavioural data to predict and shape behaviour; the base-rate problem is that for rare threats in mass data, even a very accurate detector produces overwhelmingly false positives.

**Worked example:** The chilling effect (surveillance deters lawful speech and association even without coercion — Bentham's panopticon via Foucault, *Discipline and Punish*, 1975) and function creep (surveillance built for one purpose expands to others) are the systemic harms. The base-rate problem is the statistical Achilles' heel of *dragnet* surveillance: screening millions for a rare threat floods the system with false positives while catching few real threats — which is why *targeted* surveillance has a far stronger case than mass collection.

**Trap:** "If you have nothing to hide, you have nothing to fear" — ignores the chilling effect, the base-rate problem, function creep, and the irreversibility of surveillance infrastructure.

**Key facts:** privacy = control (Westin) / contextual integrity (Nissenbaum) / dignity · surveillance capitalism = Zuboff (2019) · chilling effect + panopticon (Foucault) · base-rate problem = dragnet's weakness.

## §5 Automation vocabulary and the AI-governance frames

The labour-economics terms and the AI-safety lens.

**Rule:** Technological unemployment (Keynes, 1930) names automation-driven job loss; the lump-of-labour fallacy is the false belief that the amount of work is fixed; the task framework (David Autor) holds technology automates *tasks*, not whole *jobs*, and can *complement* the rest; the productivity paradox (Solow, 1987 — "you can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics") notes new general-purpose technologies take decades to show up in measured productivity.

**Worked example:** The task framework reframes "will AI take jobs" into "which tasks will it automate, and does that raise or lower demand for the human complement?" Related terms: skill-biased technical change and job polarisation ("hollowing out" the middle); Moravec's paradox (tasks easy for humans are hard for machines); the alignment problem (ensuring advanced systems pursue intended goals — Russell, *Human Compatible*, 2019) and existential risk (Bostrom, *Superintelligence*, 2014), a contested but influential lens; and Jevons paradox (efficiency gains can *increase* total use — Jevons, 1865).

**Trap:** Confusing narrow AI with hypothetical general AI — debating today's specific tools as if they were speculative superintelligence, or vice versa; keep the referent clear.

**Key facts:** technological unemployment = Keynes (1930) · lump-of-labour fallacy · task framework = Autor · productivity paradox = Solow (1987) · alignment = Russell; existential risk = Bostrom · Jevons paradox (1865).

## §6 Framework: permissionless innovation vs the precautionary principle

**Rule:** Permissionless innovation defaults to allowing innovation and addressing harms as concrete evidence emerges; the precautionary principle shifts the burden onto innovators to demonstrate safety where serious harm is plausible.

**Worked example:** *Permissionless* — **strongest:** most technologies' harms are over-predicted and their benefits under-imagined, and ex-ante restriction entrenches incumbents (who can afford compliance) and forfeits gains; **objection:** for catastrophic or irreversible potential, "wait and see" may be too late (the Collingridge trap). *Precautionary* — **strongest:** some harms are systemic, irreversible, and cannot be undone once realised; **objection:** taken strictly it can paralyse beneficial innovation and is *itself* risky, because the harms of *not* innovating are real too.

**Trap:** Treating either default as free — permissionless ignores irreversible tail risk; strict precaution ignores the real harms of foregone innovation.

**Key facts:** permissionless = allow, address harms as they arise · precautionary = burden on innovator · Collingridge trap vs. paralysis · both directions of risk are real.

## §7 Framework: theories of technology and society

**Rule:** Technological determinism holds technology follows its own logic and drives social change; the social construction of technology (SCOT — Pinch and Bijker) holds technologies are shaped by social groups and choices; the politics-of-artifacts view (Langdon Winner, "Do Artifacts Have Politics?", 1980) holds technologies embed political arrangements.

**Worked example:** *Determinism* — **strongest:** some technologies reshape social life broadly and predictably; **objection:** it erases the human choices (design, funding, regulation, adoption) that shape use. *SCOT* — the same artifact develops differently under different social pressures, explaining why "the same" technology has wildly different effects across societies. *Winner* — some designs concentrate power, others distribute it, so governance must attend to *design*, not just use. Critical/substantivist traditions (Mumford, Ellul, Postman) warn technological systems can subordinate human ends — with the objection that they can slide into fatalism.

**Trap:** Technological determinism — treating a technology as an autonomous force that "does" things to society, ignoring the design choices, incentives, and regulation that shape its effects.

**Key facts:** determinism = tech drives society · SCOT = Pinch/Bijker, socially shaped · politics of artifacts = Winner (1980) · substantivism = Mumford/Ellul/Postman.

## §8 Framework: platform power, liability, surveillance, automation

**Rule:** Structural remedies (breakups, interoperability, data portability) are argued where network effects and data moats make dominance durable; strict vs. fault-based liability differ in incentive effects; surveillance debates weigh security against liberty; and automation debates split into augmentation-optimist and "this time is different" pessimist camps.

**Worked example:** *Platform power* — structuralists say conduct rules can't cure network-effect dominance (self-preferencing, killer acquisitions, essential facilities), while consumer-welfare defenders say scale delivers real benefits and "big" isn't "bad." *Liability* — strict liability internalises harm and forces safety but may chill innovation and is hard to apply when causation runs through many hands; fault-based enables innovation but under-deters where the standard is unclear. *Surveillance* — the pro side cites prevented catastrophic harm, the con side the base-rate problem, chilling effect, function creep, and irreversibility. *Automation* — optimists cite historical reallocation and the task framework (the ATM/teller case), pessimists cite AI's breadth across cognitive tasks (Brynjolfsson & McAfee, *The Second Machine Age*, 2014); the honest debate is about *distribution and speed of transition*.

**Trap:** Leaning on "reallocation is painless" (optimist) or "displacement is permanent mass unemployment" (pessimist) without a mechanism — the honest question is distribution and speed.

**Key facts:** structural remedies vs. consumer-welfare · strict vs. fault liability = incentive structures · surveillance = base-rate vs. prevented harm · automation = augmentation vs. "this time is different."

## §9 Framework: AI governance and existential risk

**Rule:** Risk-based regulation (the EU AI Act) tiers obligations by the risk a system poses; the safety/alignment school (Bostrom, Russell) argues advanced AI poses tail risks warranting caution, while accelerationists argue the risks are speculative and over-caution forfeits benefits.

**Worked example:** *Risk-based regulation* — **strongest:** proportionate, heavy where harm is serious and light where it isn't; **objection:** classifying risk is hard and can freeze fast-moving capability. *Safety/alignment* — frontier systems are dual-use and potentially catastrophic; *accelerationist objection* — the risks are speculative, and over-caution cedes the field to less careful actors. Whether frontier AI poses catastrophic/existential risk is genuinely contested; a debater asserting it as settled in *either* direction is over-reaching.

**Trap:** Asserting AI existential/catastrophic risk as settled in either direction — it is genuinely contested; penalise both "it will kill us all" and "the risk is zero."

**Key facts:** risk-based = EU AI Act, tiered · safety/alignment = Bostrom/Russell · accelerationist objection = speculative + forfeits benefits · existential risk contested.

## §10 The factual record

**Rule:** Technology has historically destroyed and created jobs (aggregate employment did not collapse over two centuries of mechanisation, but transitions imposed real, concentrated, sometimes generation-long costs); the Luddites were skilled workers protesting the *use* of machinery to undercut skilled wages, not mindless technophobes.

**Worked example:** Key established facts: the ATM/bank-teller case (Bessen) shows automating *tasks* need not eliminate *jobs* — as cash machines spread, banks opened more branches and tellers shifted to relationship roles. "This time is different" is a claim that must be *earned* with a specific disanalogy (breadth across cognitive tasks, speed), not asserted — and Frey-Osborne-type figures estimate *susceptibility*, not realised job loss. Content moderation is unavoidable and value-laden ("neutral non-moderation" is not available at scale). Algorithmic bias is real and mechanism-specific ("AI is objective" is false, and so is "AI is irredeemably biased"). Regulatory-philosophy divergence — rights-based/risk-tiered (EU), innovation-first (US), state-control/data-localization (China), and data-sovereignty models (India) — is now a fact of the landscape.

**Trap:** The Luddite caricature — using "Luddite" as a synonym for "irrational technophobe" is factually wrong; they protested wage-cutting *uses* of machinery.

**Key facts:** jobs destroyed *and* created, transitions costly · Luddites = skilled workers vs. wage-cutting · ATM/teller case (Bessen) · "this time is different" must be earned · moderation unavoidable · four regulatory philosophies.

## §11 Debate: hold AI developers strictly liable for harms caused by their models

**Rule:** This question turns on incentive effects — how each regime changes developer behaviour and who is best placed to prevent harm — and on distinguishing *foreseeable* from *emergent* harms.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case: strict liability forces developers to internalise the cost of harms from systems even they don't fully understand, creating powerful safety incentives, and victims shouldn't have to prove fault in an opaque system. The **con** case: it may chill beneficial innovation (especially for smaller developers), harm often runs through the deployer or user (so pinning it on the developer can be unfair and unattributable), and a fault-based standard with safe harbors better balances safety and progress. The strongest debaters assign liability to the party best placed to prevent each *kind* of harm.

**Trap:** Arguing abstract fairness instead of incentive effects; the sharp question is who is best placed to prevent the harm.

**Key facts:** crux = incentive effects + foreseeable vs. emergent · internalise harm vs. chill innovation/unattributable · assign to the best-placed preventer.

## §12 Debate: break up dominant technology platforms

**Rule:** This question turns on whether the debater *specifies the harm* and shows the *remedy fixes it* — with interoperability/portability as a middle path.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case: network effects and data moats create durable, self-reinforcing dominance conduct rules can't cure, so structural remedies restore competition that killed-in-the-cradle rivals never got and check power price metrics miss. The **con** case: scale delivers genuine consumer benefits (free, integrated services, R&D), breakups are hard to design and may reduce those benefits, and "bigness" alone isn't harm — target specific conduct (self-preferencing, killer acquisitions) or mandate interoperability. A pro that says only "too powerful" and a con that says only "consumers love it" both under-argue.

**Trap:** Asserting "too powerful" or "consumers love it" without specifying the harm and showing the remedy fixes it.

**Key facts:** crux = specify harm + remedy fixes it · network-effect dominance vs. benefits of scale · interoperability = middle path.

## §13 Debate: mass state surveillance justified if it demonstrably prevents terrorism

**Rule:** This question turns on weighing magnitude × probability of prevented harm against reversibility and systemic risk, and scrutinising whether *mass* (vs. targeted) surveillance actually delivers the prevention.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case: the magnitude of a prevented mass-casualty attack can outweigh the privacy cost, and targeted monitoring under oversight is a legitimate tool. The **con** case: the base-rate problem means *dragnet* surveillance produces overwhelming false positives while catching few real threats, and the chilling effect, function creep, and irreversibility of surveillance infrastructure are systemic, hard-to-undo harms the "if it prevents terrorism" framing understates. Reward whoever engages the base-rate problem rather than trading "safety" against "freedom" as slogans.

**Trap:** Trading "safety" against "freedom" as slogans instead of engaging the base-rate problem and the mass-vs-targeted distinction.

**Key facts:** crux = magnitude × probability vs. reversibility/systemic risk · base-rate problem · mass vs. targeted · scrutinise "demonstrably."

## §14 Debate: will automation harm workers in developing economies more than it helps

**Rule:** This question turns on the distribution and speed of transition and *whether the manufacturing job-absorption path still exists in an automated world* — not whether work vanishes forever.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **harm** case: automation erodes exactly the low-skill manufacturing jobs that lifted East Asia out of poverty, threatening "premature deindustrialisation" and closing the development ladder before poorer countries can climb it. The **help** case: automation raises productivity, lowers prices, and creates new sectors and tasks, and historically labour reallocates — developing economies can leapfrog into services. Penalise a help-side leaning on "reallocation is painless" and a harm-side treating displacement as permanent mass unemployment without a mechanism.

**Trap:** "Reallocation is painless" or "permanent mass unemployment" — both dodge the real question of distribution, speed, and whether the manufacturing ladder still exists.

**Key facts:** crux = distribution/speed + does the manufacturing ladder still exist · premature deindustrialisation vs. leapfrogging · connects to the development subject.

## §15 Debate: abolish intermediary-liability shields for online platforms

**Rule:** This question turns on the *moderator's dilemma* — liability pushes platforms toward either over-removal (collateral censorship) or ceasing to host user content — weighed against accountability for harm.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **abolish** case: immunity lets platforms profit from harmful content (defamation, harassment, incitement) with no accountability, and liability would force them to prevent foreseeable harms. The **keep** case: without a shield, platforms would over-remove lawful speech to avoid risk or stop hosting user content at all, so the shield is what makes an open participatory internet possible, and the harms are better targeted specifically. Reward whoever engages the moderator's dilemma rather than assuming platforms can costlessly filter perfectly.

**Trap:** Assuming platforms can costlessly filter perfectly, ignoring the collateral-censorship / over-removal dynamic.

**Key facts:** crux = moderator's dilemma vs. accountability · profit-from-harm vs. collateral censorship/open internet · no costless perfect filter.

## §16 Debate: a "right to be forgotten"

**Rule:** This question turns on weighing privacy/dignity and rehabilitation against free expression and the public record, and on *who decides* what is "no longer relevant."

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case: people should not be permanently defined by old, irrelevant, or unfairly damaging information, and dignity and the ability to move on require some control over one's digital past. The **con** case: it collides with free expression, the public's right to know, and the historical record, hands private actors and the state a tool to scrub inconvenient truths, and "relevance" is a slippery, censorious standard.

**Trap:** Ignoring *who decides* relevance and how the standard resists abuse — the crux is not the principle but the decision procedure.

**Key facts:** crux = privacy/dignity vs. free expression/record + who decides · move-on vs. scrub-inconvenient-truths · "relevance" is slippery.

## §17 Debate: mandatory safety regulation for the most advanced AI before deployment

**Rule:** This question turns on how to weigh speculative tail risk against concrete, near-term costs and benefits — and proposing a *proportionate* mechanism.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro** case: frontier systems are dual-use and potentially catastrophic, so risk-based pre-deployment rules (like those for drugs or aircraft) are proportionate, and voluntary self-regulation under competitive pressure is unreliable. The **con** case: the catastrophic risks are speculative and the technology moves faster than regulators, heavy-handed rules entrench incumbents and drive development to less-careful jurisdictions, so target *concrete* harms (bias, fraud, safety) instead. Penalise both "it will kill us all" and "the risk is zero."

**Trap:** Asserting the tail risk as certain or as zero, instead of calibrating to the actual state of evidence.

**Key facts:** crux = speculative tail risk vs. concrete costs/benefits · drug/aircraft analogy vs. incumbents/jurisdiction-shopping · propose a proportionate mechanism.

## §18 Debate: a few interoperable global platforms vs many national ecosystems (the splinternet)

**Rule:** This question turns on weighing openness, innovation, and free flow against sovereignty, security, and self-governance — noting who benefits from each order.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **global** case: a shared, interoperable internet maximises reach, innovation, and the free flow of information and resists authoritarian information control. The **sovereignty** case: national ecosystems let societies govern data, protect security and culture, and resist foreign platform dominance — and a single global order often means the dominance of one country's firms and values. Note who benefits from each (incumbent-home-country firms vs. states seeking control).

**Trap:** Ignoring who benefits from each order — "global" can mean one country's firms and values; "sovereignty" can mean state control.

**Key facts:** crux = openness/innovation vs. sovereignty/security · free flow vs. data governance · who benefits from each order.

## §19 The domain's most error-prone claims

**Rule:** The signature trap is the sweeping frame — techno-utopian ("innovation solves it") or techno-doom ("this will destroy society") — asserted without a specific mechanism or harmed party; the antidote is to demand *which* harm, to *whom*, through *what* mechanism, and at what innovation cost.

**Worked example:** Walk the recurring myths. *The lump-of-labour fallacy* ("automation will permanently destroy X million jobs") — aggregate employment is not fixed, *but* the mirror error ("painless reallocation") is also wrong. *The Luddite caricature* — they protested wage-cutting *uses* of machinery, not technology itself. *"This time is different"* — must be *earned* with a specific disanalogy, not asserted (and Frey-Osborne figures estimate susceptibility, not realised loss). *"Platforms are neutral / just pipes"* — moderation at scale is unavoidable and value-laden. *"AI is objective"* — systems encode and amplify bias (and "irredeemably biased" over-claims the other way). *"Big = bad / big = fine"* — neither substitutes for a specified harm mechanism. *"Nothing to hide, nothing to fear"* — ignores chilling effects and the base-rate problem. And beware the Jevons blind spot (efficiency can *increase* total use) and the productivity-paradox blind spot (effects lag by years).

**Trap:** Correlation on social harms — claims that a technology "causes" polarisation, mental-health harm, or democratic decline often outrun the settled evidence; the mechanisms are live research questions.

**Key facts:** sweeping frame = the signature trap · lump-of-labour + its mirror · Luddite caricature · "this time is different" must be earned · neutrality/objectivity are design achievements · big≠bad without a mechanism · Jevons + productivity-paradox blind spots.
