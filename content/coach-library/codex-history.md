# History's Turning Points

*Subject-knowledge coach codex. Authored under the subject-knowledge variant: real named content; every Rule is a defensible fact — a definition, attribution, mechanism, a school's stated position, a dated event, or the crux a contested question turns on; the normative verdict of a contested debate is taught in the Worked example (both sides shown), never keyed.*

## Scope

This subject is change over time and how we judge it: whether an event, invention, or transition was a genuine turning point, whether it did more good than harm, and what the world would plausibly look like without it. It covers the vocabulary of historical explanation, the competing schools of historiography, the factual record of the major turning-point candidates, the live debates, and the recurring myths. It deliberately excludes the verdict of those debates — the learner reconstructs the strongest case on each side and identifies what a question turns on, rather than memorising who wins.

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** define a term; attribute a school, work, or concept to its thinker; state the mechanism a school proposes; identify the standard objection to a school; identify what a turning-point debate *turns on* (causation, counterfactual, or net assessment); correct a common myth.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~35% recall (definitions, attributions, dates), ~45% application (state the mechanism, match a school to its claim, name the objection), ~20% multi-step (locate a claim on the agency/structure axis; disaggregate a turning point into cause vs. accelerant).
- **In scope:** definitions; each school's claim, mechanism, strongest case, and objection; the dated record and the mechanisms behind each episode; the recurring myths and corrections.
- **Out of scope:** the verdict of a contested debate. No quiz item keys "was the Industrial Revolution good" or "did contingency or structure decide it."
- **Classic traps:** single-cause history; convenient counterfactuals; treating an accelerant as a turning point; false precision on contested casualty/population figures; the Whig/teleological fallacy and its mirror, declinism.

## §1 Turning point, accelerant, symptom, marker

The domain's central classification — most "turning point" disputes are really about which of these an event was.

**Rule:** A turning point is a moment after which a society's trajectory measurably diverges from before; it requires a demonstrable before/after break, and is distinct from an accelerant (speeds an existing direction), a symptom (reflects change caused elsewhere), and a marker (a periodising label that may correspond to no real rupture).

**Worked example:** Take "the printing press changed the world." To classify it, ask whether the trajectory *diverged* after it (turning point), whether it merely *sped up* a rupture already underway (accelerant), whether it *reflected* deeper forces like rising urban literacy (symptom), or whether "1450" is just a convenient date historians use to bracket an era (marker). The same event can be argued into different boxes — which is exactly what a turning-point debate contests.

**Trap:** Treating an accelerant as a turning point — crediting an event with divergence when it only sped a trend already in motion. Always ask whether the event *caused* the divergence or *rode* it.

**Key facts:** turning point = direction changes · accelerant = speed changes · symptom = caused elsewhere · marker = labelling convenience · requires before/after break.

## §2 Causation: proximate vs ultimate, necessary vs sufficient, overdetermination

The causal toolkit that separates strong from weak turning-point claims.

**Rule:** A proximate cause is the immediate trigger and an ultimate cause is the deep condition; a cause can be necessary (without it, no outcome) without being sufficient (enough alone); overdetermination is when several independently sufficient causes are present, so the outcome would have occurred even without any one.

**Worked example:** "The assassination caused the First World War." Distinguish the spark (proximate) from the powder keg (ultimate: alliance blocs, arms races, mobilisation timetables). Then ask: was the assassination *necessary* (nothing else would have lit it), *sufficient* (enough on its own), or one of several sufficient triggers in an overdetermined system? If the system was primed to explode, the counterfactual "without the assassination, no war" is weak — overdetermination undercuts it.

**Trap:** Single-cause history — reducing a multi-causal, often overdetermined event to one lever. The standing rebuttal is "compared to what, and alongside what else?"

**Key facts:** proximate = spark, ultimate = powder keg · necessary ≠ sufficient · overdetermination undercuts single-cause counterfactuals · specify which is being claimed.

## §3 Counterfactual history

Disciplined "what if" reasoning — the single most decisive and most abused move in the domain.

**Rule:** A disciplined counterfactual changes exactly one variable and traces consequences through known mechanisms; a lazy one simply assumes the alternative would obviously be better or worse.

**Worked example:** To test whether the printing press was a genuine cause, ask what plausibly follows if it had not existed — holding everything else fixed and reasoning from real mechanisms (how ideas spread without cheap print), not wishful assertion. Niall Ferguson's *Virtual History* (1997) defends this as a test of causal claims; E.H. Carr dismissed it as a "parlour game" that invites wishful thinking. The quality of a debater's counterfactual is the best predictor of who wins a turning-point argument.

**Trap:** Convenient counterfactuals — "without X it would obviously have been better/worse." Demand the mechanism and the plausibility of the alternative.

**Key facts:** change one variable · trace through known mechanisms · Ferguson defends (*Virtual History*, 1997), Carr dismisses as a parlour game · discipline of the counterfactual usually decides the round.

## §4 Contingency vs determinism and the longue durée

The field's master axis: did accidents and choices drive history, or deep structural forces?

**Rule:** Contingency holds that outcomes hinge on accidents and choices that could easily have gone otherwise (Gould's "replay the tape"); determinism holds that structural forces (geography, demography, economics) would have produced a broadly similar result regardless.

**Worked example:** The same fact — "the printing press spread the Reformation" — supports opposite conclusions depending on where you place it on this axis: a contingency reading says the press *caused* a rupture that might not have happened; a determinist reading says structural forces had already made the rupture likely and the press merely *carried* it. Braudel and the Annales School push toward the *longue durée* — history understood over long spans of slow-moving structures (climate, geography, demography) rather than the "froth" of events.

**Trap:** Arguing a turning point without locating the claim on the agency/structure axis — the debater who cannot say whether they mean the cause was necessary, sufficient, or merely facilitating is arguing blind.

**Key facts:** contingency = accidents decide (Gould) · determinism = structure decides (Marx, Diamond) · longue durée = Braudel/Annales, slow structures · the axis the same fact can cut both ways on.

## §5 Judging the past: Whig history, presentism, and net assessment

The second, quieter tension — whether and how we may judge across time.

**Rule:** Whig history (Herbert Butterfield, 1931) is the fallacy of reading the past as an inevitable, righteous march to the present; presentism is judging past actors by present standards; net assessment weighs aggregate gains against distributional costs, for whom and over what timeframe.

**Worked example:** A "regret X" or "celebrate X" motion is a net-assessment question: good or bad *for whom*, over *what timeframe*? A change can be catastrophic for one generation and liberating for the next. The presentism debate has a defensible middle: some standards (e.g. against chattel slavery) were articulated and contested *in their own time*, so condemning slaveholders is not purely anachronistic. Path dependence (early events lock in costly-to-reverse trajectories; the QWERTY example) and historiography (causal "facts" are often current scholarly consensus) round out the toolkit.

**Trap:** The Whig/teleological fallacy ("this led straight to modern democracy") and its mirror, declinism ("a fall from a golden age") — both narrate a convenient arc rather than the multi-causal reality.

**Key facts:** Whig history = Butterfield, 1931 · teleology = history "aiming" at an endpoint · presentism vs. anachronism, with a defensible middle · net assessment = for whom, over what timeframe · historiography = consensus, not gospel.

## §6 School: Great Man / agency theory

**Rule:** Great Man theory (Thomas Carlyle, *On Heroes*, 1841) holds that individuals' decisions tip outcomes at genuine hinge moments; its defensible modern form is that structure under-determines timing and selection, so *someone* had to make the call.

**Worked example:** At a close-run war, a contested succession, or a strategic choice, different individuals demonstrably made different calls with different results — structure left the outcome open and agency executed it. **Strongest case:** structure under-determines *which* possibility occurred and *when*. **Standard objection:** it mistakes the visible actor for the underlying cause; "great men" often ride forces that would have thrown up a functional equivalent, and it flatters winners while writing out collective and anonymous action.

**Trap:** Crediting the visible individual with a cause that was structurally overdetermined — remove one actor and another fills the slot.

**Key facts:** Carlyle, 1841 · structure under-determines timing/selection · strongest: someone made the call · objection: replaceable actor, flatters winners.

## §7 School: structural / materialist (Marx)

**Rule:** Marxist theory holds that history is driven by the mode of production and the class relations it generates, with ideas, law, and leaders largely a *superstructure* rising from the economic *base*.

**Worked example:** Broad patterns — feudalism to capitalism, industrialisation, urbanisation, the demographic transition — recur across very different cultures, which the school reads as evidence of deep economic drivers rather than a thousand independent accidents. **Strongest case:** the cross-cultural recurrence of these transitions. **Standard objection:** structure explains the *range of the possible* but not *which* possibility occurred or *when*; it under-predicts contingency, and Marx's own forecasts of where revolution would come first were wrong.

**Trap:** Treating structure as explaining specific events; it bounds what is possible, it does not select which possibility happens.

**Key facts:** base determines superstructure · recurring cross-cultural transitions = strongest case · objection: under-predicts contingency, poor at specifics.

## §8 School: the Annales and the longue durée

**Rule:** The Annales School (Marc Bloch, Lucien Febvre, Fernand Braudel, *The Mediterranean*, 1949) shifts attention from kings and battles to the slow structures of geography, climate, trade routes, and mentalities.

**Worked example:** Where event-history narrates the "froth" of battles and reigns, the *longue durée* reveals how much of history is set by forces operating over centuries that no individual could move — the sea, the soil, the slow rhythms of trade. **Strongest case:** it exposes the deep constraints event-history misses. **Standard objection:** it can make change itself hard to explain — if everything important moves slowly, why do ruptures happen at all? — and it de-emphasises agency and events to a fault.

**Trap:** Using slow-structure explanation to dissolve genuine ruptures; the school struggles precisely with *why* sudden change occurs.

**Key facts:** Bloch, Febvre, Braudel (1949) · slow structures over events · *histoire événementielle* = the dismissed "froth" · objection: weak on explaining rupture.

## §9 School: geographic / environmental determinism (Diamond)

**Rule:** Geographic determinism (Jared Diamond, *Guns, Germs, and Steel*, 1997) traces the unequal fates of continents to biogeography — domesticable species, Eurasia's east–west axis easing diffusion, and disease immunities.

**Worked example:** The mechanism: some regions had more domesticable plants and animals; Eurasia's continental axis let crops and technology spread along shared latitudes; and long exposure to livestock diseases gave Eurasians immunities that conquest later exploited. **Strongest case:** it explains the very-long-run head start of Eurasia *without* recourse to racial or cultural superiority — its scientific and moral appeal. **Standard objection:** it is weak on the last five centuries (why Europe and not China, both in temperate Eurasia?) and critics say it flattens agency, institutions, and culture into a geographic backdrop.

**Trap:** Extending the theory from deep prehistory to recent divergence, where it is weakest — geography does not explain why Europe rather than China industrialised first.

**Key facts:** Diamond, 1997 · domesticable species + east–west axis + disease immunities · strongest: non-racial deep explanation · objection: weak on the last 500 years.

## §10 School: institutional

**Rule:** Institutional theory (Douglass North; Acemoglu, Johnson, Robinson, *Why Nations Fail*, 2012; 2024 Nobel) holds that the decisive variable is whether institutions are inclusive (broad property rights, checks on power) or extractive (elite capture).

**Worked example:** Natural experiments hold geography and culture roughly constant while institutions vary: divided Korea (one people, one peninsula, opposite institutions, divergent outcomes within two generations) and the "reversal of fortune," whereby regions rich at European contact became relatively poor where extractive institutions were built. **Strongest case:** these natural experiments. **Standard objection:** it can be circular (what made institutions inclusive in the first place?) and struggles with fast growth under manifestly non-inclusive institutions — above all modern China.

**Trap:** Treating "inclusive institutions" as complete; the China anomaly and the circularity problem both bite.

**Key facts:** North; Acemoglu–Johnson–Robinson, 2012, 2024 Nobel · inclusive vs. extractive · divided Korea = the natural experiment · anomaly = China.

## §11 School: cultural explanation (Weber, Landes)

**Rule:** Cultural explanation (Max Weber, *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, 1905; David Landes, *The Wealth and Poverty of Nations*, 1998) holds that values shape economic behaviour and can make the difference between similarly-placed societies.

**Worked example:** Weber argued a particular religious ethic disposed believers toward disciplined, accumulative economic behaviour; Landes generalised that culture "makes all the difference." **Strongest case:** it captures why materially and geographically similar societies behave differently. **Standard objection:** "culture" is slippery and often deployed circularly — success is credited to culture, then culture is *inferred from* success — and it shades easily into stereotype and just-so stories.

**Trap:** Circular culture explanations — inferring the culture from the outcome it is supposed to explain.

**Key facts:** Weber, 1905; Landes, 1998 · values shape economic behaviour · strongest: similar societies diverge · objection: circular, slides into stereotype.

## §12 School: contingency / chaos (Gould)

**Rule:** The contingency view (Stephen Jay Gould; much counterfactual history) holds that small accidents cascade through sensitive systems, so replaying the tape yields a different world.

**Worked example:** Many "inevitable" outcomes turned on genuine near-misses — weather at a battle, a survived or failed assassination, a single decision. **Strongest case:** these documented near-misses. **Standard objection:** pressed to the limit it makes history unintelligible and unfalsifiable — if everything is accident, nothing can be explained or learned.

**Trap:** Pushing contingency so far that history becomes pure accident; the extreme version explains nothing.

**Key facts:** Gould's "replay the tape" · accidents cascade · strongest: real near-misses · objection: extreme version is unfalsifiable.

## §13 School: world-systems and postcolonial/subaltern

**Rule:** World-systems theory (Immanuel Wallerstein) reads modern history as one expanding capitalist system dividing the globe into core, semi-periphery, and periphery; subaltern studies (Ranajit Guha) insists on recovering the agency of the colonised and the anonymous.

**Worked example:** World-systems foregrounds who paid for others' "progress" by tracing surplus from periphery to core; subaltern history corrects an elite, top-down, Eurocentric record by centring peasants and the written-out. **Strongest case:** it corrects Eurocentrism and foregrounds distributional cost. **Standard objection:** the strong world-systems thesis can be economically deterministic, and some postcolonial history is criticised as more interpretive than evidentiary.

**Trap:** Taking the strong economic-determinist version as the school's defensible core; its usable claim is corrective, not an iron law.

**Key facts:** Wallerstein = core/periphery · Guha = subaltern agency · strongest: corrects Eurocentric record · objection: strong version is deterministic.

## §14 The mature layered move

**Rule:** The strongest historical reasoning specifies which layer a claim operates on — structure sets the possibilities, contingency selects among them, agency executes the selection — and then weighs the layers for the specific case.

**Worked example:** "The Reformation was caused by the printing press" is unfinished until the debater says whether the press was *necessary* (structure could not have produced the rupture otherwise), *sufficient* (it would have happened regardless of Luther), or *facilitating* (it shaped the speed and form). Forcing that precision is often how the argument is won. Professionals rarely pick one school outright; they layer them.

**Trap:** Declaring wholesale for one school. The layered position weighs structure, contingency, and agency case by case rather than choosing a side.

**Key facts:** structure = possibilities · contingency = selection · agency = execution · necessary/sufficient/facilitating = the precision to force.

## §15 The Neolithic (Agricultural) Revolution (from ~10,000 BCE)

**Rule:** The Neolithic Revolution — the transition from foraging to settled farming, begun independently in several regions — enabled surplus, cities, writing, states, and hierarchy, yet on skeletal evidence often left early farmers shorter, sicker, and more overworked than the foragers they replaced.

**Worked example:** Agriculture is the strongest candidate for the deepest turning point because nearly everything we call "civilisation" follows from farming surplus. But the complicating record — narrowed nutrition, rising disease with density and proximity to animals, intensified labour — makes it the model case of "a turning point for the species, a catastrophe for the individual generation." Both halves are true simultaneously.

**Trap:** Assuming agriculture was an unambiguous individual improvement; the skeletal evidence often suggests the reverse for the first agricultural populations.

**Key facts:** from ~10,000 BCE, multiple independent origins · enabled surplus/cities/writing/states · early farmers often worse off individually · species-gain vs. individual-loss.

## §16 Writing (from ~3200 BCE)

**Rule:** Writing, invented independently in Mesopotamia, Egypt, China, and Mesoamerica, made law, bureaucracy, accumulated knowledge, contracts, and history itself possible — a precondition of most later turning points.

**Worked example:** Because writing underlies later inventions, crediting a *later* innovation with "creating the modern world" sometimes ignores that writing did the deeper enabling work. It is the substrate on which law, record-keeping, and cumulative knowledge stand.

**Trap:** Over-crediting later inventions while ignoring that writing was the deeper precondition.

**Key facts:** from ~3200 BCE · Mesopotamia (cuneiform), Egypt, China, Mesoamerica · enabled law, bureaucracy, cumulative knowledge · precondition of later ruptures.

## §17 The printing press

**Rule:** Movable type predates Gutenberg — Bi Sheng's ceramic type in Song China (~1040) and Korean metal type (the *Jikji*, 1377) — while Johannes Gutenberg's Mainz press (~1440–1450; the Gutenberg Bible ~1455) pioneered a *commercially effective European system* of metal type, press, and oil-based ink.

**Worked example:** The case for a turning point: an explosion in book production, the standardisation and fixing of texts (Elizabeth Eisenstein's "typographical fixity"), the viral spread of the Reformation in print (Luther's 95 Theses, 1517), and the cumulative build-up of the Scientific Revolution (Eisenstein, *The Printing Press as an Agent of Change*, 1979). The complicating record: print also mass-produced witch-hunting manuals (the *Malleus Maleficarum*, 1487) and propaganda, literacy rose slowly, and movable type existed in East Asia for four centuries *without* the same rupture — strong evidence that the social, religious, and linguistic *context* did much of the work "the press alone" is credited with. This is the domain's best case study against technological determinism.

**Trap:** "Gutenberg invented movable type" — he did not; China and Korea preceded him by centuries. Credit the commercially effective European *system*, not the invention of the concept.

**Key facts:** Bi Sheng ~1040, Korean metal type/*Jikji* 1377, Gutenberg ~1440–50 · Reformation 1517 · Eisenstein (typographical fixity, 1979) · East Asia had type without the rupture.

## §18 The Black Death (1347–1351)

**Rule:** The Black Death killed on the order of a third to a half of Europe's population in a few years — an estimate with huge regional variation — with arguable consequences including labour scarcity that raised surviving peasants' bargaining power and undermined serfdom in Western Europe.

**Worked example:** The demographic shock plausibly raised wages, shook faith in the Church, and on some accounts helped clear ground for later economic and social change. It is a powerful case for a *demographic* turning point — and a caution that turning points are not always products of human intention.

**Trap:** Stating the mortality figure as precise; it is a wide estimate with large regional variation.

**Key facts:** 1347–1351 · ~⅓ to ½ of Europe (estimate) · labour scarcity → peasant bargaining power, serfdom eroded · unintended demographic turning point.

## §19 The Columbian Exchange (post-1492)

**Rule:** The Columbian Exchange (a term coined by Alfred Crosby, 1972) transferred crops, animals, people, and pathogens between hemispheres, and Old World diseases devastated indigenous American populations with no acquired immunity.

**Worked example:** To the Old World came calorie-dense staples (potato, maize, tomato, cassava, chili, cacao) that later fed population booms; to the New World came wheat, sugar, rice, coffee, horses, cattle, pigs. Old World pathogens (smallpox, measles, influenza) caused a demographic collapse commonly estimated at a 70–90% fall across the Americas over the century-plus after contact — though the pre-contact baseline is itself uncertain. Potosí silver (from 1545) flowing to Europe and China linked the world's economies into the first genuinely global system.

**Trap:** Stating indigenous death tolls as settled — the 70–90% range is a set of scholarly estimates on an uncertain baseline; and "Columbus discovered an empty world" ignores tens of millions already present, with the catastrophe largely *unintended disease* rather than only conquest.

**Key facts:** Crosby, 1972 · potato/maize to Old World; horses/wheat to New · 70–90% decline (contested estimate) · Potosí silver from 1545 → first global economy.

## §20 The Atlantic slave trade (c. 1500–1866)

**Rule:** The Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database records roughly 12.5 million Africans embarked and roughly 10.7 million surviving the Middle Passage — among the domain's better-documented figures.

**Worked example:** The trade was foundational to plantation economies (sugar, cotton, tobacco) and Atlantic capital accumulation, a demographic and social catastrophe for West and Central Africa, and the root of enduring racial hierarchies. Whether — and how much — slavery *caused* the Industrial Revolution and Western wealth is a live question launched by Eric Williams's *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944); the honest position credits it as a major contributor without treating it as the sole cause.

**Trap:** "Slavery alone built Western wealth" — single-cause overreach on a genuinely contested question; credit it as a major contributor, weighed against other causes.

**Key facts:** ~12.5m embarked, ~10.7m survived the Middle Passage · plantation economies + capital accumulation · Williams thesis (1944) contested · major contributor, not sole cause.

## §21 The Scientific Revolution (c. 1543–1687)

**Rule:** The Scientific Revolution, conventionally bracketed by Copernicus's *De revolutionibus* (1543) and Newton's *Principia* (1687), established systematic experiment, the mathematisation of nature, and institutionalised skepticism.

**Worked example:** With Galileo, Kepler, and Bacon between the brackets, the period created the method that produced the modern world. The complication: it built on Islamic, ancient, and medieval foundations and was entangled with astrology and alchemy, so the tidy "revolution" is partly a later narrative.

**Trap:** Treating it as a clean, self-contained rupture; it built on prior foundations and the neat "revolution" framing is partly retrospective.

**Key facts:** Copernicus 1543 → Newton 1687 · experiment + mathematisation + skepticism · built on prior foundations · tidy "revolution" partly a later narrative.

## §22 The Industrial Revolution (c. 1760–1840, Britain first)

**Rule:** The Industrial Revolution — mechanised textiles, Watt's improved steam engine (1760s–80s), coal, iron, and railways — began sustained per-capita economic growth after millennia of near-flat output, the single most consequential economic break in human history.

**Worked example:** Britain's coincidence of coal, capital, colonial markets, high wages (incentivising labour-saving machinery), and institutions is the standard explanation; Pomeranz's *The Great Divergence* (2000) reframed it as a near-run thing between Britain and China's Yangzi Delta, tipped by coal location and New World resources. The standard-of-living debate is the template for every "regret a transition" motion: optimists cite rising real wages and consumption; pessimists cite stagnant or falling early real wages, brutal hours, child labour, and disease-ridden cities. The honest position: *long-run* gains are undeniable while *short-run* distributional costs were severe.

**Trap:** "The Industrial Revolution immediately raised living standards" — contested; the early decades' real-wage record is exactly what the optimist/pessimist debate is about.

**Key facts:** c. 1760–1840, Britain first · Watt's steam engine 1760s–80s · the growth "hockey stick" (Maddison series) · Pomeranz's near-run "Great Divergence" (2000) · long-run gain, severe short-run cost.

## §23 The Enlightenment (late 17th–18th century)

**Rule:** The Enlightenment advanced reason, empiricism, natural rights, toleration, separation of powers, and the idea of progress (Locke, Montesquieu, Voltaire, Hume, Kant — whose 1784 essay gave the motto *sapere aude*, "dare to know") — and its legacy is genuinely dual.

**Worked example:** The same era that generated rights discourse and later abolitionism also produced early "scientific" racial classification and intellectual cover for empire. A strong account holds both truths at once: the Enlightenment supplied the *vocabulary of universal rights* later used to attack slavery and colonialism *and* the pseudo-scientific hierarchies used to justify them.

**Trap:** "The Enlightenment was uniformly progressive" *or* "uniformly a tool of empire" — both false; the legacy is dual and the strongest account holds both halves.

**Key facts:** Locke, Montesquieu, Voltaire, Hume, Kant · *sapere aude* (Kant, 1784) · rights discourse *and* scientific racism · dual legacy.

## §24 The Age of Revolutions (1776–1848)

**Rule:** The American (1776), French (1789), and Haitian (1791–1804) revolutions and the 1848 European wave universalised the language of rights, popular sovereignty, and the nation-state.

**Worked example:** The Haitian Revolution — the only successful large-scale slave revolt to found a state — is the sharpest test of whether Enlightenment universalism meant what it said, and is often omitted from turning-point lists. The French Revolution is the classic contingency-vs-structure case: was the Terror inherent in the revolution or a contingent turn?

**Trap:** Omitting the Haitian Revolution from the story — it is the decisive test of Enlightenment universalism and a corrective to Eurocentric lists.

**Key facts:** American 1776, French 1789, Haitian 1791–1804, European 1848 · universalised rights/sovereignty/nation-state · Haiti = the universalism test · French Revolution = contingency-vs-structure case.

## §25 The twentieth century's ruptures

**Rule:** The century's turning-point candidates include the World Wars (1914–18, 1939–45), the Russian Revolution (1917), decolonisation (post-1945), Bretton Woods (1944) and the Marshall Plan (from 1948), and the digital/internet revolution.

**Worked example:** The World Wars collapsed the pre-1945 order; 1917 launched the century's central ideological contest; decolonisation (Indian/Pakistani independence and Partition, 1947, with mass displacement; the "Year of Africa," 1960, ~17 states independent) reshaped the map. The internet is the most recent candidate — too recent for confident net assessment, and a good account notes we may be *inside* the rupture rather than after it, which makes counterfactual and net-benefit claims especially uncertain.

**Trap:** Confident net assessment of the digital revolution — we may be inside the rupture, so its counterfactuals and net-benefit claims are especially uncertain.

**Key facts:** World Wars 1914–18/1939–45 · Russian Revolution 1917 · Partition 1947, Year of Africa 1960 · Bretton Woods 1944, Marshall Plan from 1948 · internet = too recent to assess.

## §26 Debate: the printing press vs political revolution for human freedom

**Rule:** This question turns on the causal chain — whether print was necessary, sufficient, or merely facilitating for the freedoms attributed to it.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **pro-press** case: print democratised knowledge, broke clerical and state information monopolies, enabled the Reformation and the public sphere, and made every later revolution possible by spreading ideas — freedom is downstream of literacy. The **pro-revolution** case: freedom was *won* by constitutions, abolition, suffrage, and revolutions through struggle; print also armed censors and propagandists, so credit belongs to the *movements* print carried, not the neutral amplifier. The crux: nail the causal role (necessary/sufficient/facilitating). The East-Asia-without-a-print-revolution fact is the sharpest weapon for the revolution side; "no Reformation without print" for the press side.

**Trap:** Asserting the machine's importance without specifying its causal role; a tool used for both liberation and repression cannot be simply credited with the net gain.

**Key facts:** crux = necessary/sufficient/facilitating · East Asia had type without rupture · print carried both Luther and witch-panic · freedom won by movements vs. downstream of literacy.

## §27 Debate: regret the Industrial Revolution

**Rule:** This question turns on the counterfactual (what is the alternative?) and on timeframe weighting (short-run suffering vs. long-run gain, present ecological cost vs. two centuries of welfare gains).

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **regret** case: it inaugurated anthropogenic climate change, mass pollution, brutal early-industrial and child labour, with immense first-century human cost and an ecological bill now due. The **no-regret** case: it ended the Malthusian trap, multiplied life expectancy, and lifted humanity off a subsistence ceiling it had never escaped — and "regret" implies a *preferable* counterfactual, which permanent pre-industrial stagnation is not. The strongest arguments concede the opposing magnitude and win on weighing.

**Trap:** Pretending the other side's harms or gains do not exist. Both the human/ecological cost and the escape-from-poverty gain are real; the debate is weighing.

**Key facts:** crux = counterfactual + timeframe weighting · Malthusian escape vs. ecological/human cost · "regret implies a preferable alternative" · concede magnitude, win on weighing.

## §28 Debate: did decolonisation, on balance, harm the societies it liberated

**Rule:** This deliberately uncomfortable question turns on the counterfactual and the baseline — harmed *compared to what*: continued colonial rule, a slower transition, or an idealised clean break?

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **harmed** case: rushed departures left arbitrary borders, weak institutions, and unprepared elites, with some states seeing civil war, kleptocracy, and worse living standards for a period — so the *manner and speed*, not the end of colonialism itself, produced avoidable harm. The **not-harmed** case: self-determination is a good in itself, not reducible to GDP; colonial rule was itself extractive and the *cause* of the weak institutions blamed on independence, so measuring "harm" against a colonial baseline smuggles in that continued subjugation was the fair comparison. Penalise any argument that treats colonialism as a benign baseline.

**Trap:** Treating colonialism as a benign baseline for the comparison — that smuggles in the assumption the debate is supposed to test.

**Key facts:** crux = counterfactual + baseline · manner/speed vs. the end itself · dignity/self-rule vs. material outcomes · colonialism is not a benign baseline.

## §29 Debate: contingency vs structural forces

**Rule:** This question turns on layering — structure bounds the possible, contingency selects within it, agency executes — weighed for the specific case rather than declared wholesale.

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **contingency** case: key hinges turned on accidents (weather, a survived assassination, a lost battle) no structural account predicts. The **structure** case: zoom out and the same broad outcomes recur across cultures; accidents change the *who* and *when*, not the *what*, and overdetermination means the system was primed regardless. The sophisticated resolution is layered. The First World War (overdetermined, or did the assassination genuinely tip it?) is the ideal battleground.

**Trap:** Declaring wholesale for one; the strong move weighs the layers for concrete cases.

**Key facts:** crux = weighing structure/contingency/agency per case · accidents change who/when, not what · overdetermination · WWI = the battleground example.

## §30 Debate: rather have been born a forager than an early farmer

**Rule:** This vivid framing of the Neolithic net-assessment question turns on individual-vs-collective and present-vs-future weighing, plus the framing trick in "would you rather."

**Worked example (both sides, taught, not keyed):** The **forager** case: foragers on the archaeological evidence were often healthier, taller, less overworked, and more egalitarian; farming brought disease, drudgery, hierarchy, and famine vulnerability. The **farmer** case: farming's surplus produced everything the question-asker values — security, culture, medicine, longevity for descendants — so the *species* trade-off was transformative even if the individual one was bad, and "would you rather" smuggles in modern preferences.

**Trap:** Missing the framing trick — "would you rather" imports present preferences into a past choice, and the individual and species trade-offs point opposite ways.

**Key facts:** crux = individual-vs-collective + present-vs-future · skeletal evidence favours foragers individually · species-gain from surplus · the "would you rather" framing smuggles in modern values.

## §31 The domain's most error-prone claims

**Rule:** In this domain the master error is single-cause history — reducing multi-causal, often overdetermined events to one lever — and its companion, the convenient counterfactual asserted without mechanism.

**Worked example:** Walk the recurring myths. *"Gutenberg invented movable type"* — no; credit the commercially effective European system. *"Columbus discovered an empty world"* — tens of millions were present; the catastrophe was largely unintended disease. *"Precise indigenous death tolls are settled"* — they are estimate ranges (70–90%) on an uncertain baseline. *"The Industrial Revolution immediately raised living standards"* — contested; that is the optimist/pessimist debate. *"Early farmers were better off than foragers"* — the skeletal evidence often says the reverse. *"The Enlightenment was uniformly progressive / uniformly imperial"* — its legacy is dual. The Whig/teleological fallacy narrates an inevitable righteous march; declinism narrates a fall from a golden age; both are traps. And beware reifying periodisation ("the Middle Ages ended in 1492") as a fact about the world rather than a labelling convenience.

**Trap:** False precision on contested casualty/population figures, and dodging a *defensible* moral judgment by crying "presentism" — the test is whether the standard was available and contested in the period.

**Key facts:** single-cause history = master error · Gutenberg/Columbus/death-toll/living-standards/forager myths · Whig fallacy + declinism · periodisation is a convention · counterfactuals judged on mechanism, not confidence · historiography = consensus, not gospel.
