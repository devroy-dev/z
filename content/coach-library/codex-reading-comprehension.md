# Reading Comprehension

*Coach house skill codex. Authored to the v2 standard for skill codices: substance not voice; globally and culturally neutral (self-contained passages on neutral universal topics — science, general essays — with no country-specific or culturally-loaded content); every rule and every worked example has exactly one defensible, text-supported answer; § is the citation unit; section order is the study plan.*

## Scope

This skill is understanding a written passage: identifying its main idea and purpose, its structure, its explicit details, what it implies, its tone, its argument, and the meaning of words in context. It covers the reading and question-attack methods that apply to any passage. It deliberately excludes outside subject knowledge and personal opinion — every answer must be grounded in the passage itself — and excludes the grammar of sentence construction (that is English Grammar & Usage).

## How this subject is tested

- **Question formats:** a passage followed by questions on main idea, purpose, structure, explicit detail, inference, tone, argument, and vocabulary-in-context.
- **Difficulty mix:** ~30% recall/recognition (explicit detail, stated main idea), ~50% application (inference, tone, structure), ~20% multi-step (argument analysis, combining details across the passage).
- **In scope:** main idea and purpose; passage structure; explicit detail retrieval; inference from the text; author's tone; argument and rhetoric within a passage; vocabulary in context; reading strategy.
- **Out of scope:** outside knowledge or opinion beyond the passage; the factual accuracy of the passage's claims; sentence-level grammar (English Grammar & Usage).
- **Classic traps:** choosing a true detail as the "main idea"; inferring more than the text supports; picking a dictionary sense of a word that does not fit the context; importing outside knowledge the passage does not state.

## §1 Main idea and purpose

The main idea is the single point the whole passage is built to convey; supporting details serve it but are not it.

**Rule:** The main idea is the central claim the whole passage supports; a supporting detail or example illustrates that claim but is narrower than it.

**Worked example:** Passage: *"Many metals conduct heat well because their loosely held electrons move freely, carrying energy quickly through the material. Non-metals lack such mobile electrons, so heat passes through them slowly. This is why a metal spoon in hot water warms along its length faster than a wooden one."* Method: ask what single point every sentence serves. The first two sentences state a contrast (metals conduct heat faster than non-metals, because of mobile electrons); the spoon sentence is an *example* of that point. So the main idea is the general contrast, not the spoon. Answer: the main idea is **"metals conduct heat faster than non-metals because they have mobile electrons."**

**Trap:** Selecting the vivid example (the spoon) as the main idea — an illustration supports the main idea but is narrower than it.

**Key facts:** main idea = the central claim the whole passage supports · examples illustrate but are narrower · test each sentence: does it serve this point? · the broadest supported claim, not the most memorable detail.

## §2 Passage structure and organisation

Passages follow recognisable shapes, and naming the shape helps you locate any part quickly.

**Rule:** A passage's structure is the sequence of functions its parts perform — for example, stating a common belief, presenting evidence against it, then giving a revised conclusion.

**Worked example:** Passage: *"It is widely assumed that heavier objects fall faster than lighter ones. Yet careful measurement shows that, ignoring air resistance, objects of different weights fall at the same rate. The old assumption, it turns out, mistook the effect of air resistance for a difference in the falling itself."* Method: label each sentence's function — sentence 1 states a common belief, sentence 2 presents evidence against it, sentence 3 gives the corrected explanation. Answer: the structure is **"a common belief, then evidence against it, then a revised conclusion."**

**Trap:** Describing the topic ("this passage is about falling objects") instead of the *organisation* — a structure answer names how the parts function and follow one another.

**Key facts:** structure = the sequence of functions of the parts · common shapes: belief → counter-evidence → revision; claim → support → implication · label each part's job · structure is *how* it is organised, not *what* it is about.

## §3 Explicit detail retrieval

A detail question is answered by what the passage actually states, located precisely, not by memory or inference.

**Rule:** An explicit-detail answer is stated directly in the passage; locate the exact sentence and match it, without adding or generalising.

**Worked example:** Passage: *"The river runs high from the spring melt through early summer, then falls steadily as the dry months arrive, reaching its lowest point in late autumn before the winter rains begin."* Question: when is the river at its lowest? Method: scan for the stated fact about the lowest point — "reaching its lowest point in late autumn." Answer: **late autumn.** (The passage states it directly; no inference is needed.)

**Trap:** Answering from a general impression ("in the dry season") instead of the exact stated detail ("late autumn") — a detail question is keyed to the precise wording.

**Key facts:** the answer is stated in the text · locate the exact sentence · match the wording, do not generalise · detail questions need retrieval, not inference.

## §4 Inference from the text

An inference is what the passage necessarily implies — supported by the text, but not spelled out.

**Rule:** A valid inference follows necessarily from what the passage states; it must be supported by the text and go no further than the text allows.

**Worked example:** Passage: *"The bridge was closed for repairs, so commuters took the longer river route until it reopened."* Question: what can be inferred about the commuters' normal route? Method: the passage says they took the longer river route *because* the bridge was closed and only *until it reopened* — implying that when the bridge is open, they normally use it (the shorter route). This is supported without being stated outright. Answer: the inference is **"commuters normally use the bridge, which is the shorter route."**

**Trap:** Inferring beyond the text (e.g. "the commuters were angry about the closure") — an inference must be supported by the passage, and feelings not indicated cannot be inferred.

**Key facts:** inference = necessarily implied by the text · supported but not stated outright · go no further than the text allows · reject options the passage does not support.

## §5 Author's tone and attitude

Tone is read from the author's word choices, not from the topic itself.

**Rule:** The author's tone is the attitude conveyed by word choice; identify it from evaluative language, not from how you feel about the subject.

**Worked example:** Passage: *"The proposal is promising, though it rests on assumptions that have not yet been tested; if those hold, it could prove genuinely useful."* Method: weigh the evaluative words — "promising" and "genuinely useful" are positive, but "though," "not yet been tested," and the conditional "if those hold" add reservation. The blend is favourable but guarded. Answer: the tone is **cautiously optimistic.**

**Trap:** Choosing an extreme tone ("enthusiastic" or "dismissive") when the language is measured — tone must match the actual strength of the wording, and hedges ("though," "if") signal caution.

**Key facts:** tone = attitude shown by word choice · read the evaluative words and hedges · "promising … not yet tested … if those hold" = cautiously optimistic · match the strength of the language, avoid extremes.

## §6 Argument and rhetoric within a passage

When a passage argues, identify its conclusion and how its sentences function to support it.

**Rule:** In an argumentative passage, the conclusion is the claim being urged, and each other sentence functions as support, an example, or a concession to the other side.

**Worked example:** Passage: *"Regular walking has clear benefits. Admittedly, it is slower than more intense exercise. But it can be sustained for years with little risk of injury, which is why it suits people seeking a lasting habit."* Method: find the claim urged — "regular walking … suits people seeking a lasting habit" (the conclusion). The sentence "admittedly, it is slower" is a *concession* to the opposing view, not the author's main point; "it can be sustained … with little risk" is support. Answer: the conclusion is **"walking suits people seeking a lasting habit,"** and "admittedly, it is slower" functions as **a concession.**

**Trap:** Reading the concession ("admittedly, it is slower") as the author's own position — a concession acknowledges the other side before the author argues past it.

**Key facts:** conclusion = the claim urged · sentences function as support, example, or concession · "admittedly…" marks a concession · the author's view is what the concession is overridden *by*.

## §7 Vocabulary in context

The meaning of a word in a passage is fixed by its context, which may not be its most common dictionary sense.

**Rule:** A word's meaning in context is the sense that fits the surrounding sentence; choose it from context, not from the word's most familiar general meaning.

**Worked example:** Passage: *"The findings were novel, unlike anything reported in earlier studies."* Question: what does "novel" mean here? Method: use the context — "unlike anything reported before" signals newness and originality, so "novel" means *new or original*, not its unrelated sense of "a work of fiction." Answer: here "novel" means **new / original.**

**Trap:** Picking a common dictionary sense that does not fit ("novel" as a book) — the context clause ("unlike anything reported before") fixes the intended meaning.

**Key facts:** meaning is fixed by context · use the surrounding clue words · "novel … unlike anything before" = new/original · reject dictionary senses that do not fit the sentence.

## §8 Reading strategy

An efficient approach reads the questions first, maps the passage by the function of its parts, then locates each answer.

**Rule:** Read the question stems first, map each paragraph or sentence by its function, then use that map to locate the answer to each question rather than rereading the whole passage.

**Worked example:** Passage: *"[1] A common view holds that reading speed always comes at the cost of understanding. [2] But studies of skilled readers show they read quickly and comprehend well, by predicting and grouping words. [3] So the speed–understanding trade-off is not fixed; training can improve both together."* Method: map the three sentences by function — [1] common view, [2] contrary evidence, [3] conclusion. For a detail question about *skilled readers*, go straight to sentence [2] rather than rereading; for a main-idea question, use sentence [3]. Answer: mapping the passage as *belief → evidence → conclusion* lets you locate that skilled readers "read quickly and comprehend well" in sentence **[2]** directly.

**Trap:** Rereading the whole passage for every question instead of mapping its parts once — the map (which sentence does which job) lets you jump to the relevant place.

**Key facts:** read the question stems first · map each part by its function · jump to the mapped location for each question · a function-map (belief → evidence → conclusion) replaces rereading.
