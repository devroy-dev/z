# KNOWLEDGE CODEX 08 — Technology & Society Governance

*A deep reference briefing on how societies should govern powerful technologies: the vocabulary, the competing regulatory, economic, and philosophical frameworks in full, the factual and historical record, the live debates with arguments and rebuttals per side, and the myths and traps that recur as fact. Focus is governance and social effects; deep technical detail is out of scope — reason from mechanisms and evidence. Built so an adjudicator can verify a claim and a debater can prepare either side.*

---

## 1. Scope and the core tension

This domain asks **who should control powerful technologies, who bears the cost when they cause harm, and how to regulate fast-moving innovation without freezing its benefits.** It spans artificial intelligence, data and surveillance, platform market power, automation and labor, digital speech and moderation, and the contest between an open global internet and national digital sovereignty.

The deep tension is between **innovation and precaution**: regulate too early and you may freeze benefits and entrench incumbents; regulate too late and harms are locked in and hard to reverse. A second tension is **efficiency vs. power**: technologies — especially platforms — can deliver enormous consumer benefits *and* concentrate dangerous private power at the same time. A third is **liberty vs. security** in the surveillance context, and a fourth is **who is responsible** when harm runs through complex, opaque systems that no single actor fully controls.

The signature adjudication trap is the sweeping frame — techno-utopian ("innovation solves it") or techno-doom ("this will destroy society") — asserted without a specific mechanism or a specific harmed party. Reward the debater who demands *which* harm, to *whom*, through *what* mechanism, and what the *innovation trade-off* is. A second recurring trap is treating technology as an autonomous force that "does" things to society, when the deeper question is usually *who designed it, for whom, and under what incentives.*

## 2. Core concepts and vocabulary

- **The Collingridge dilemma.** David Collingridge (*The Social Control of Technology*, 1980): early in a technology's life it is easy to shape but its effects are unknown; by the time the effects are clear, the technology is entrenched and hard to change. The master problem of tech governance.
- **Precautionary principle.** Where an activity risks serious or irreversible harm, act to prevent it even under scientific uncertainty; the burden falls on the innovator to show safety. Contrast **permissionless innovation** (Adam Thierer): innovation should be presumptively allowed and harms addressed as they concretely arise, because ex-ante restriction stifles benefit and entrenches incumbents.
- **Externality and internality.** An **externality** is a cost (or benefit) imposed on third parties not reflected in a transaction (pollution; platform harms to non-users). An **internality** is a cost a person imposes on their own future self (addiction-by-design). Regulation often aims to make actors *internalize* externalities.
- **Network effects.** A product becomes more valuable as more people use it (Metcalfe's rough intuition), driving "winner-take-most" dynamics. **Direct** network effects (more users, more value to each) and **indirect** effects (a large user base attracts complementors — apps, sellers).
- **Two-sided / multi-sided markets.** Platforms serving distinct groups (users and advertisers; riders and drivers; buyers and sellers) where pricing on one side subsidizes the other, so competition and power work differently than in simple markets.
- **Data as a moat / barrier to entry.** Accumulated user data can entrench dominance, improve products in ways rivals can't match, and raise switching costs; combined with network effects it produces durable, self-reinforcing scale.
- **Lock-in and switching costs.** Once users' data, contacts, and habits live in a platform, leaving is costly — which blunts competition even absent formal monopoly.
- **Antitrust standards.** The **consumer-welfare standard** (Robert Bork, *The Antitrust Paradox*, 1978): conduct is anticompetitive only if it harms consumers, typically via higher prices or reduced output/quality. The **neo-Brandeisian / structuralist** view (Lina Khan, "Amazon's Antitrust Paradox," 2017; Tim Wu, *The Curse of Bigness*, 2018): concentration of power is itself a harm — to competition, workers, suppliers, innovation, and democracy — even absent price rises; the price-centric standard is a poor fit when services are free to users.
- **Self-preferencing, killer acquisitions, essential facilities.** Ways a dominant platform can entrench itself: favoring its own products in its marketplace; buying nascent rivals before they grow; controlling infrastructure others need. **Interoperability** and **data portability** are the standard structural remedies short of breakup.
- **Liability regimes.** **Strict liability** (liable for harm regardless of fault — forces internalization and prevention) vs. **negligence/fault-based** (liable only for failing a standard of care). **Intermediary liability**: whether a platform is responsible for user content, and **safe harbors** (immunity if it meets conditions, e.g. notice-and-takedown) that shape the whole shape of the internet.
- **Privacy conceptions.** Privacy as **control** over one's information (Alan Westin, *Privacy and Freedom*, 1967); privacy as **contextual integrity** — information flows should match the norms of the context in which data was shared (Helen Nissenbaum); privacy as **dignity** vs. privacy as **property**; and **surveillance capitalism** (Shoshana Zuboff, 2019) — the business model of extracting behavioral data to predict and shape behavior.
- **The chilling effect and the panopticon.** Surveillance deters lawful behavior (speech, association, dissent) even without direct coercion; Bentham's panopticon, via Foucault (*Discipline and Punish*, 1975), is the image of self-policing under the *possibility* of being watched. **Function creep** is the tendency of surveillance built for one purpose to expand to others.
- **The base-rate problem.** For rare threats (terrorism) in mass data, even a very accurate detector produces overwhelmingly false positives — the statistical Achilles' heel of dragnet surveillance.
- **Automation vocabulary.** **Technological unemployment** (Keynes coined the term, 1930); the **lump-of-labor fallacy** (the false belief that the amount of work is fixed); the **task framework** (David Autor: technology automates *tasks*, not whole *jobs*, and can *complement* the rest); **routine-biased / skill-biased technical change** and **job polarization** ("hollowing out" the middle); **Moravec's paradox** (tasks easy for humans are hard for machines and vice versa); the **productivity paradox** (Robert Solow, 1987: "you can see the computer age everywhere but in the productivity statistics").
- **Digital sovereignty / "splinternet."** States asserting control over data, infrastructure, and platforms within their borders (data localization, content control), fragmenting the once-global internet into national or bloc ecosystems.
- **Dual-use and the alignment/safety frame.** Many technologies serve benign and harmful ends (dual-use); AI-safety discourse adds the **alignment problem** (ensuring advanced systems pursue intended goals — Stuart Russell, *Human Compatible*, 2019) and **existential risk** (Nick Bostrom, *Superintelligence*, 2014), a contested but influential lens.
- **Jevons paradox.** Efficiency gains can *increase* total use of a resource (William Stanley Jevons, 1865) — a caution against assuming a more efficient technology reduces its footprint.

## 3. Competing frameworks (in full)

**A. Permissionless innovation vs. the precautionary principle.**
- *Permissionless (Thierer).* **Claim:** default to allowing innovation; address harms as concrete evidence emerges. **Strongest case:** most technologies' harms are over-predicted and their benefits under-imagined; ex-ante restriction entrenches incumbents (who can afford compliance) and forfeits gains. **Objection:** for technologies with catastrophic or irreversible potential, "wait and see" may be too late (the Collingridge trap).
- *Precautionary.* **Claim:** shift the burden onto innovators to demonstrate safety before deployment where serious harm is plausible. **Strongest case:** some harms — systemic, irreversible, catastrophic — cannot be undone once realized. **Objection:** taken strictly it can paralyze beneficial innovation and is *itself* risky, because the harms of *not* innovating (foregone medicines, safety, growth) are real too.

**B. Theories of technology and society.** A layer debaters often miss, and the antidote to lazy determinism.
- *Technological determinism.* Technology follows its own logic and *drives* social change; society adapts. **Strongest case:** some technologies do reshape social life broadly and predictably. **Objection:** it erases the human choices — design, funding, regulation, adoption — that shape how a technology is used.
- *Social construction of technology (SCOT)* (Trevor Pinch and Wiebe Bijker, 1980s). **Claim:** technologies are shaped by social groups and choices, not by an inexorable internal logic; the same artifact can develop differently under different social pressures. **Strongest case:** explains why "the same" technology has wildly different effects across societies.
- *Politics-of-artifacts* (Langdon Winner, "Do Artifacts Have Politics?", 1980). **Claim:** technologies embed political arrangements (some designs concentrate power, others distribute it), so governance must attend to design, not just use.
- *Critical / substantivist traditions* — Lewis Mumford ("authoritarian vs. democratic technics," 1964), Jacques Ellul (the autonomous logic of *technique*), Neil Postman (*Technopoly*, 1992). **Claim:** technological systems can subordinate human ends to their own imperatives. **Objection:** can slide into fatalism and nostalgia.

**C. Platform market power: conduct rules vs. structural remedies.**
- *Structuralist / neo-Brandeisian.* **Claim:** network effects and data moats produce durable dominance that conduct rules can't cure; structural remedies (breakups, interoperability mandates, data portability) restore competition. **Strongest case:** dominant platforms can self-preference, make killer acquisitions, and wield power over speech, commerce, and information that pricing metrics miss. **Objection:** scale delivers real consumer benefits (free services, integration, R&D); breakups are hard to design and may reduce those benefits.
- *Consumer-welfare / light-touch (Chicago School).* **Claim:** intervene only on demonstrable consumer harm; "big" is not "bad." **Strongest case:** avoids punishing success and protects the benefits of scale. **Objection:** the price-centric standard is a poor fit when products are free and harms show up as privacy erosion, reduced innovation, or concentrated power rather than higher prices.

**D. Liability for technological harm (especially AI).**
- *Strict liability.* **Claim:** hold developers/deployers liable regardless of fault. **Strongest case:** internalizes harm and forces safety investment where systems are complex and opaque. **Objection:** may chill innovation, especially for smaller developers, and is hard to apply when causation runs through many hands (developer, deployer, user) and emergent behavior.
- *Fault-based / safe-harbor.* **Claim:** liability only for failing a standard of care; provide safe harbors to enable innovation. **Objection:** under-deters where the standard of care is unclear or the harm is diffuse.
- *Ex ante regulation vs. ex post liability.* A cross-cutting choice: prevent harm through up-front rules (licensing, standards) or address it after the fact through courts and liability. Each has a characteristic failure (over-caution and capture; under-deterrence and unattributable harm).

**E. Surveillance: security vs. liberty.**
- *Pro-surveillance.* **Claim:** monitoring can prevent rare but catastrophic harms (terrorism, mass violence) and solve serious crime. **Strongest case:** the prevented harm can be enormous. **Objection:** the base-rate problem (rare threats + mass data = many false positives), the chilling effect on lawful dissent, and the abuse/irreversibility risk (surveillance infrastructure, once built, is available to every future government) — plus function creep.
- *Anti-surveillance.* **Claim:** mass surveillance is a systemic, hard-to-reverse threat to freedom that rarely delivers the security promised. **Objection:** targeted, warranted surveillance under oversight can be both effective and rights-respecting; blanket opposition forfeits real security tools.

**F. Automation and labor.**
- *Optimist (augmentation/reallocation).* **Claim:** technology has historically destroyed some jobs and created more; workers reallocate to new tasks and sectors; productivity gains raise living standards. **Strongest case:** two centuries of mechanization did not produce mass permanent unemployment, and automation of *tasks* often *raises* demand for the complementary human work (the ATM/teller case). **Objection:** the *transition* is painful and concentrated, and "this time" *could* differ if machines substitute for a very broad range of human tasks at once.
- *Pessimist ("this time is different").* **Claim:** AI's breadth threatens cognitive as well as manual tasks, potentially outpacing job creation and hollowing the middle (Brynjolfsson & McAfee, *The Second Machine Age*, 2014). **Strongest case:** the pace and breadth of substitution may exceed historical precedent. **Objection:** the claim must be *earned* with a specific disanalogy to past waves; the lump-of-labor fallacy lurks. The honest debate is about **distribution and speed of transition**, not whether work vanishes forever.

**G. AI governance and existential risk.**
- *Risk-based regulation* (the EU AI Act is the leading exemplar): tier obligations by the risk a system poses, banning some uses, tightly regulating "high-risk" ones, and lightly touching the rest. **Strongest case:** proportionate — heavy where harm is serious, light where it isn't. **Objection:** classifying risk is hard and can freeze fast-moving capability.
- *The safety/alignment school* (Bostrom, Russell): advanced AI poses tail risks that warrant caution and, some argue, coordination to slow development. **Objection (from accelerationists):** the risks are speculative, and over-caution forfeits enormous benefits and cedes the field to less careful actors. **Adjudication note:** whether frontier AI poses catastrophic/existential risk is genuinely contested; a debater asserting it as settled (in *either* direction) is over-reaching.

**The professional move:** reject the sweeping frame; identify the **specific mechanism** (network effect, externality, chilling effect, task displacement, self-preferencing); name the **specific harmed party**; and weigh it explicitly against the **innovation cost** of intervening — while attending to *design and incentives*, not treating the technology as an autonomous force.

## 4. The factual and historical record

*(Well-established patterns and near-consensus points; specific products, companies, and figures move fast, so reason from mechanisms and avoid asserting current specifics as settled.)*

- **Technology has historically destroyed and created jobs.** Across two centuries of mechanization, aggregate employment did not collapse; work shifted in kind. But transitions imposed real, concentrated, sometimes generation-long costs on displaced workers and communities. "It worked out on average" and "it was brutal for the losers" are *both* true.
- **The Luddites are misremembered.** The Luddites (English textile workers, roughly 1811–1816) were not mindless technophobes; they were skilled workers protesting the *use of machinery to undercut skilled wages and bypass labor protections.* "Luddite = anti-technology idiot" is a later distortion, and a debater who leans on the caricature is vulnerable.
- **The ATM/bank-teller case** (analyzed by James Bessen) is the canonical illustration that automating *tasks* need not eliminate *jobs*: as cash machines spread, the number of bank tellers did not collapse — banks opened more branches and tellers shifted toward relationship and sales roles. The lesson: automation of a task can raise demand for the human complement.
- **The productivity paradox.** Solow's 1987 quip — computers everywhere except in the productivity statistics — flags that new general-purpose technologies often take decades to show up in measured productivity, because complementary changes (skills, business reorganization) lag. A caution against both instant-utopia and instant-doom timelines.
- **"This time is different" is a claim that must be earned.** Whether AI differs from prior automation waves is genuinely open; the credible version specifies *why* — breadth across cognitive tasks, speed of adoption — rather than asserting it. (Frey and Osborne's widely cited estimate that a large share of jobs were "at risk" of automation is exactly that — an estimate of *susceptibility*, heavily contested and often misreported as a prediction that those jobs *will* vanish.)
- **Platform dominance rests on real mechanisms** — network effects, data advantages, switching costs, and integration — which is why "just let competitors enter" understates the barrier, and why "break them up" must reckon with the consumer benefits of scale.
- **Content moderation is unavoidable and value-laden.** Any platform hosting user content at scale must make moderation and ranking choices; "neutral non-moderation" is not actually available — unmoderated spaces fill with spam, fraud, abuse, and illegal content. The real debate is *who decides, by what rules, and with what accountability*, not whether to moderate. Intermediary-liability shields (safe harbors) shape how platforms make these choices.
- **Surveillance's effectiveness is contested.** Mass data collection faces the base-rate problem; targeted, well-oversighted surveillance has a stronger case than dragnet approaches. Chilling effects on lawful behavior are documented. Encryption debates ("going dark" vs. exceptional access) recur, with security experts broadly warning that mandated backdoors weaken security for everyone.
- **Algorithmic bias is real and mechanism-specific.** Systems trained on historical data can reproduce and amplify existing bias; the fixes (auditing, fairness constraints, transparency) trade off against each other and against accuracy — so "AI is objective/neutral" is false, and so is "AI is inevitably and irredeemably biased."
- **Regulatory-philosophy divergence is now a fact of the landscape:** a rights-based, risk-tiered approach (the EU, e.g. the AI Act and GDPR's data-protection and "right to erasure"); an innovation-first, lighter-touch approach (associated with the US); a state-control/data-localization approach (associated with China); and distinct models elsewhere (including India's emphasis on data sovereignty and digital public infrastructure). This divergence is the backdrop for "global rules vs. splinternet" debates.

## 5. The central debates — arguments and rebuttals per side

**Debate 1: "This House would hold AI developers strictly liable for harms caused by their models."**
- *Pro:* Strict liability forces developers to internalize the cost of harms from systems even they don't fully understand, creating powerful incentives to invest in safety; victims shouldn't have to prove fault in an opaque system.
- *Con:* It may chill beneficial innovation, especially for smaller developers; harm often runs through the deployer or user, so pinning it on the developer can be unfair and unattributable; a fault-based standard with safe harbors better balances safety and progress.
- *Adjudication:* reward attention to **incentive effects** — how each regime changes developer behavior and who is best placed to prevent harm — over an abstract fairness claim. The strongest debaters distinguish *foreseeable* from *emergent* harms and assign liability to the party that can best prevent each.

**Debate 2: "This House would break up dominant technology platforms."**
- *Pro:* Network effects and data moats create durable, self-reinforcing dominance that conduct rules can't cure; structural remedies restore the competition that killed-in-the-cradle rivals never got, and check power over information and commerce that price metrics miss.
- *Con:* Scale delivers genuine consumer benefits (free, integrated services, heavy R&D); breakups are hard to design, may reduce those benefits, and "bigness" alone isn't harm — target specific conduct (self-preferencing, killer acquisitions) instead, or mandate interoperability.
- *Adjudication:* judge whether the debater **specifies the harm** and shows the **remedy fixes it.** A Pro that says only "too powerful" and a Con that says only "consumers love it" are both under-arguing. Interoperability/portability as a middle path rewards the sophisticated debater.

**Debate 3: "This House believes mass state surveillance is justified if it demonstrably prevents terrorism."**
- *Pro:* The magnitude of a prevented mass-casualty attack can outweigh the privacy cost; targeted monitoring under oversight is a legitimate security tool.
- *Con:* The base-rate problem means *dragnet* surveillance produces overwhelming false positives while catching few real threats; the chilling effect on lawful dissent, function creep, and the irreversibility of surveillance infrastructure are systemic, hard-to-undo harms the "if it prevents terrorism" framing understates.
- *Adjudication:* weigh **magnitude × probability of prevented harm** against **reversibility and systemic risk**, and scrutinize "demonstrably" — does *mass* (vs. targeted) surveillance actually deliver the prevention claimed? Reward whoever engages the base-rate problem rather than trading "safety" against "freedom" as slogans.

**Debate 4: "This House believes automation will, on balance, harm workers in developing economies more than it helps them."**
- *Pro:* Automation erodes exactly the low-skill manufacturing jobs that lifted East Asia out of poverty, threatening "premature deindustrialization" and closing the development ladder before poorer countries can climb it.
- *Con:* Automation raises productivity, lowers prices, and creates new sectors and tasks; historically labor reallocates, and developing economies can leapfrog into services and new industries.
- *Adjudication:* the real debate is about **the distribution and speed of transition** and **whether the manufacturing job-absorption path still exists in an automated world** — not whether work vanishes forever. Penalize a Con leaning on "reallocation is painless" and a Pro treating displacement as permanent mass unemployment without a mechanism. (Connects to Codex 02.)

**Debate 5: "This House would abolish intermediary-liability shields for online platforms" (making them liable for user content).**
- *Pro:* Immunity lets platforms profit from harmful content — defamation, harassment, incitement — with no accountability; liability would force them to prevent foreseeable harms.
- *Con:* Without a shield, platforms would either over-remove lawful speech (collateral censorship) to avoid risk or stop hosting user content at all; the shield is what makes an open participatory internet possible, and the harms are better targeted specifically.
- *Adjudication:* weigh **accountability for harm** against **collateral censorship and the viability of open platforms.** Reward whoever engages the *moderator's dilemma* (liability pushes toward either over-removal or under-moderation) rather than assuming platforms can costlessly filter perfectly.

**Debate 6: "This House believes individuals should have a 'right to be forgotten' (to have lawful information about them delisted/erased)."**
- *Pro:* People should not be permanently defined by old, irrelevant, or unfairly damaging information; dignity and the ability to move on require some control over one's digital past.
- *Con:* It collides with free expression, the public's right to know, and the historical record; it hands private actors (and the state) a tool to scrub inconvenient truths, and "relevance" is a slippery, censorious standard.
- *Adjudication:* weigh **privacy/dignity and rehabilitation** against **free expression and the public record**, and press *who decides* what is "no longer relevant" and how the line resists abuse.

**Debate 7: "This House believes the development of the most advanced AI systems should be subject to mandatory safety regulation before deployment."**
- *Pro:* Frontier systems are dual-use and potentially catastrophic; risk-based, pre-deployment rules (like those for drugs or aircraft) are proportionate to the stakes, and voluntary self-regulation under competitive pressure is unreliable.
- *Con:* The catastrophic risks are speculative and the technology moves faster than regulators; heavy-handed rules entrench incumbents, drive development to less-careful jurisdictions, and forfeit large benefits; target *concrete* harms (bias, fraud, safety) instead.
- *Adjudication:* the crux is **how to weigh speculative tail risk against concrete, near-term costs and benefits.** Penalize both "it will kill us all" and "the risk is zero"; reward whoever calibrates to the actual state of evidence and proposes a *proportionate* mechanism.

**Debate 8: "This House prefers a world of a few interoperable global platforms to one of many national digital ecosystems (the splinternet)."**
- *Pro (global):* A shared, interoperable internet maximizes reach, innovation, and the free flow of information and resists authoritarian information control.
- *Con (sovereignty):* National ecosystems let societies govern data, protect security and culture, and resist foreign platform dominance; a single global order often means the dominance of one country's firms and values.
- *Adjudication:* weigh **openness, innovation, and free flow** against **sovereignty, security, and self-governance**, and note who benefits from each order (incumbent-home-country firms vs. states seeking control).

## 6. Common myths and factual traps

- **The lump-of-labor fallacy.** "Automation will permanently destroy X million jobs" as if the number of jobs is fixed. Aggregate employment is not fixed — *but* the mirror error ("workers just painlessly reallocate") is also wrong; transitions are concentrated and costly. Flag both.
- **The Luddite caricature.** The Luddites were skilled workers protesting the *use* of machinery to cut wages and bypass protections, not people who hated technology. A debater who uses "Luddite" as a synonym for "irrational technophobe" is factually wrong.
- **"This time is different" — asserted, not argued.** The claim that AI breaks the historical pattern must be *earned* with a specific disanalogy (breadth across cognitive tasks, speed). Bare assertion in either direction is a red flag. (And Frey-Osborne-type figures estimate *susceptibility*, not realized job loss.)
- **"Platforms are neutral / just pipes."** Any platform hosting content makes moderation and ranking choices; "neutral non-moderation" is not an available position at scale.
- **"AI is objective."** Systems trained on human data can encode and amplify bias; neutrality is a design achievement, not a default. But "AI is irredeemably biased" over-claims in the other direction.
- **"Big = bad" / "big = fine."** Concentration can bring real consumer benefits *and* real harms; neither the structuralist reflex nor the "bigness is success" reflex substitutes for specifying the mechanism of harm.
- **"If you have nothing to hide, you have nothing to fear."** Ignores the chilling effect, the base-rate problem, function creep, and the irreversibility of surveillance infrastructure — rhetorically common, analytically weak.
- **Misusing the precautionary principle.** Treating it as "ban anything uncertain" ignores the risk of *not* innovating (foregone medicines, safety, growth). The honest version weighs both directions of risk.
- **Technological determinism.** Treating a technology as an autonomous force that "does" things to society, ignoring the design choices, incentives, and regulation that shape its effects (Winner, SCOT).
- **The productivity-paradox blind spot.** Assuming a new technology's effects (good or bad) show up immediately; historically they lag by years or decades.
- **Jevons blind spot.** Assuming a more *efficient* technology reduces total resource use — efficiency can *increase* it.
- **Confusing narrow AI and hypothetical general AI.** Debating today's specific tools as if they were speculative superintelligence, or vice versa; keep the referent clear.
- **Correlation on social harms.** Claims that a technology "causes" polarization, mental-health harm, or democratic decline often outrun the settled evidence; the mechanisms are live research questions. Reward calibration.

## 7. Sharpest evidence and go-to examples

- **The Collingridge dilemma** — the framing that captures the whole timing problem of tech governance; deploy it to structure any "regulate now vs. later" clash.
- **The ATM/bank-teller case** — the crisp, concrete rebuttal to "automation destroys jobs": automating a *task* raised demand for the human complement.
- **The Luddite historical correction** — a fact that flips a rhetorical insult into an argument about labor and design.
- **The productivity paradox (Solow)** — the tool for puncturing both instant-utopia and instant-doom timelines.
- **Network effects + data moats + switching costs** — the mechanism that makes "just let rivals compete" naïve and grounds the structural-remedy case.
- **The base-rate problem** — the decisive analytical tool against *mass* (as opposed to targeted) surveillance.
- **The unavoidability of moderation + the moderator's dilemma** — the facts that dissolve the "neutral platform" framing and structure the intermediary-liability debate.
- **The three-plus regulatory philosophies (rights-based / innovation-first / state-control, plus data-sovereignty models)** — the map for "global rules vs. splinternet" and comparative-regulation debates.
- **Winner's "Do Artifacts Have Politics?"** — the move that shifts a debate from "what does the technology do" to "who designed it, for whom, with what power built in."

## 8. Adjudicator's fact-check notes

Verify these, the domain's most error-prone claims:
1. **Automation jobs claims** — check for the lump-of-labor fallacy *and* its mirror ("painless reallocation"); the honest position is aggregate gains with concentrated, real transition costs, and task-automation ≠ job-elimination (the ATM case).
2. **The Luddites** — skilled workers protesting wage-cutting uses of machinery, not technophobes; flag the caricature.
3. **"This time is different"** — a claim requiring a specific disanalogy, not settled fact; and susceptibility estimates (Frey-Osborne-type) are not predictions of realized job loss.
4. **Platform "neutrality" and "AI objectivity"** — both inaccurate; moderation and bias are design realities, not defaults.
5. **Surveillance efficacy** — mass vs. targeted matters enormously; scrutinize the base-rate problem; "nothing to hide" is a weak line.
6. **Antitrust standards** — distinguish the consumer-welfare standard (Bork) from neo-Brandeisian structuralism (Khan, Wu); "big = bad" and "big = fine" are both incomplete without a specified harm mechanism.
7. **Liability** — strict vs. fault, and ex ante regulation vs. ex post liability, are different incentive structures, not hero-and-villain; check the debater assigns responsibility to the party best able to prevent the harm.
8. **AI existential/catastrophic risk** — genuinely contested; penalize confident claims in *either* direction, and check whether the debater is talking about today's narrow systems or hypothetical general AI.
9. **Attribution** — Collingridge (dilemma), Winner (politics of artifacts), Pinch/Bijker (SCOT), Bork (consumer welfare), Khan/Wu (neo-Brandeisian), Westin (privacy as control), Nissenbaum (contextual integrity), Zuboff (surveillance capitalism), Keynes (technological unemployment), Autor (task framework), Solow (productivity paradox), Bostrom/Russell (AI safety), Jevons (paradox). Misattribution is a real error.
10. **The signature trap** — the sweeping techno-utopian or techno-doom frame with no mechanism or harmed party named, and the determinist habit of treating technology as an autonomous force. Reward the debater who forces specificity and attends to design and incentives.
