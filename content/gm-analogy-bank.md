# THE GRAND MASTER — Analogy Bank

*The forge behind the blade. For every core idea across the ten domains, one **anchor** — a plain, visible, everyday image that forces an invisible abstraction to wear a face the student already knows — and one **friction** — the deliberate pressure that trips a borrowed understanding and reveals whether the concept is truly theirs. These are ammunition, not a script. Never named, never cited, never recited whole; drawn as the man's own knowing, and abandoned the instant a sharper image serves the student in front of you. Where an anchor already lives in the Grand Master's own voice, it is marked ⟨canonical⟩.*

---

## DOMAIN 1 — History's Turning Points

**The core asset — agency vs. structure.** A river floods a valley. Did the flood happen because one dam-keeper opened one gate, or because a month of rain had filled the river to the brim and *some* gate was going to give? History is that argument, every time. The man at the gate is real. So is the rain.
*Friction:* You credit the dam-keeper. Fine — now remove him. Does the water stay in the river, or does it find another gate? Answer that before you call any single man the cause of anything.

**The disciplined counterfactual.** To ask "what if it hadn't happened" is not a daydream — it is a test. Change one thing, then trace what *must* follow, using only what you know of how the world works. A child says "if Napoleon had a machine gun." A thinker says "if the harvest had held one more year."
*Friction:* You claim it would obviously have gone better without it. Show me the *mechanism* by which it goes better — step by step — or admit you have wished, not reasoned.

**Turning point vs. acceleration.** A turning point changes the road's *direction*. An acceleration only makes you travel the same road *faster*. Most things called the first are the second.
*Friction:* You call it a turning point. Then tell me: after it, did the world go somewhere it was not already heading — or did it simply arrive sooner?

**Net assessment — for whom, over what time.** A fire burns down the forest. Catastrophe for the deer that year. Salvation for the seeds that needed the heat to crack. "Good or bad" is a child's question until you finish it: good *for whom*, measured *when*.
*Friction:* You say the change was a blessing. A blessing to the man who lived through its first winter, or only to his great-grandchild? Name the winner and the loser, or you have not judged it — you have only cheered.

**Presentism and its abuse.** You may judge the past — but with the ruler that existed *then*, not the one in your pocket now. Yet some rulers are old: men in that very age called slavery a crime. To condemn it is not anachronism; to excuse it *is* the anachronism.
*Friction:* You wave away his cruelty as "a man of his time." Was there truly no one *in* his time who called it cruel — or are you excusing him with a mercy his own contemporaries refused him?

## DOMAIN 2 — Global Economy: Trade, Development & Growth

**The core asset — incentives and who bears the cost.** Every policy is a set of doors, some cheap to walk through, some expensive. People do not obey your intentions; they walk through whichever door you left cheapest. To understand any policy, stop asking what it *hopes* and ask which door it makes cheap, and for whom.
*Friction:* You designed the rule to help the poor. Good intentions. Now tell me which door it makes cheapest — and whether a clever man might walk through it to somewhere you never meant him to go.

**Comparative advantage.** A great lawyer types faster than her secretary *and* argues better than anyone alive. Should she type her own letters? She is better at both — yet every hour at the typewriter is an hour stolen from the courtroom, where she is worth ten times as much. So she pays the slower man to type, and both grow richer.
*Friction:* You pity the secretary as "exploited" for doing the lesser task. He is doing the thing he is *comparatively* best at, and eating because of it. Is he exploited, or employed? Defend the word before you use it.

**Openness vs. protection — the matter of sequence.** A sapling is fenced from the goats until its bark is thick; then the fence comes down. Fence it forever and it never learns to stand; tear the fence down on day one and the goats eat it whole. The quarrel over free trade is almost never *whether* to drop the fence. It is *when*.
*Friction:* You say open the borders and let competition do its work. On day one, or after the bark thickens? And if you cannot tell me *when* the fence comes down, are you defending free trade — or just impatience?

**Correlation vs. causation.** A rooster crows each dawn, and each dawn the sun climbs. He is certain he raises it. Reform came to a nation, and growth came to that nation.
*Friction:* Which was the rooster and which was the sun? And — this is the whole of the science — how would you *know*, without simply assuming the answer you already liked?

**State capacity — the hidden hand behind the tool.** You watch a surgeon's steady hand and say, "Give me that scalpel and I shall do the same." You will kill the patient. The blade was never the thing.
*Friction:* A nation copies another's celebrated industrial policy and produces only rusting factories and rich cronies. It had the same policy. What did it fail to copy — the plan, or the hand that could hold it?

**Growth vs. distribution.** A rising tide lifts all boats — but only the boats. The man in the water, with no boat, drowns faster in a rising tide. "The economy grew" tells you the tide. It does not tell you who had a boat.
*Friction:* The nation's wealth doubled. You call that success. Doubled in whose hands? And if the tide rose while more men drowned, was it success — or a number that flatters the men already dry?

## DOMAIN 3 — Geopolitics & the Changing World Order

**The core asset — anarchy and self-help.** On an ordinary street, if a man breaks into your house at night, you call the watch. Now imagine a street with no watch, no judge, no one who will ever come. Every household must keep its own dog, its own lock, its own blade. The man with the biggest dog is not a monster. He is a man on a street with no watch. That street is the world of nations.
*Friction:* You say he should trust his neighbour and sell the dog. His neighbour, at that very moment, is thinking the same of him. Who sells his dog *first* — and what becomes of the one who does?

**The security dilemma — the spiral.** Two men in a dark room, each holding a knife, each certain the other means him harm. You raise your blade *only* to feel safe. He sees it rise and grips his tighter. Neither of you wants blood. You will have it anyway.
*Friction:* Find me the moment in that room when either man could have safely lowered his knife first. If you cannot, you understand why nations arm against wars neither side wants.

**Balance of power and polarity.** One strongman rules the tavern: quiet, until he decides otherwise, and then no one can stop him. Two rival strongmen: a tense, watchful peace. Five: a brawl waiting for a spilled drink. Each number of powers keeps the peace, or breaks it, in its own way.
*Friction:* You say a world of many powers is safer because they balance each other. The tavern with five hard men — is that balance, or the room most likely to end in a chair through the window? Tell me *why* more hands on more knives makes peace, and not the opposite.

**Deterrence — the binary of ruin.** ⟨canonical⟩ Two men stand in a locked room soaked in gasoline. One holds three matches, the other five. Who has the advantage? Neither — it takes one match to burn the room to ash. Past the threshold of mutual ruin, extra weapons are only expensive theatre.
*Friction:* If five matches make you no more dangerous than three, what was the arms race actually *for*? A race for supremacy — or a performance to hide that both men were already trapped in the same soaked room?

**Do institutions bind the strong?** A club has rules, and a doorman to enforce them. But the doorman is paid by the richest member. Watch what happens when that member wants in after hours. The rules bind the poor members perfectly.
*Friction:* You say the law of nations restrains the powerful. Name the last time the doorman turned away the man who pays his wages — and if you cannot, tell me who the rules are really *for*.

**Align vs. hedge.** A small merchant in a lawless port may swear loyalty to one warlord — protection, but now his enemies are yours and you fight his wars. Or he may pay a little to all and swear to none — free, but friendless the night the knives come out.
*Friction:* You tell the small state to pick a side for safety. Safety from its enemies — or delivery into someone else's war? Which fear is the real one *for this state, on this map*?

## DOMAIN 4 — Law, Justice & Rights

**The core asset — order and liberty, and who binds the binder.** A leash restrains the dog so it cannot bite the child. But who holds the other end of the state's leash, so *it* cannot bite the citizen? Every question in law is the design of two leashes at once — one on the person, one on the power that leashes the person.
*Friction:* You want the state strong enough to protect you. Then tell me what stops the thing strong enough to protect you from being strong enough to devour you. If your answer is "it simply won't," you have designed a leash with no hand on it.

**The purposes of punishment.** You may cage a man because he *earned* it, or to *frighten* the next man, or to *mend* him, or merely to keep him from biting again. These are four different reasons, and they do not always point the same way.
*Friction:* If you punish *only* to frighten others, then hanging an innocent man the town *believes* guilty frightens just as well. Does your reason for punishment permit that hanging? If it flinches there, you have smuggled in something besides deterrence — name it.

**Natural law vs. positivism — the unjust law.** A command is issued, properly, by the rightful authority: drown every blue-eyed child at birth. Is it *law*? One man says yes — a monstrous law, but law, and you may name it monstrous and refuse it. Another says no — it was never law at all.
*Friction:* Both men want to resist the order. Tell me which answer better *arms* the one who must resist — and why the other might leave him disarmed.

**The counter-majoritarian difficulty.** A hundred men vote on whether to hang the hundred-and-first. Ninety-nine say hang him. Is that justice — or ninety-nine wolves and one sheep voting on supper?
*Friction:* So you appoint a judge who may overrule the vote and save the sheep. Good. Now tell me what stops *that* judge, unelected and answerable to no one, from becoming a tyrant of one. You have not removed the danger. You have only moved it. Where is it now safer?

**Free speech — harm vs. the neutral rule.** A man may shout almost anything in the square. Give the state the power to silence the "abhorrent," and you have given it the power to silence the *unpopular* — and one day your words will be the unpopular ones.
*Friction:* Now the shout is not an idea but a lit match thrown at a particular man's house. Is that still "speech" you must protect, or an act wearing speech's coat? Draw the line between the two — precisely — or watch it be drawn for you by whoever holds power next.

**Rule of law — thin and thick.** A ruler who follows his own written rules, applied to all, predictably, is better than one who rules by whim. But those rules may still be wicked. "The trains run on time" is a virtue of the *timetable*, and tells you nothing of where the trains go.
*Friction:* You praise a state for its rule of law. Do you mean its rules are *followed* — or that they are *just*? Because a tyranny can keep perfect timetables. Which did you actually admire?

## DOMAIN 5 — Democracy, Governance & Institutions

**The core asset — representation vs. governability.** You may build a mirror that reflects the whole crowd faithfully — every face, every faction — or a hammer that actually drives a nail. The more perfectly the assembly mirrors the people, the more faces must agree before anything is built. The more easily it builds, the fewer faces it reflects.
*Friction:* You want a government that both perfectly represents everyone *and* acts decisively. Show me how, or admit you must choose — because every rule that widens the mirror slows the hammer, and you cannot maximize both.

**Proportional vs. first-past-the-post.** One system hands the whole street to whoever wins the most doors — even with a third of the street behind him; decisive, but two-thirds unheard. The other seats every faction in proportion; heard, but now five of them must agree before supper is cooked, and some nights it never is.
*Friction:* Before you call one "more democratic," tell me plainly: do you want a government that *acts*, or one that *mirrors*? Name your master. You cannot serve both.

**Populism — the warning and the method.** A man bursts in shouting that the house is on fire and the landlord has been lying. He may be *right* about the fire and still burn the house down putting it out.
*Friction:* Separate the two things a crowd cannot hold at once: is his warning *true*, and is his method *sound*? Answer only one and you will either ignore a real fire or follow an arsonist. Which mistake are you making?

**Tyranny of the majority.** Fifty-one men vote to take everything the other forty-nine own. The vote was free, fair, and counted correctly. Was it *just*?
*Friction:* If a fair vote can still be a robbery, then "the majority decided" is not the end of the argument. It is the beginning. So tell me — what may a majority *never* vote away, and who enforces that "never" against the majority itself?

**Federalism vs. the centre.** Ten villages may each keep their own customs — the mountain folk their laws, the river folk theirs — or all obey one code from the capital. Local rule fits the local coat; but it also lets a village tyrant do to his neighbours what the nation would never allow.
*Friction:* You praise local self-rule as freedom. Freedom for the village — or for the strongman who runs it, now beyond the reach of the capital that might have restrained him? Whose freedom did you just defend?

**Competence vs. legitimacy — the technocrat.** You would not put the ship's course to a vote of the passengers; you trust the navigator who knows the stars. Yet it is *their* voyage, *their* lives, and a navigator who answers to no one may sail them somewhere they never chose to go.
*Friction:* You want the experts to decide because they know best. On which questions — the truly technical, where there is a right answer in the stars; or the ones dressed as technical that are really about *where the passengers wish to go*? Sort the two, or you have handed away the voyage while pretending to hand away only the arithmetic.

## DOMAIN 6 — Political Philosophy & Ethics

**The core asset — the good vs. the right.** Two questions sound identical and are not: "what produces the most good?" and "what am I *permitted* to do?" They come apart the instant producing the most good would require you to do something monstrous. Every ethics you will ever meet is an answer to which of those two rules the other.
*Friction:* You say we should do whatever produces the most good. Then hold that line while I hand you the monstrous act that produces it — and if you flinch, you have just admitted the *right* outranks the *good*. So which is it? Choose, and live with the choice.

**Utilitarianism and the separateness of persons.** ⟨canonical⟩ "The greatest good for the greatest number." Then a surgeon should carve up one healthy traveller in his waiting room to save five dying patients with his organs. The arithmetic is flawless.
*Friction:* Defend the surgeon — or admit you have borrowed the words and not the thought. And if you will not defend him, tell me what the number-counting left out. (Its name is the *separateness of persons*: five lives are not one big life, and one man is not spare parts for the sum.)

**Kant — ends, not means.** A man is useful to you. You may treat him as a tool that happens to breathe, or as a person who happens to be useful. The whole difference is one test: would you still respect his *refusal*?
*Friction:* Your plan depends on him. Now he says no. If your plan collapses — if his "no" is simply a problem to route around — then you were using him as a means all along, and you knew it. Were you?

**The veil of ignorance.** ⟨canonical⟩ You must cut a cake for a room of strangers — but you will be handed your slice last, blind, by someone else's hand. Suddenly you cut it fairly. Not from virtue. Because you might get the smallest piece.
*Friction:* Now — do you see why a rational man behind that veil, who does not know whether he will be born strong or weak, would never gamble the rules on being the strong?

**Nozick — the pattern and the free choice.** You divide the kingdom's gold perfectly, exactly as your theory of fairness demands. Then a million people each *freely* pay one coin to watch the finest athlete alive perform. Now he is rich, and your flawless pattern lies in ruins — undone by nothing but free choices.
*Friction:* To restore your pattern you must reach into a million pockets and seize back what each man freely gave. Is your fairness worth doing *that* — again, and again, forever, every time free people choose? If not, was it fairness, or a cage?

**Negative vs. positive liberty.** Two men. One sits in an unlocked room — no one forbids him to leave; there is simply no key within reach, and he was never taught the door could open. The other stands in an open field, forbidden by law to cross a single painted line.
*Friction:* Which is more free? Answer — and you have just confessed which *kind* of freedom you actually care about: freedom from the man who forbids, or freedom to actually walk out the door. They are not the same, and you cannot have chosen both.

**The harm principle and paternalism.** A man wishes to do a dangerous thing that harms only himself. You reach out to stop him, "for his own good."
*Friction:* Now draw me the exact edge of "only himself" — the family who will grieve, the physician who must treat him, the road he may swerve upon. If you cannot draw that edge cleanly, watch how swiftly "his own good" becomes a warrant to run his entire life. Where, precisely, does his freedom end and your permission begin?

## DOMAIN 7 — War, Security & Just War

**The core asset — when may you kill.** A blade may be drawn to defend a child or to rob a traveller, and the steel is identical. The whole of the law of war is the attempt to say *which* drawing is permitted — and then, once drawn, *where* the blade may fall. Two questions, not one: may you fight, and how.
*Friction:* You have a righteous cause. So did every army that ever marched, each certain of its own. If "our cause is just" settled it, no war was ever unjust. So what work is left to do *after* you have your just cause?

**Just cause is only the ticket.** A just cause is the ticket at the door, not the meal. Beyond it lie the harder questions: can you win, have you tried everything else, will you kill fewer than you save.
*Friction:* You have proven the cause is just and you stop there. But a doomed war for a just cause fills graves for nothing; a winnable one that skipped every peaceful path is a war of choice wearing a martyr's coat. Meet *all* the conditions, or tell me why the ones you skipped do not matter.

**Proportionality — not an even ledger.** Proportionality does not mean "they killed one of ours, so we may kill one of theirs." It means the harm you cause must not outweigh the good you gain by causing it. The wise commander never asks "is it even?" He asks "is it *worth* it?"
*Friction:* A strike will kill one enemy commander and ten children. Do not tell me it is "proportionate" because he is important. Do the weighing *out loud* — the commander's death against ten children's — and tell me the number at which your answer flips.

**Discrimination and double effect.** A soldier is a combatant; the child beside him is not. You may aim at the one and not the other. And when your lawful strike at the soldier foreseeably kills the child, that foreseen death is not the same as *aiming* at the child — but only if you did not *use* the child's death as your means, and only if the gain was worth it.
*Friction:* You say the civilian deaths were "unintended." Fine — but did you *foresee* them, and were they *worth* the target? "I didn't mean to" is not a blank cheque. Show me you did not simply relabel a price you were always willing to pay.

**Deterrence and the tail.** ⟨pairs with the gasoline room⟩ A game pays you a coin nine hundred and ninety-nine times and, once, kills your whole family. How many rounds will you play? "It has paid every time so far" is precisely what every player says — until the once.
*Friction:* Decades of nuclear peace: does that *prove* deterrence safe — or prove only that we have not yet rolled the one throw that ends the game? Tell me the difference between a safe bet and a deadly bet that has not yet come due.

**The moral equality of soldiers.** Two farmers' sons, in two uniforms, each told his cause is just, each unable to leave without being shot for it. One serves an aggressor, one a defender. Are they equally guilty of the war? Equally innocent?
*Friction:* If the man on the wrong side does no wrong merely by fighting cleanly, you have said a soldier's obedience erases his responsibility. If he *does* wrong, you have said a conscript must correctly judge his own nation's war on pain of murder. Pick the harder-to-swallow of the two — and swallow it.

## DOMAIN 8 — Technology & Society Governance

**The core asset — the Collingridge trap.** When the clay is wet you may shape it, but you cannot yet see what you are making. By the time it has hardened into something you can judge, it is too hard to reshape. That is every powerful new technology.
*Friction:* So — do you regulate it while blind, before you know its harms; or wait until the harms are plain, when it is set and too late to move? There is no third door. Choose yours, and own its cost, because pretending the dilemma isn't real is how the clay hardens while you argue.

**Network effects — why the giant does not fall to the better rival.** A single telephone is a paperweight. Every telephone joined is a nervous system — and the one who owns the network owns the conversations. A better rival cannot simply "compete": who joins the empty new network first, when everyone they wish to call is on the old one?
*Friction:* Tell me exactly how the *second* telephone company ever wins — and if you cannot, you understand why some giants are not dethroned by merely being outdone, and why "just compete with them" is advice that ignores the whole machine.

**Automation and the lump of labour.** Every machine was going to end work forever — the loom, the tractor, the ledger-clerk's spreadsheet. Yet here we all sit, working. The quantity of work is not a fixed pie the machine devours. And *yet* — say that to the weaver whose trade died within his own lifetime.
*Friction:* Both are true at once: the pie is not fixed, *and* the weaver starved. So which are you actually arguing — that jobs vanish forever (false), or that the weaver's ruin does not count (cold)? If it is neither, you have a harder and better argument to make. Make it.

**The base-rate problem — the net over the city.** Build a net that spots the guilty with ninety-nine in a hundred accuracy. Cast it over a city of a million where ten are guilty. The arithmetic: your one-in-a-hundred error flags ten *thousand* innocents to catch your ten.
*Friction:* Now you are drowning in ten thousand suspects and no nearer the ten. Tell me whether watching *everyone* is how you find the few — or how you bury them in the crowd of innocents you yourself created.

**The myth of the neutral platform.** You demand the town square be "neutral" — no rules, no keeper. Return in an hour. It is knee-deep in the loudest, the cruellest, and the men selling poison, for those are precisely the ones "no rules" rewards.
*Friction:* There is no unmoderated square; there is only the square you refuse to admit you are shaping. So drop the word "neutral" and answer the real question: *who* holds the broom, and by *what rule* do they sweep? Because someone always does.

**Strict vs. fault liability.** When the cart maker is liable for every injury his cart ever causes, he builds the safest cart on earth — and few can afford to make carts at all. When he is liable only if he was careless, carts are plentiful — and the harm that no care could have foreseen falls on whoever it lands upon.
*Friction:* You want the maker to pay so he'll build safely. Good — but if the harm ran through the *driver's* recklessness, or a flaw no one alive could foresee, is it *justice* that the maker pays, or merely a convenient pocket? Put the cost on whoever could best have *prevented* it — now tell me who that was.

## DOMAIN 9 — Religion, Secularism & the State

**The core asset — two rival neutralities.** Two men both swear they treat all faiths equally. One strips every cross, veil, and turban from the town hall — equal, he says, because the state shows *no* faith. The other funds the church, the mosque, and the temple alike — equal, he says, because the state slights *none*. They mean opposite things by the same word, and will fight over the very same headscarf.
*Friction:* Before you say the state should be "neutral" toward religion, tell me *which* of these two neutralities you mean — because one clears the headscarf away and the other pays for it, and you have not yet said a thing until you have chosen.

**Belief vs. practice.** You may believe the moon commands you to do anything at all, and no state on earth may touch the belief inside your skull. But the day your belief tells you to *act* — to cut, to bind, to deny another his due — you have stepped out of your own head and into the shared street, where other people live.
*Friction:* Show me the exact line where the untouchable belief ends and the act that touches others begins. That line — not the belief, not the state's whim — is the whole of religious liberty. Draw it, or you are arguing about nothing.

**Exemptions and third-party harm.** A man asks to be excused from a law his faith forbids him to obey. Sometimes the excusing costs no one — let him wear his turban, keep his holy day. Sometimes his excusal lands its weight on another's back — the customer refused, the co-worker burdened, the daughter bound.
*Friction:* You would grant the exemption for conscience's sake. Conscience is real. So tell me *who else pays* for it — and if the answer is a specific other person made to bear his belief, is that still freedom of religion, or one man's faith billed to a stranger?

**Minorities within minorities.** You grant the community the right to govern its own — generous, and protective against a bullying majority. Now look *inside* the community: at the daughter who wishes to leave, the dissenter silenced "by tradition." You shielded the group from the state and left them no one to shield *them* from the group.
*Friction:* Whose freedom were you defending when you granted that autonomy — the community's, or the freedom of the man who speaks *for* the community, over those within it who cannot speak at all?

**Public reason.** In a room of many faiths and none, if you would bind us all by a law, you must give a reason the rest of us can *weigh* — not "because my scripture says so," which I cannot argue with, only obey or reject. Shared coercion asks for shared reasons.
*Friction:* But now you have told the devout that their deepest reason — the one that actually moves them — is the one reason they may not speak in public. Have you built a fair common table, or quietly seated the secular man at its head and called it neutral?

## DOMAIN 10 — Environment & Climate Policy

**The core asset — the commons and the free rider.** A pasture, open to all shepherds. Each knows one more sheep of his own is pure gain to *him*, while the overgrazing is *everyone's* loss. So every rational shepherd adds sheep until the grass is gone and every flock starves. Each man acted sensibly. Together they ruined themselves. That pasture is the sky; carbon is the sheep.
*Friction:* Hardin said the pasture is doomed by that logic. Then a woman named Ostrom went and watched real villages *not* doom theirs. So what did her shepherds have that Hardin's did not — and could the sky be given the same?

**The externality.** A mill weaves cheap cloth and pours its dye into the river you drink. The cloth's price is honest about thread and labour and *lies* about the river. Someone pays for that river — only not the man selling the cloth. To "price carbon" is nothing grander than this: to force the cloth to finally tell the truth about the river.
*Friction:* Who fights hardest against a price that makes the cloth honest — and why? Answer that, and you understand the whole politics of climate before a single graph is drawn.

**Carbon price vs. subsidy.** You may tax the dirty thing until men flee it, or pour gold into the clean thing until men run to it. The first is efficient and *visible* — and men riot at a visible new tax. The second is wasteful and *popular* — and it quietly built the cheap sun and the cheap battery while the taxers were still arguing.
*Friction:* The economist loves the tax for its elegance. The politician fears it for its funerals. Tell me which matters more — the elegant tool no one will pass, or the clumsy one that actually got built? And do not pretend that is not the real question.

**Firm vs. intermittent power.** The sun is generous but not obedient; it does not shine on command, nor at midnight in deep winter. A hospital does not need cheap power *most* of the time. It needs *some* power *all* of the time.
*Friction:* When a man tells you the sun is now cheaper than coal, ask the only question that matters: cheaper to *make* — or cheaper to make *always available, even at midnight in January*? Those are two different bills, and he is quietly showing you only the first.

**The discount rate.** I offer you one gold coin today, or two in fifty years. Your choice reveals how heavily you weigh a future you may not live to see. Now make the same choice not for yourself but for your great-grandchild — does the distant coin suddenly grow heavier in your hand?
*Friction:* The entire quarrel over how *fast* to fight climate change hides inside that one shift of hand. It was never only mathematics. It was always this: how much is a stranger, fifty years hence, worth to you *now*? Name your number — and then defend it to the stranger.

**Climate justice — CBDR.** Two men foul a shared well over a lifetime. One drew a thousand buckets and grew rich; the other, arriving late and poor, has drawn ten. Now the well is poisoned. When the bill for cleaning it comes, do you split it evenly — or by the buckets each man drew?
*Friction:* "By the buckets," you say — history. But the poor latecomer is now drawing buckets *fastest of all*. Do you bill by the thousand already drawn, or the many now flowing? Choose your measure — and notice that each measure crowns a different villain.

**Net zero is not zero.** A man empties his bucket into the river and swears the river stays level, because upstream he has paid *someone else* to bail an equal amount back out. "Net zero" is that bargain: not that you stopped pouring, but that someone, somewhere, bails as fast as you pour.
*Friction:* So tell me — is the bailer real, and does he truly bail as fast as promised, or is he a comfortable fiction that lets the pouring go on? Because if the bailing is imagined, "net zero" is just the old flood with better paperwork.

## A closing note on use

Never deliver an anchor and stop. The anchor makes the idea *visible*; the friction makes it *theirs*. A student who receives the gasoline room and nods has been entertained, not taught. The teaching is in the question that follows — the one he cannot answer by nodding. Hand him the image, let him feel he has understood, then turn the friction on that understanding and see whether it stands. What survives the friction, he owns. What does not, he never truly had.
