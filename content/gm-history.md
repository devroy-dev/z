# KNOWLEDGE CODEX 01 — History's Turning Points

*A deep reference briefing for adjudicators and debaters. Covers the core concepts and vocabulary of historical explanation, the competing schools of historiography in full, the factual record of the major "turning point" candidates, the live debates with arguments and rebuttals on each side, and the myths and traps that recur as if they were fact. Use it to generate motions, to prepare either side, and to check whether a claim made in-round is actually true.*

---

## 1. Scope and the core tension

This domain is about **change over time and how we judge it**. Its motions ask whether a given event, invention, or transition was a genuine "turning point," whether it did more good than harm, whether it should be celebrated or regretted, and what the world would plausibly look like without it. However the motion is worded, it almost always reduces to one of three deeper questions:

1. **Causation.** Did event X *cause* the divergence attributed to it, or did it merely ride, accelerate, or symbolize a trend already underway? Historians distinguish **proximate causes** (the immediate trigger) from **ultimate causes** (the deep conditions), and warn against **overdetermination** (treating one cause as decisive when several sufficient causes were present).
2. **The counterfactual.** What is the *plausible* alternative history if X had not happened, and is that alternative disciplined or merely convenient? The quality of a debater's counterfactual is the single best predictor of who wins a turning-point debate.
3. **Net assessment.** Good or bad — *for whom*, and *over what timeframe*? A change can be catastrophic for one generation and liberating for the next, ruinous for one region and enriching for another.

Underneath all three sits the field's master tension: **agency versus structure.** Did individuals, ideas, and accidents drive history (contingency), or did deep forces — geography, demography, technology, economics — make broad outcomes close to inevitable (determinism)? A debater who cannot locate their claim on this axis is arguing blind, because the same fact ("the printing press spread the Reformation") supports opposite conclusions depending on whether you think the press *caused* the rupture or merely *carried* a rupture that structural forces had already made likely.

A second, quieter tension is **how we are entitled to judge the past at all** — whether moral appraisal across centuries is legitimate or an anachronism. This surfaces in every "regret" and "celebrate" motion.

## 2. Core concepts and vocabulary

- **Turning point.** A moment after which the trajectory of a society, region, or the world measurably diverges from what came before. The claim requires a demonstrable *before/after* break, not merely an important or memorable event. Distinguish a **turning point** (changes direction) from an **accelerant** (speeds an existing direction), a **symptom** (reflects change caused elsewhere), and a **marker** (a convenient date historians use to *periodize*, which may not correspond to any real rupture).
- **Periodization.** The division of the past into eras ("the Renaissance," "the early modern period"). Periodization is a tool imposed by historians, not a natural feature of the past; debating whether an event "ended the medieval world" is partly a debate about a human labeling convention.
- **Proximate vs. ultimate causation.** The spark vs. the powder keg. Strong debaters separate them; weak debaters credit the spark with the whole explosion.
- **Necessary vs. sufficient conditions.** A cause can be *necessary* (without it, no outcome) without being *sufficient* (enough on its own), and vice versa. Much causal confusion in-round is a failure to say which is being claimed.
- **Counterfactual history.** Disciplined "what if" reasoning. Respectable counterfactuals change *one* variable and trace consequences using known mechanisms; lazy ones assume the alternative would obviously be better or worse. Niall Ferguson (ed.), *Virtual History* (1997), defends the method as a test of causal claims; critics (e.g. E.H. Carr, who dismissed it as a "parlour game") warn it invites wishful thinking.
- **Contingency vs. determinism.** Contingency: outcomes hinge on accidents and choices that could easily have gone otherwise (Stephen Jay Gould's "replay the tape of life" metaphor). Determinism: outcomes are driven by structural forces that would have produced a broadly similar result regardless (Marx's modes of production; Jared Diamond's geography).
- **Overdetermination.** When several independently sufficient causes are present, so the outcome would have happened even without any one of them — which undercuts single-cause counterfactuals ("without the assassination, no First World War" is weak if the system was primed to explode anyway).
- **Longue durée.** Fernand Braudel and the Annales School: history is best understood over long spans of slow-moving structures (climate, geography, demography), not the "froth" of events (*histoire événementielle*).
- **Whig history.** Herbert Butterfield's term (*The Whig Interpretation of History*, 1931) for the fallacy of reading the past as an inevitable, righteous march of progress culminating in the present. Its mirror is **declinism** (reading the past as a fall from a golden age).
- **Teleology.** Treating history as if it were *aiming* at an endpoint. A subtle version of the Whig error; almost always a mistake in-round.
- **Presentism.** Judging past actors by present moral standards. Its opposite error is a lazy relativism that refuses any cross-temporal judgment. The defensible middle: some standards (e.g. against chattel slavery) were *articulated and contested in their own time*, so condemning slaveholders is not purely anachronistic.
- **Path dependence.** Early events lock in trajectories that are costly to reverse; small initial advantages compound (the economic exemplar is the QWERTY keyboard; historically, institutional and technological lock-in).
- **Historiography.** The history of how history is written. Because interpretations shift with each generation of historians, a "fact" about causation is often really a *current scholarly consensus* that a debater should present as such, not as settled truth.
- **Net assessment.** Weighing aggregate gains (literacy, longevity, wealth, rights) against distributional costs (whose bodies paid, whose cultures were destroyed) across the relevant timeframe.

## 3. Competing schools of historical explanation (in full)

**A. Great Man / agency theory.** Thomas Carlyle (*On Heroes, Hero-Worship, and the Heroic in History*, 1841): "the history of the world is but the biography of great men." *Modern, defensible form:* at genuine hinge moments — a close-run war, a contested succession, a technological or strategic choice — the decisions and idiosyncrasies of individuals plausibly tip outcomes that structure leaves open. *Strongest case:* structure under-determines timing and selection; *someone* had to make the call, and different individuals demonstrably made different calls with different results. *Standard objection:* it mistakes the visible actor for the underlying cause. "Great men" are often riding forces that would have thrown up a functional equivalent; remove one and another fills the slot. It also flatters winners and writes out collective and anonymous action.

**B. Structural / materialist theory.** Karl Marx: history is driven by the mode of production and the class relations it generates; ideas, law, and leaders are largely *superstructure* rising from the economic *base*. *Strongest case:* broad patterns — the transition from feudalism to capitalism, industrialization, urbanization, the demographic transition — recur across very different cultures, suggesting deep economic drivers rather than a thousand independent accidents. *Standard objection:* structure explains the *range of the possible* but not *which* possibility occurred, or *when*; it under-predicts contingency and has a poor record of predicting specific events (Marx's own forecasts of where revolution would come first were wrong).

**C. The Annales School and the *longue durée*.** Marc Bloch, Lucien Febvre, and above all Fernand Braudel (*The Mediterranean and the Mediterranean World in the Age of Philip II*, 1949) shifted attention from kings and battles to the slow structures of geography, climate, trade routes, and mentalities. *Strongest case:* it reveals how much of "history" is set by forces operating over centuries that no individual could move. *Standard objection:* it can make change itself hard to explain — if everything important moves slowly, why do ruptures happen at all? — and it de-emphasizes agency and events to a fault.

**D. Geographic / environmental determinism.** Jared Diamond, *Guns, Germs, and Steel* (1997): the unequal fates of continents trace to biogeography — the availability of domesticable plants and animals, the east–west axis of Eurasia easing crop and technology diffusion, and the disease immunities that conquest later exploited. *Strongest case:* it explains the *very long-run* head start of Eurasia without recourse to racial or cultural superiority, which is both its scientific and its moral appeal. *Standard objection:* it is weak on the last five centuries — why Europe and not China, both in temperate Eurasia? — and critics charge it flattens human agency, institutions, and culture into a geographic backdrop; some environmental history slides toward crude determinism.

**E. Institutional theory.** Douglass North; Daron Acemoglu, Simon Johnson, and James Robinson (*Why Nations Fail*, 2012; awarded the 2024 economics Nobel). The decisive variable is whether institutions are **inclusive** (broad property rights, checks on power, open opportunity) or **extractive** (elite capture of resources and power). *Strongest case:* natural experiments — divided Korea; the "reversal of fortune" whereby regions that were rich and densely populated at contact became relatively poor where Europeans built extractive institutions — hold geography and culture roughly constant while institutions vary, and outcomes track the institutions. *Standard objection:* it can be circular (what made institutions inclusive in the first place?), and it struggles with fast growth under manifestly non-inclusive institutions — most glaringly modern China (see Codex 02).

**F. Cultural explanation.** Max Weber (*The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, 1905) argued values shaped economic behavior; David Landes (*The Wealth and Poverty of Nations*, 1998) held that culture "makes all the difference." *Strongest case:* it captures why materially and geographically similar societies behave differently. *Standard objection:* "culture" is slippery and frequently deployed circularly — success is credited to culture, then culture is inferred from success — and it shades easily into stereotype and just-so stories.

**G. Contingency / chaos.** Gould, much of counterfactual history, and "cliodynamics" skeptics: small accidents cascade through sensitive systems; replay the tape and you get a different world. *Strongest case:* many "inevitable" outcomes turned on genuine near-misses — weather at a battle, a survived or failed assassination, a single decision. *Standard objection:* pressed to the limit it makes history unintelligible and unfalsifiable; if everything is accident, nothing can be explained or learned.

**H. World-systems and postcolonial/subaltern history.** Immanuel Wallerstein's world-systems theory reads modern history as the expansion of a single capitalist system dividing the globe into core, semi-periphery, and periphery. The **subaltern studies** school (Ranajit Guha and others) insists on recovering the agency of the colonized, the peasant, and the anonymous — the people written out of elite and imperial narratives. *Strongest case:* it corrects a Eurocentric, top-down record and foregrounds who paid for others' "progress." *Standard objection:* the strong world-systems thesis can be economically deterministic, and some postcolonial history is criticized as more interpretive than evidentiary.

**The mature move.** Professional debaters rarely pick one school outright. The strong play is to specify **which layer** a claim operates on — structure sets the possibilities, contingency selects among them, agency executes the selection — and then to *weigh* the layers for the specific case. A debater who says "the Reformation was caused by the printing press" has not yet said whether they mean the press was *necessary* (structure could not have produced it otherwise), *sufficient* (it would have happened regardless of Luther), or merely *facilitating* (it shaped the speed and form). Forcing that precision is often how the round is won.

## 4. The factual record — the major turning-point candidates

*(Dates and figures below are the durable, well-established ones. Where a number is genuinely contested that is flagged; a debater asserting a false precise figure should be caught, but so should an adjudicator who "corrects" a defensible range with false confidence of their own.)*

### 4.1 The Neolithic (Agricultural) Revolution (from roughly 10,000 BCE)
The transition from foraging to farming and settled life, beginning independently in several regions (the Fertile Crescent, China, Mesoamerica, the Andes, New Guinea). *The case for the deepest turning point:* agriculture enabled surplus, cities, writing, states, social hierarchy, and population growth — nearly everything we call "civilization." *The complicating record:* skeletal evidence suggests early farmers were often *shorter, sicker, and more overworked* than the foragers they replaced (nutrition narrowed, disease rose with density and proximity to animals, labor intensified), which is why some scholars (following Jared Diamond's provocative phrasing) have called agriculture a decline in individual welfare traded for collective power. A superb example of "turning point for the species, catastrophe for the individual generation."

### 4.2 Writing (from roughly 3200 BCE)
Independent invention in Mesopotamia (cuneiform), Egypt, China, and Mesoamerica. Writing made law, bureaucracy, accumulated knowledge, contracts, and history itself possible — it is the precondition of most later "turning points," which is why crediting *later* inventions with rupture sometimes ignores that writing did the deeper work.

### 4.3 The printing press (movable type)
- **Chronology.** Movable type predates Europe: Bi Sheng in Song China developed ceramic movable type around 1040; Korea produced metal movable type, with the *Jikji* (1377) the oldest surviving book printed with it. Johannes Gutenberg's press in Mainz, using metal movable type and an oil-based ink adapted to it, dates to around 1440–1450; the Gutenberg Bible is around 1455.
- **The case for a turning point.** An explosion in book production and a collapse in the cost of reproducing text; the standardization of vernacular languages and the fixing of texts (Elizabeth Eisenstein's "typographical fixity"); the spread of the Protestant Reformation (Luther's 95 Theses, 1517, went "viral" in print in a way earlier heresies could not); the acceleration and cumulative build-up of the Scientific Revolution, because results could be reliably shared, compared, and corrected. The canonical scholarly statement is Eisenstein, *The Printing Press as an Agent of Change* (1979).
- **The complicating record.** Print also mass-produced witch-hunting manuals (the *Malleus Maleficarum*, 1487, ran through many editions), propaganda, and error at scale. Literacy rose slowly and unevenly for centuries. And movable type had existed in East Asia for four centuries *without* the same social rupture — which strongly suggests the *social, religious, and linguistic context* (a fragmented, competitive Europe; alphabetic scripts with few characters; rising urban literacy demand) did much of the work the "press alone" story credits to the machine. This is the single best case study in the domain for puncturing technological determinism.

### 4.4 The Black Death (1347–1351)
Bubonic plague killed on the order of a third to a half of Europe's population in a few years (the figure is an estimate, and regional variation was huge). *Arguable consequences:* labor scarcity that raised the bargaining power and wages of surviving peasants and undermined serfdom in Western Europe; shaken faith in the Church; and, on some accounts, a jolt that helped clear the ground for later economic and social change. A powerful case for a *demographic* turning point — and a caution against assuming turning points are always the product of human intention.

### 4.5 The Columbian Exchange (post-1492)
- **Term.** Coined by Alfred Crosby, *The Columbian Exchange* (1972).
- **Crops and animals.** To the Old World: potato, maize, tomato, cassava, chili, cacao — calorie-dense staples that later fed European and Asian population booms (the potato's role in northern European population growth is well documented). To the New World: wheat, sugar, rice, coffee, horses, cattle, pigs.
- **Disease and demographic collapse.** Old World pathogens (smallpox, measles, influenza) devastated indigenous populations with no acquired immunity. Estimates of decline vary widely and are genuinely contested — figures on the order of a 70–90% population fall across the Americas over the century-plus after contact are commonly cited, but the pre-contact baseline itself is uncertain. Present this as an estimate, never a precise fact.
- **Silver and the first global economy.** The Potosí silver mines (from 1545) and Spanish American silver flowed to Europe and, heavily, to China, linking the world's economies into a genuinely global system for the first time.

### 4.6 The Atlantic slave trade (c. 1500–1866)
- **Scale.** The Trans-Atlantic Slave Trade Database records roughly 12.5 million Africans embarked and roughly 10.7 million surviving the Middle Passage — among the better-documented figures in the domain.
- **Consequences.** Foundational to plantation economies (sugar, cotton, tobacco) and to Atlantic capital accumulation; a demographic and social catastrophe for West and Central Africa; the root of enduring racial hierarchies. Whether — and how much — slavery *caused* the Industrial Revolution and Western wealth is a live, contested question launched by Eric Williams's *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944); the honest position credits it as a major contributor without treating it as the sole cause.

### 4.7 The Scientific Revolution (roughly 1543–1687)
Conventionally bracketed by Copernicus's *De revolutionibus* (1543) and Newton's *Principia* (1687), with Galileo, Kepler, and Bacon between. The rise of systematic experiment, mathematization of nature, and institutionalized skepticism. *The case for a turning point:* it created the method that produced the modern world. *The complication:* it built on Islamic, ancient, and medieval foundations and was entangled with astrology and alchemy; the tidy "revolution" is partly a later narrative.

### 4.8 The Industrial Revolution (c. 1760–1840, Britain first)
- **Mechanism.** Mechanized textile production, the improved steam engine (James Watt's separate-condenser improvements, 1760s–1780s), coal, iron, and later railways. Britain's coincidence of coal, capital, colonial markets, comparatively high wages (which incentivized labor-saving machinery), and institutions is the standard explanation; Kenneth Pomeranz's *The Great Divergence* (2000) reframed it as a near-run thing between Britain and China's Yangzi Delta, tipped by the location of coal and access to New World resources.
- **The growth "hockey stick."** After millennia of near-flat GDP per capita, sustained per-capita growth begins — the single most consequential economic break in human history (the long-run data associated with Angus Maddison).
- **The standard-of-living debate.** *Optimists* argue real wages and consumption rose; *pessimists* argue the first two generations of workers saw stagnant or falling real wages, brutal hours, child labor, disease-ridden cities, and lost autonomy, with clear gains arriving only later. Both sides use real data; the honest position is that *long-run* gains are undeniable while *short-run* distributional costs were severe. This is the template for every "This House regrets [a transition]" debate.

### 4.9 The Enlightenment (roughly late 17th–18th century)
- **Content.** Reason, empiricism, natural rights, religious toleration, separation of powers, and the idea of progress (Locke, Montesquieu, Voltaire, Hume, Kant — whose 1784 essay "What Is Enlightenment?" gave the movement its motto, *sapere aude*, "dare to know").
- **The dual legacy (crucial for balanced debate).** The same era that generated rights discourse and later abolitionism also produced early "scientific" racial classification and supplied intellectual cover for empire. A strong debater holds both truths at once: the Enlightenment supplied the *vocabulary of universal rights* later used to attack slavery and colonialism *and* supplied pseudo-scientific hierarchies used to justify them.

### 4.10 The Age of Revolutions (1776–1848)
The American (1776), French (1789), and Haitian (1791–1804) revolutions, and the 1848 European wave. *Significance:* they universalized the language of rights, popular sovereignty, and the nation-state. The **Haitian Revolution** is especially important and often omitted — the only successful large-scale slave revolt to found a state, and the sharpest test of whether Enlightenment universalism meant what it said. The French Revolution is the classic contested case of contingency vs. structure (was the Terror inherent in the revolution or a contingent turn?).

### 4.11 The twentieth century's ruptures
- **The World Wars (1914–1918, 1939–1945).** The collapse of the pre-1945 order; the interwar period is multipolarity's cautionary tale (see Codex 03).
- **The Russian Revolution (1917)** launched the communist experiment and the century's central ideological contest.
- **Decolonization (post-1945).** Indian and Pakistani independence and Partition (1947, with mass displacement and death); the "Year of Africa" (1960, when around 17 countries gained independence); a wave through the 1950s–70s. Whether decolonization, on balance, helped or harmed the societies it freed is a live and deliberately provocative debate (see §5).
- **Bretton Woods (1944) and the Marshall Plan (from 1948)** built the postwar economic order (see Codex 02/03).
- **The digital/internet revolution (late 20th century onward).** The most recent turning-point candidate: a transformation in information comparable, some argue, to print. Too recent for confident net assessment — a good debater notes that we may be inside the rupture rather than after it, which makes counterfactual and net-benefit claims especially uncertain.

## 5. The central debates — arguments and rebuttals per side

**Debate 1: "This House believes the printing press did more to advance human freedom than any political revolution."**
- *Pro:* Print democratized knowledge, broke clerical and state monopolies on information, enabled the Reformation and the emergence of a public sphere, and made every later revolution *possible* by spreading its ideas across borders and generations. Freedom, on this view, is downstream of literacy and the free circulation of information.
- *Con:* Freedom was *won*, not printed — by constitutions, abolition, suffrage movements, and revolutions, through political struggle and often bloodshed. Print also armed censors, propagandists, and inquisitors; the same technology that spread Luther spread witch-panic and later totalitarian propaganda. The turning-point credit belongs to the *movements* print carried, not the press itself, which is a neutral amplifier of liberation and repression alike.
- *Rebuttals:* Pro answers that revolutions themselves depended on the printed word (pamphlets, declarations, constitutions), so print is the deeper cause; Con answers that causation runs through human agency, and a tool used for both freedom and tyranny cannot be credited with the *net* gain. *Adjudication:* reward whoever nails the causal chain — necessary, sufficient, or facilitating — rather than asserting the machine's importance. The East-Asia-without-a-print-revolution fact is the sharpest weapon for Con; the "no Reformation without print" case is the sharpest for Pro.

**Debate 2: "This House regrets the Industrial Revolution."**
- *Pro (regret):* It inaugurated anthropogenic climate change, mass pollution, brutal early-industrial labor, child labor, and the commodification of life; the human cost of the first century was immense and the ecological bill is now falling due catastrophically.
- *Con:* It ended the Malthusian trap, multiplied life expectancy, and lifted humanity off a subsistence ceiling it had never before escaped; without it most of the audience would not exist, and its harms are the price of an escape from grinding, universal poverty. Regret implies a *preferable* counterfactual — and permanent pre-industrial stagnation is not preferable.
- *Adjudication:* the debate turns on the **counterfactual** (what is the alternative — no industrialization at all, or a gentler, slower, better-distributed one?) and on **timeframe weighting** (short-run suffering vs. long-run gain, present ecological cost vs. two centuries of welfare gains). Penalize either side that pretends the other's harms or gains do not exist; the strongest debaters concede the opposing magnitude and win on weighing.

**Debate 3: "This House believes decolonization, on balance, harmed the societies it liberated."** *(A deliberately uncomfortable motion — mark the argumentation, hold viewpoint-neutrality, and do not penalize either side for the discomfort of the position.)*
- *Pro (harmed):* Rushed departures left arbitrary borders, weak institutions, and elites unprepared to govern; some post-independence states saw civil war, kleptocracy, and worse living standards for a period. On this framing, the *manner and speed* of decolonization, not colonialism's end itself, produced avoidable harm.
- *Con:* Self-determination is a good in itself, not reducible to GDP; colonial rule was itself extractive, violent, and the *cause* of the weak institutions blamed on independence; measuring "harm" against a colonial baseline smuggles in the assumption that continued subjugation was the fair comparison.
- *Adjudication:* the crux is the **counterfactual and the baseline** — harmed *compared to what*: continued colonial rule, a slower transition, or an idealized clean break? Reward whoever controls the comparison and honestly weighs dignity/self-rule against material and institutional outcomes. Penalize any argument that treats colonialism as a benign baseline.

**Debate 4: "This House believes contingency, not structural forces, explains most turning points."**
- *Pro (contingency):* Key hinges turned on accidents — weather, a survived or failed assassination, a single decision, a lost battle — that no structural account predicts. Replay the tape and the specifics change decisively.
- *Con (structure):* Zoom out and the same broad outcomes recur across cultures and centuries; accidents change the *who* and the *when*, not the *what*. Overdetermination means that even without the famous trigger, the system was primed to produce a similar result.
- *Adjudication:* the sophisticated resolution is layered — structure bounds the possible, contingency selects within it, agency executes. Reward the debater who *weighs the layers* for concrete cases rather than declaring wholesale for one. The First World War (was it overdetermined, or did the assassination genuinely tip it?) is the ideal battleground example.

**Debate 5: "This House would rather have been born a forager than an early farmer."** *(A vivid way to run the Neolithic net-assessment debate.)*
- *Pro:* Foragers on the archaeological evidence were often healthier, taller, less overworked, and more egalitarian; farming brought disease, drudgery, hierarchy, and famine vulnerability.
- *Con:* Farming's surplus produced everything the question-asker values — security, culture, medicine, longevity for their descendants; the individual trade-off may have been bad but the *species* trade-off was transformative, and "would you rather" smuggles in modern preferences.
- *Adjudication:* a clean individual-vs-collective and present-vs-future weighing debate; reward honest engagement with the skeletal evidence and with the framing trick in "would you rather."

## 6. Common myths and factual traps

- **"Gutenberg invented movable type."** He did not (China and Korea preceded him by centuries); he pioneered a *commercially effective European system* combining metal type, a press, and suitable ink. Catch the overclaim; credit the real innovation.
- **"Columbus discovered a new/empty world."** The Americas held tens of millions of people and complex civilizations. The demographic catastrophe was largely *unintended disease*, not only conquest — an important distinction a good debater draws (it bears on moral responsibility).
- **"Precise indigenous death tolls are settled."** They are not; the pre-contact baseline is itself an estimate. Treat 70–90% as a *range of scholarly estimates*, not a datum, and penalize false precision on either side.
- **"The Industrial Revolution immediately raised living standards."** Contested; the early decades' real-wage record is the heart of the optimist/pessimist debate. Asserting an immediate rise as settled fact is an error.
- **"Early farmers were better off than foragers."** The skeletal evidence often suggests the reverse for the first agricultural populations. A debater who assumes agriculture was an unambiguous individual improvement is vulnerable.
- **"The Enlightenment was uniformly progressive" / "uniformly a tool of empire."** Both false; its legacy is genuinely dual, and the strongest debaters hold both halves.
- **The Whig / teleological fallacy.** Narrating any event as an inevitable, righteous step toward the present ("this led straight to modern democracy"). Its mirror, declinism, is equally a trap. Flag both.
- **Single-cause history.** "The printing press created the modern world"; "slavery alone built Western wealth"; "the assassination caused the First World War." Multi-causal, often overdetermined events reduced to one lever. The standing rebuttal is "compared to what, and alongside what else?"
- **Convenient counterfactuals.** "Without X it would obviously have been better/worse." Demand the mechanism and the plausibility of the alternative.
- **The "acceleration ≠ turning point" trap.** Many claimed turning points sped up an existing trend rather than redirecting it. Always ask whether the event *caused* divergence or *rode* it.
- **Presentism vs. its abuse.** Do not let a debater dodge a *defensible* moral judgment by crying "presentism," nor let one impose modern frameworks anachronistically without argument. The test is whether the standard was available and contested in the period.
- **Reifying periodization.** Treating "the Middle Ages ended in 1492/1517" as a fact about the world rather than a labeling convenience.

## 7. Sharpest evidence and go-to examples

- **Movable type in East Asia without a "print revolution"** — the single best counter to technological determinism about the press; forces the "the machine wasn't sufficient" concession.
- **Divided Korea** — the cleanest natural experiment for *institutions over geography/culture*: one people, one peninsula, opposite institutions, opposite outcomes within two generations.
- **The skeletal record of early farmers** — the crisp evidence that a species-level turning point can be an individual-level welfare loss (the Neolithic debate's trump).
- **The potato and northern European population growth** — a tangible illustration of the Columbian Exchange's material force.
- **Luther in print** — the case that *technology + a message + a fractured, competitive political landscape* together produced a rupture that none alone would have.
- **The Great Divergence "near-run" framing (Pomeranz)** — the go-to for arguing industrialization was contingent rather than a foreordained European destiny.
- **The Maddison long-run per-capita GDP series** — the empirical backbone of "the Industrial Revolution is the single great economic break."
- **The Haitian Revolution** — the sharpest test of whether Enlightenment universalism was universal, and a corrective to Eurocentric turning-point lists.
- **The First World War's origins** — the standard battleground for overdetermination vs. contingency.

## 8. Adjudicator's fact-check notes

Watch for and verify these, the claims most often gotten wrong in this domain:
1. **Priority claims about inventions** ("who was first") — usually oversimplified; movable type and many "European firsts" have earlier non-European antecedents. Reward the debater who gets the priority right.
2. **Precise casualty/population figures** presented as settled — most are contested ranges (indigenous collapse, Black Death mortality, Middle Passage totals except where the database is firm). Penalize false precision, not honest estimates.
3. **Dates** — Reformation 1517; Columbian contact 1492; Black Death 1347–51; Watt's key improvements 1760s–80s; Bretton Woods 1944; Partition 1947; Year of Africa 1960. A confidently wrong date on a hinge event is a real error.
4. **Causal overreach** — "X caused modernity/freedom/wealth" claims should specify the mechanism (necessary? sufficient? facilitating?) and survive the "compared to what?" test. Reward the debater who forces this precision.
5. **Presentism vs. legitimate judgment** — do not let a debater dodge a defensible moral judgment by invoking "presentism," nor let one impose modern frameworks anachronistically without argument; the test is contemporaneous availability of the standard.
6. **Single-cause and overdetermination** — flag mono-causal explanations of multi-causal events, and reward the debater who raises overdetermination against a fragile counterfactual.
7. **The counterfactual** — the single most decisive and most abused move in the domain. A won debate almost always features the side with the more disciplined alternative history; judge counterfactuals on mechanism and plausibility, not rhetorical confidence.
8. **Historiography as consensus, not gospel** — many "facts" of causation are current scholarly interpretations; reward a debater who signals this, and don't penalize a defensible reading that departs from the most familiar narrative.
