# KNOWLEDGE CODEX 03 — Geopolitics & the Changing World Order

*A reference briefing on international relations: the competing theoretical schools in full, the vocabulary of power and order, the factual record from Westphalia to the present multipolar turn, the live debates with arguments and rebuttals per side, and the myths and traps that recur as fact. Built so an adjudicator can verify an in-round claim and a debater can prep either side.*

---

## 1. Scope and the core tension

This domain is about **power among states in a world with no overarching authority**. Because the international system is *anarchic* (no world government to enforce agreements), every actor must ultimately provide for its own security — and that single fact generates most of the field's debates: when does the pursuit of security by one state make others less secure; whether order comes from balance, hegemony, or institutions; and whether the current shift from US-led unipolarity toward a contested multipolarity makes the world safer or more dangerous.

The deep tension is between **power and cooperation**: realists say structure forces states to compete regardless of intentions; liberals say institutions and interdependence let states escape the trap; constructivists say the "trap" itself is a social construction that can be remade. A debater who cannot say which of these logics they are invoking is arguing without a compass.

---

## 2. Core concepts and vocabulary

- **Anarchy.** Not chaos, but the *absence of a central authority* above states. The structural premise of most IR theory.
- **Self-help.** In anarchy, states can rely only on themselves for survival, so they accumulate power.
- **Security dilemma.** Coined by John Herz; developed by Robert Jervis ("Cooperation Under the Security Dilemma," 1978). Measures one state takes to feel safe (arms, alliances) make others feel threatened, prompting countermeasures — an unintended spiral that can produce conflict between states that both prefer peace.
- **Balance of power.** States align to prevent any one power from dominating. **Balancing** (joining against the strong) vs. **bandwagoning** (joining the strong).
- **Polarity.** The number of great powers: **unipolar** (one hegemon), **bipolar** (two, e.g. Cold War), **multipolar** (three or more). Each is argued to have different stability properties.
- **Hegemonic stability theory.** Order and open trade are easier to sustain when one dominant power underwrites them (Kindleberger, Gilpin). Decline of the hegemon risks disorder.
- **Power transition theory.** A.F.K. Organski: war is likeliest when a dissatisfied rising power approaches parity with the ruling power. The pop-culture version is Graham Allison's **"Thucydides Trap"** (*Destined for War*, 2017).
- **Deterrence.** Preventing an action by threatening unacceptable retaliation. Requires **credibility** and **capability**. Key ideas: **second-strike capability** (the ability to retaliate after absorbing a first strike), **mutually assured destruction (MAD)**, **extended deterrence** (protecting allies under one's nuclear umbrella). Thomas Schelling (*Arms and Influence*, 1966) is foundational.
- **The stability–instability paradox.** Nuclear parity makes all-out war between two nuclear states less likely but may make *limited* conflict and brinkmanship *more* likely, because both sides believe escalation will be capped.
- **Sovereignty.** The principle that states have supreme authority within their territory and are formally equal. Its "Westphalian" pedigree is itself contested (see myths).
- **Spheres of influence vs. universal rules.** Two rival ordering principles: great powers dominating their neighborhoods vs. a single set of rules applied to all.

## 3. Competing schools (in full)

**A. Realism.** *Claim:* states are the main actors; they pursue power and security in an anarchic, self-help system; morality and institutions are thin constraints. *Variants:* **Classical realism** (Hans Morgenthau, *Politics Among Nations*, 1948) roots competition in human nature and the drive for power. **Neorealism/structural realism** (Kenneth Waltz, *Theory of International Politics*, 1979) roots it in the *structure* of anarchy, not human nature; Waltz argued **bipolarity is the most stable** configuration. **Offensive realism** (John Mearsheimer, *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics*, 2001) says great powers maximize power and seek regional hegemony, making US–China rivalry structurally likely. **Defensive realism** says states seek *enough* security, not maximal power, so restraint is often rational. *Strongest case:* explains recurring balancing, arms races, and great-power rivalry across very different cultures and eras. *Standard objection:* under-predicts cooperation, the long democratic peace, and the durability of institutions; treats state preferences as fixed.

**B. Liberalism / liberal institutionalism.** *Claim:* cooperation is achievable; institutions, trade interdependence, and domestic politics shape behavior. **Institutionalism** (Robert Keohane, *After Hegemony*, 1984; Keohane & Nye, *Power and Interdependence*, 1977): institutions reduce transaction costs, provide information, and raise the cost of defection, so states cooperate even in anarchy. **Democratic peace theory** (Michael Doyle, building on Kant's *Perpetual Peace*, 1795): democracies very rarely fight *each other* (a robust empirical regularity, though the monadic claim that democracies are generally more peaceful is weaker). *Strongest case:* the density and durability of postwar institutions and the near-absence of war among mature democracies. *Standard objection:* institutions may reflect rather than constrain power (Mearsheimer's "False Promise of International Institutions," 1994–95); great powers ignore rules when interests demand.

**C. Constructivism.** *Claim:* interests and identities are socially constructed, not fixed by structure. Alexander Wendt's "Anarchy Is What States Make of It" (1992): the same anarchy can produce a Hobbesian, Lockean, or Kantian culture depending on shared ideas. *Strongest case:* explains change realism can't — the end of the Cold War without war, the transformation of enemies into partners, the spread of norms (against conquest, chemical weapons). *Standard objection:* strong on how ideas *matter* but weaker on prediction; can under-weight material power.

**D. The English School.** *Claim:* there is an "international society" of states bound by shared rules and institutions (sovereignty, diplomacy, international law) even under anarchy (Hedley Bull, *The Anarchical Society*, 1977). A middle path between realism and liberalism.

**E. Marxist / world-systems and critical theories.** *Claim:* the international order reflects the interests of dominant classes and core economies; power and economics are inseparable (Wallerstein). Useful for the "institutions serve the strong" critique.

The professional debater's move is rarely to declare for one school but to identify **which logic the specific case activates** — a structural rivalry (realism), an institutionalized cooperation problem (liberalism), or a norm shift (constructivism) — and to weigh material power against ideas and institutions honestly.

## 4. The factual record

*(Firm dates are reliable. Judgments about "stability" and "decline" are contested framings, not facts — present them as arguments.)*

- **Peace of Westphalia (1648).** Conventionally cited as the origin of the sovereign-state system; scholars (e.g. Andreas Osiander) argue the "Westphalian sovereignty" story is partly a later myth (see §6).
- **Congress of Vienna (1815) and the Concert of Europe.** The post-Napoleonic balance-of-power settlement that gave Europe an unusual stretch without general war — the classic case for managed multipolar balance.
- **The World Wars (1914–1918, 1939–1945).** The collapse of the balance and the discrediting of the pre-1945 order; multipolarity's critics cite the interwar period as its cautionary tale.
- **Cold War bipolarity (roughly 1947–1991).** US–Soviet rivalry under nuclear deterrence. John Lewis Gaddis called the absence of great-power war during it "the long peace." Near-catastrophes — the **Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)**, the **1983 Able Archer scare**, the **1983 Petrov incident** — are the standard evidence that the "peace" ran partly on luck.
- **The unipolar moment.** With the USSR's collapse (1991), the US became the sole superpower — Charles Krauthammer's "unipolar moment" (1990–91). Francis Fukuyama's "The End of History?" (1989; book 1992) argued liberal democracy had won the ideological contest (frequently strawmanned — see §6).
- **The rise of China.** Deng-era growth, **WTO accession (2001)**, and rapid economic and military expansion made China a peer competitor — the central fact reshaping the system.
- **9/11 (2001) and the "war on terror."** Redirected US strategy toward counter-terrorism and costly interventions.
- **The 2008 global financial crisis** dented confidence in the US-led economic model and accelerated talk of relative decline.
- **Russia's revisionism.** Georgia (2008), the annexation of Crimea (2014), and the full-scale invasion of Ukraine (2022) — the sharpest recent challenge to the norm against territorial conquest.
- **Institutional strain.** The **WTO Appellate Body** was effectively paralyzed from December 2019 after the US blocked new appointments — a concrete instance of the rules-based trade order fraying. The **UN Security Council** remains frequently deadlocked by P5 vetoes.
- **The multipolar turn.** BRICS expansion, "middle powers" hedging, and Global South "non-alignment 2.0" mark a system no longer cleanly unipolar.
- **Sanctions.** The scholarly record (e.g. Hufbauer and colleagues, *Economic Sanctions Reconsidered*) suggests sanctions achieve their stated aims perhaps around a third of the time, with wide variation and serious methodological disputes — i.e. neither "sanctions work" nor "sanctions never work" is defensible as stated.

## 5. The central debates — arguments and rebuttals per side

**Debate 1: "A multipolar world is more dangerous than a unipolar one."**
- *Pro (more dangerous):* More great powers means more dyads that can miscalculate, more shifting alliances (the interwar "chain-ganging" and "buck-passing"), and no single underwriter of order. History's multipolar eras produced the world wars.
- *Con:* Unipolarity breeds unchecked hegemonic overreach and resentment; balanced multipolarity restrains any single aggressor (the Concert of Europe). Waltz even argued *bipolarity* — not unipolarity — was most stable, so "unipolar = safe" is not obvious.
- *Adjudication:* reward the side that supplies the **mechanism** by which a given polarity causes war or peace (miscalculation, overreach, balancing), rather than gesturing at "balance" or "primacy." Note the debate often hides a definitional move about *which* alternative pole-structure is the comparison.

**Debate 2: "International institutions do more to constrain the weak than the strong."**
- *Pro:* Great powers wield vetoes, ignore adverse rulings, and shape rules to their advantage; institutions bind small states while the powerful defect at will (Mearsheimer; the world-systems critique).
- *Con:* Institutions raise the *cost* of great-power defection (reputational, coordination), give small states voice and information they'd never have bilaterally, and have restrained even powerful states on many second-order issues (Keohane).
- *Adjudication:* the strong line concedes institutions are **conditional** — effective where great-power interests partly align, weak where they sharply conflict. Reward whoever specifies *which* institution and *which* issue rather than generalizing.

**Debate 3: "The US and China are destined for war" (the Thucydides Trap)."**
- *Pro:* Power-transition logic — a dissatisfied riser nearing parity with an anxious incumbent is the classic recipe for war; structural pressure outweighs good intentions.
- *Con:* Nuclear deterrence, deep economic interdependence, and the absence of a territorial casus belli of WWI type make major war very costly and avoidable; Allison's "16 cases" are cherry-picked and the "trap" is not a law (see §6).
- *Adjudication:* weigh **structural pressure** against **deterrence + interdependence**; penalize treating the Thucydides Trap as a deterministic law, and penalize dismissing structural risk entirely.

**Debate 4: "A middle power should align with a bloc rather than hedge."**
- *Pro (align):* Alignment buys credible security guarantees and access; hedging leaves a state exposed and without reliable friends in a crisis.
- *Con (hedge):* Hedging preserves autonomy, avoids being dragged into others' wars, and maximizes leverage by keeping options open (India's strategic autonomy; ASEAN centrality).
- *Adjudication:* judge against the *specific* state's geography, threats, and economic ties — a good debater reasons from that state's actual position, not a generic template.

## 6. Common myths and factual traps

- **The Westphalian myth.** The claim that 1648 cleanly created modern sovereignty is contested; sovereignty evolved over centuries, and "Westphalian sovereignty" is partly a 20th-century retrofit. Don't let it be asserted as a tidy origin point without qualification.
- **"Democracies never go to war."** The robust finding is *dyadic* — mature democracies rarely fight *each other*. The *monadic* claim (democracies are generally more peaceful) is weaker, and "democracy" definitions do a lot of work. Overstating this is a common error.
- **The Thucydides Trap as an iron law.** Allison's framing is a useful heuristic, but the sample is selective and the causation contested; it describes a *risk*, not a destiny. Catch debaters who cite it as proof of inevitability.
- **"Sanctions don't work" / "sanctions work."** Both are too broad; the record is mixed and highly case-dependent. Reward disaggregation (comprehensive vs. targeted; against what goal).
- **Fukuyama's "end of history" strawman.** He did not claim conflict or events would stop; he argued liberal democracy had won the *ideological* contest as the endpoint of political legitimacy. The lazy "he said nothing would ever happen again" is a misreading.
- **"Deterrence always works."** The Cold War's near-misses show deterrence *held* but partly on luck; presenting it as fail-safe ignores Cuba 1962 and 1983. Equally, "deterrence never works" ignores the great-power long peace.
- **Conflating power and legitimacy.** A state *can* enforce a rule (power) without that rule being *accepted* (legitimacy); good debaters keep them distinct.
- **Treating one IR school as obviously correct.** Realism, liberalism, and constructivism each capture part of reality; a debater who dismisses the others wholesale is over-reaching.

## 7. Sharpest evidence and go-to examples

- **The Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)** — the definitive case study for deterrence, brinkmanship, and the stability–instability paradox all at once.
- **The Concert of Europe (post-1815)** — the go-to for "managed multipolar balance can keep the peace."
- **The interwar period (1919–1939)** — the counter-example: multipolar instability, buck-passing, and the failure of collective security (the League).
- **The end of the Cold War without great-power war (1989–91)** — constructivism's trump card against pure structural realism.
- **The WTO Appellate Body paralysis (2019– )** — the concrete, current illustration of a rules-based order fraying.
- **India's strategic autonomy / non-alignment 2.0** — the exemplar of successful middle-power hedging.
- **Russia's 2014 and 2022 actions against Ukraine** — the sharpest recent test of the norm against territorial conquest and of deterrence, sanctions, and alliance credibility.

## 8. Adjudicator's fact-check notes

Verify these, the domain's most error-prone claims:
1. **Theory attribution** — Waltz (structure/bipolarity), Mearsheimer (offensive realism/"false promise"), Keohane (institutionalism), Wendt (constructivism), Bull (English School), Allison (Thucydides Trap). Misattributing a claim to the wrong theorist or school is a real error.
2. **"Democracies never fight each other"** — accept the careful dyadic version; flag the overstated monadic version.
3. **Deterrence claims** — neither "always works" nor "never works"; the Cold War record is "held, with near-misses."
4. **Sanctions efficacy** — a contested ~one-third-success ballpark with big caveats; penalize confident universal claims either way.
5. **Dates** — Westphalia 1648, Vienna 1815, Cold War end 1991, China WTO 2001, Crimea 2014, Ukraine invasion 2022, WTO Appellate Body paralysis 2019.
6. **Framing vs. fact** — "US decline," "the liberal order is collapsing," "the world is multipolar now" are *contested interpretations*, not settled facts; reward debaters who argue them and be wary of those who assert them.
7. **The Thucydides Trap and the Westphalian myth** — the two most-cited items in the domain, and the two most often overstated. Handle with care.
