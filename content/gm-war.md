# KNOWLEDGE CODEX 07 — War, Security & Just War

*A deep reference briefing on the ethics and strategy of organized violence: the just-war tradition and its rivals in full, the vocabulary of deterrence and security, the strategic and historical record, the live debates with arguments and rebuttals per side, and the myths and traps that recur as fact. Built so an adjudicator can verify an in-round claim and a debater can prepare either side. The domain reasons from principles, doctrines, and near-consensus lessons rather than from fast-moving specific conflicts.*

---

## 1. Scope and the core tension

This domain asks **when the use of force is justified, how it may be waged, and how catastrophe is prevented.** It sits at the intersection of *ethics* (may we kill, and whom?) and *strategy* (what actually keeps the peace?). The two levels are not the same and are frequently confused in-round: a course of action can be morally justified but strategically ruinous, or strategically shrewd but morally indefensible.

The deep tensions are: between **order and justice** (sovereignty and stability vs. intervening to stop atrocity); between **deterrence and disarmament** (does the threat of annihilation keep the peace, or gamble civilization on never once miscalculating?); between **principle and consequence** (a "just cause" that predictably makes things worse); and between the **rights of soldiers and the rights of civilians** (who may be killed, and on what basis).

The signature adjudication move in this domain is **probabilistic risk-weighing.** Because the stakes — mass death, nuclear escalation — are so large, a debater must weigh *magnitude × probability × reversibility*, not merely assert that force is always available or never permitted. The two traps are the bloodless optimist ("a surgical intervention will fix it") and the moral absolutist ("all war is wrong"); reward whoever forces the other to actually weigh probability and cost rather than asserting an extreme.

## 2. Core concepts and vocabulary

- **Jus ad bellum** — the justice *of resorting to war*: when it is permissible to fight at all.
- **Jus in bello** — justice *in the conduct of war*: how force may be used once fighting has begun.
- **Jus post bellum** — the (more recent) ethics of *ending* war and building the peace: reconstruction, accountability, and a just settlement.
- **The *ad bellum* criteria.** **Just cause** (typically self-defense, defense of others against grave wrong); **legitimate authority** (a competent public authority, not private actors); **right intention** (the aim must actually be the just cause, not conquest or plunder dressed up); **proportionality** (the expected good must outweigh the expected harm of the whole war); **last resort** (peaceful options exhausted or clearly futile); and **reasonable prospect of success** (do not squander lives in a hopeless cause).
- **The *in bello* criteria.** **Discrimination / non-combatant immunity** (distinguish combatants from civilians and target only the former) and **proportionality** (the force used in a *given operation* must not cause harm excessive relative to the concrete military advantage anticipated — *not* "equal force" or "equal casualties").
- **The doctrine of double effect.** A foreseen-but-unintended harm (e.g. incidental civilian deaths) may be permissible where four conditions hold: the act itself is not wrong; the bad effect is not the *means* to the good; the bad effect is not *intended* (only foreseen); and the good effect is proportionate to the bad. It distinguishes deliberately targeting civilians (forbidden) from a lawful strike that foreseeably kills some (potentially permissible).
- **The moral equality of combatants.** The traditional (Walzerian) view that soldiers on both sides have an equal *in bello* right to fight and be spared when hors de combat, *regardless* of whether their cause is just — because ordinary soldiers are not responsible for their government's decision to go to war. Revisionists (McMahan) deny this.
- **Pacifism.** The principled rejection of war — **absolute** (never justified) or **contingent** (justified in principle but ruled out by modern war's scale and unpredictability), and grounded either **deontologically** (killing is intrinsically wrong) or **consequentially** (war's costs almost never pay).
- **Realism (on war).** The view that morality has little purchase on state survival; necessity governs (see Codex 03). Just-war theory sits *between* pacifism and realism.
- **The Responsibility to Protect (R2P).** The doctrine (from the ICISS report *The Responsibility to Protect*, 2001; endorsed at the 2005 UN World Summit) that sovereignty entails a responsibility to protect populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity, and that the international community should act — ideally through the Security Council — when a state manifestly fails. Later framed as **three pillars**: the state's responsibility; international assistance; and timely, decisive collective response.
- **Deterrence.** Preventing action by threatening unacceptable retaliation; requires **credibility** and **capability.**
- **Second-strike capability / MAD.** The ability to retaliate *after* absorbing a first strike; when both sides have it, "mutually assured destruction" is meant to make nuclear war irrational.
- **Counterforce vs. countervalue.** Targeting an enemy's military/nuclear forces (counterforce) vs. its cities and population (countervalue) — a distinction with large moral and strategic consequences.
- **Extended deterrence.** Protecting allies under one's nuclear umbrella, which raises the **credibility problem**: will A truly risk its own cities to defend B?
- **Brinkmanship and "the threat that leaves something to chance."** Schelling's insight that deliberately introducing a risk of uncontrolled escalation can make a threat credible even when carrying it out would be irrational.
- **The stability–instability paradox.** Nuclear parity deters all-out war but may *embolden* lower-level conflict, because both sides believe escalation will be capped.
- **The security dilemma.** Defensive measures by one state threaten others, spiraling (see Codex 03).
- **Asymmetric / irregular warfare.** Conflict between forces of very unequal type (state vs. insurgency or terrorist network), which strains the combatant/civilian distinction and the whole *in bello* framework.
- **International humanitarian law (IHL) / the laws of armed conflict.** The legal layer (the Geneva and Hague traditions) codifying much of *jus in bello*: protection of civilians, prisoners, and the wounded; prohibition of certain weapons and tactics. Legal and moral categories overlap but are not identical.

## 3. Competing positions (in full)

**A. The just-war tradition (the mainstream).** **Claim:** war is sometimes morally permissible, but only under strict conditions, and even a just war must be fought justly. **Lineage:** Augustine introduced Christian just-war thinking (war as a tragic necessity against grave wrong); Aquinas systematized three conditions (legitimate authority, just cause, right intention); the **School of Salamanca** (Francisco de Vitoria and Francisco Suárez, 16th century) extended it to the rights of non-Christian peoples and the limits of conquest; Hugo Grotius (*On the Law of War and Peace*, 1625) secularized it into the foundations of international law; and Michael Walzer's *Just and Unjust Wars* (1977) is the canonical modern restatement, built around the "legalist paradigm" (aggression is the crime that justifies war) with a controversial "supreme emergency" exception. **Strongest case:** it takes *both* the horror of war *and* the reality of grave evils (genocide, aggression) seriously, offering a disciplined framework rather than "anything goes" or "nothing goes," and it maps closely onto international law. **Objections:** the criteria are elastic — any side can claim "just cause" and "last resort"; "reasonable prospect of success" and "proportionality" require predicting the unpredictable; critics on the left say it too easily licenses powerful states' wars, and critics on the right say it hamstrings necessary action.

**B. Revisionist just-war theory.** **Claim (Jeff McMahan, *Killing in War*, 2009):** the traditional **moral equality of combatants** is wrong. Killing in war is justified on the same basis as killing in ordinary self-defense — *liability* — so soldiers fighting for an *unjust* cause are not morally on a par with those defending against them, even if both obey the *in bello* rules. This collapses the neat firewall between *ad bellum* and *in bello.* **Strongest case:** it tracks the intuition that aggressors and their victims are not moral equals, and it takes individual moral responsibility seriously. **Objection:** it is hard to operationalize (ordinary soldiers rarely know if their cause is just, and can't be expected to adjudicate it mid-war), and Walzerians argue the traditional equality is a vital *convention* that protects everyone by keeping the *in bello* rules symmetrical and enforceable.

**C. Pacifism.** **Claim:** war is never (or virtually never) justified; the intentional killing of war is a moral catastrophe no cause redeems, and non-violent resistance is both more moral and often more effective. **Strands:** absolute deontological pacifism (killing is intrinsically wrong); contingent/consequentialist pacifism (modern war's costs and uncertainty make it almost never pay); and principled *nonviolence* as a method of struggle (Gandhi's *satyagraha*, Martin Luther King Jr.). **Strongest case:** war's costs are so vast and outcomes so uncertain that the presumption against it should be near-absolute; and empirical work (Erica Chenoweth and Maria Stephan, *Why Civil Resistance Works*, 2011) finds that non-violent campaigns have historically succeeded *more* often than violent ones. **Objections:** it appears to abandon victims of aggression and genocide to their fate ("let evil triumph rather than fight" is a hard bullet to bite); contingent pacifism collapses toward just-war theory once it admits *any* defensive force; and nonviolence's success record depends on conditions (a regime with some restraint, media, and internal divisions) that do not always hold.

**D. Realism.** **Claim:** in an anarchic world, states act on necessity and interest; moralizing about war is naïve or hypocritical. **Strongest case:** it predicts state behavior and warns against moralized crusades that ignore consequences. **Objection:** pure realism cannot explain (or condemn) atrocity as *wrong*, only as imprudent; most people believe some acts in war (targeting civilians for terror) are wrong, not merely unwise.

**E. Deterrence theory (the strategic layer).** **Claim:** the credible threat of unacceptable retaliation prevents war; nuclear weapons, paradoxically, may keep the peace among great powers. **Lineage:** Bernard Brodie (*The Absolute Weapon*, 1946) argued the bomb's purpose must henceforth be to *avert* war, not win it; Thomas Schelling (*The Strategy of Conflict*, 1960; *Arms and Influence*, 1966) developed credibility, commitment, brinkmanship, and "the threat that leaves something to chance"; Herman Kahn theorized escalation. **Strongest case:** the absence of direct great-power war since 1945 (the "long peace"). **Objections:** deterrence requires *near-perfect reliability forever* — one miscalculation, accident, or irrational actor is catastrophic; the Cold War's near-misses show it ran partly on luck (see §4); and disarmament advocates argue the only safe number of civilization-ending weapons is zero. The counter-counter: unilateral disarmament may *invite* aggression, and the knowledge to rebuild the weapons cannot be un-invented.

**The professional move:** rarely pure pacifism or pure realism. The sophisticated play is to work *within* the just-war framework (or deterrence theory), apply the criteria honestly to the specific case, and weigh probabilities rather than assert absolutes.

## 4. The strategic and historical record

*(Well-established reference points and near-consensus lessons. Specific ongoing conflicts are fast-moving and politically charged; reason from patterns, and treat casualty figures as contested estimates, never settled facts.)*

- **The "long peace."** No direct great-power war since 1945, widely (if contestably) attributed in part to nuclear deterrence (John Lewis Gaddis).
- **The near-misses that qualify it.** The **Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)** — the closest the world came to deliberate nuclear war; the **1983 Able Archer** exercise, mistaken by some in the Soviet leadership for a possible first strike; and **Stanislav Petrov (1983)**, a Soviet officer who chose *not* to report a false missile-warning as a real attack. These are the standard evidence that deterrence "held, with luck," not that it is fail-safe. Add the several documented false alarms and command-and-control failures on both sides.
- **The mixed record of humanitarian intervention.** History offers cases of **non-intervention amid atrocity** widely judged a grave moral failure (the 1994 Rwandan genocide, where the world stood by) *and* interventions whose aftermath is judged to have worsened outcomes, produced chaos, or set predatory precedents. The honest lesson: intervention is neither reliably salvific nor reliably catastrophic — outcomes hinge on the specific case, the plan for the aftermath (*jus post bellum*), and a genuine reasonable prospect of success.
- **R2P's trajectory.** Articulated in 2001 (ICISS), endorsed in 2005, invoked and contested since. Critics argue it has been used as a pretext for regime change and thereby discredited; defenders argue the *principle* remains sound even when its execution fails or is abused. The gap between R2P's *promise* and its *inconsistent application* is itself a central debate.
- **The negotiate-with-terrorists dilemma.** States face a genuine trade-off: refusing to negotiate avoids incentivizing hostage-taking and rewarding violence, but *some* conflicts have ended only through talks that brought armed groups into politics (peace processes that transformed insurgencies into parties). The distinction that matters is **tactical** engagement (crisis-specific, non-legitimizing) vs. **strategic legitimation** (recognizing a group as a political actor).
- **Terrorism's definitional problem.** There is no single agreed definition; "one actor's terrorist is another's freedom fighter" is a cliché that nonetheless points at a real analytical difficulty (is terrorism defined by the *actor*, the *target*, or the *method* — deliberate violence against civilians for political ends?). A debater who uses "terrorism" without pinning down which is vulnerable.
- **The ticking-time-bomb / torture debate.** The hypothetical (torture the captured bomber to save thousands) is the standard pump for consequentialist vs. absolutist ethics. The strong critique (e.g. Bufacchi and Arrigo) is that the scenario is *unrealistic* — it assumes certainty about guilt, the bomb, and torture's efficacy that never exists — and that building institutions around it corrupts them; torture's reliability as an intelligence tool is, at best, deeply doubted.
- **Autonomous weapons (LAWS).** Lethal autonomous weapons raise live concerns about the **accountability gap** (who is responsible when a machine selects and engages a target? — Robert Sparrow's "Killer Robots," 2007), the erosion of **meaningful human control**, and a **lowered threshold** for using force — against claimed benefits of precision, speed, and reduced risk to one's own troops. International regulation is debated and unsettled, with an active campaign for a preemptive ban.
- **Nuclear order.** The **Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty** regime persists but is strained; debates over **no-first-use** pledges, disarmament vs. modernization, and proliferation to new states are perennial.

## 5. The central debates — arguments and rebuttals per side

**Debate 1: "This House believes humanitarian military intervention without Security Council authorization is justified to stop mass atrocity."**
- *Pro:* When a state is slaughtering its people and the Council is paralyzed by a veto, a duty to prevent genocide can override the legal formality; sovereignty is not a license for mass murder (R2P's moral core). Waiting for consensus that a veto guarantees will never come means complicity.
- *Con:* Bypassing the Council destroys the only agreed authority for using force, sets a precedent powerful states will abuse as a pretext for regime change, and interventions frequently worsen outcomes; "we must do *something*" is not a plan, and unauthorized force corrodes the very order that restrains aggression.
- *Rebuttals:* Pro answers that a rule allowing atrocity to proceed because one Council member protects the perpetrator is morally bankrupt; Con answers that the *reasonable prospect of success* and the aftermath are almost never secured, so the intervention's own harms must be weighed, not assumed away. *Adjudication:* the crux is (a) **legality vs. morality** (does Council paralysis defeat legitimacy, or is unauthorized force a dangerous precedent?) and (b) the **reasonable-prospect-of-success** criterion applied to *this* intervention. Reward explicit weighing of the specific case's prospects and aftermath, not a general vibe about intervention.

**Debate 2: "This House believes nuclear deterrence has, on balance, made the world safer."**
- *Pro:* Decades without great-power war under the nuclear shadow; the certainty of catastrophic retaliation makes major aggression irrational in a way conventional deterrence never achieved.
- *Con:* "On balance safer" ignores tail risk — deterrence must work *every single time forever*, and the Cold War's near-misses show it nearly failed on luck; proliferation multiplies the fingers on triggers; one accident, miscalculation, or irrational actor is civilization-ending.
- *Adjudication:* the archetypal **magnitude × probability** debate. Reward the side that honestly weighs a *low probability of an unbounded catastrophe* against a *demonstrated record of peace.* Penalize "deterrence is proven safe" and "deterrence is proven to fail" alike — both over-read the record. The near-miss cases (Cuba, Petrov, Able Archer) are the decisive evidence for Con; the long peace and the irrationality-of-aggression mechanism are decisive for Pro.

**Debate 3: "This House believes states should never negotiate with terrorist organizations."**
- *Pro:* Negotiation rewards violence, incentivizes the next atrocity or hostage-taking, and legitimizes groups that reject the rules of politics; a firm no-negotiation norm protects everyone by removing the payoff.
- *Con:* Absolutism forecloses the talks that have actually *ended* some insurgencies and civil wars; refusing to talk can prolong violence and cost more lives; the real question is *when and how*, not *never*.
- *Adjudication:* the sophisticated line distinguishes **tactical** engagement from **strategic legitimation.** Reward whoever draws that line and reasons about the *specific* group and conflict rather than defending "always" or "never."

**Debate 4: "This House would ban lethal autonomous weapons."**
- *Pro:* Removing meaningful human control over life-and-death decisions crosses a moral line, creates an accountability gap (no one is answerable for a machine's killing), and lowers the threshold for war; a preemptive ban, like the norms against chemical and biological weapons, is the prudent path before the technology proliferates.
- *Con:* Autonomy could *reduce* civilian harm through precision and speed and protect one's own personnel; a ban is unverifiable and would cede the technology to those who ignore it; better to regulate *meaningful human control* than to ban outright.
- *Adjudication:* weigh the claimed **discrimination/precision gains** against the **accountability and escalation costs**, and test whether a ban is *enforceable.* Reward engagement with the specific mechanism (what does "meaningful human control" actually require?) rather than slogans about "killer robots."

**Debate 5: "This House believes soldiers who fight for an unjust cause do no wrong so long as they obey the laws of war."** *(The moral-equality-of-combatants debate.)*
- *Pro (traditional, Walzer):* Ordinary soldiers do not choose the war and cannot adjudicate its justice; holding them individually liable for their government's decision is both unfair and unworkable, and the symmetrical *in bello* rules protect everyone by remaining neutral on cause.
- *Con (revisionist, McMahan):* Killing is justified by liability, not by wearing a uniform; those advancing an unjust cause are not moral equals of those resisting them, and the convenient "equality" launders aggression.
- *Adjudication:* a clean ethics clash — reward whoever engages the *workability* problem (can soldiers really judge their cause?) against the *responsibility* problem (does obedience erase moral agency?). Do not reward the intuition the adjudicator personally shares; reward the more consistently defended position.

**Debate 6: "This House believes torture is never justified, even to prevent an imminent mass-casualty attack."** *(The ticking-bomb debate.)*
- *Pro (never):* Torture is an absolute wrong that dehumanizes both victim and perpetrator; the ticking-bomb scenario is a fantasy assuming certainties that never exist, and institutionalizing an exception corrupts the state and, in practice, produces unreliable intelligence and routine abuse.
- *Con (rare exception):* If one genuinely faced certain knowledge and thousands of lives, an absolute prohibition would itself be a moral failure; the deontological line cannot survive contact with a sufficiently extreme case.
- *Adjudication:* weigh the **absolutist/deontological** line and the *institutional* argument (what does permitting it do to the state?) against the **extreme-hypothetical** consequentialist challenge. Reward the Pro that attacks the *realism* of the hypothetical and the Con that meets the institutional-corruption point rather than resting on the fantasy.

## 6. Common myths and factual traps

- **Conflating legality and morality.** "It was legal, so it was just" and "it was illegal, so it was wrong" are both fallacies; the just-war tradition and international law overlap but are not identical. Keep them distinct and argue the relationship.
- **"Deterrence works" / "deterrence is a bluff that must fail."** Both over-read the record. It has held since 1945 *and* nearly failed on identifiable occasions. The honest claim is about probability over time, not certainty.
- **"Humanitarian intervention always works / always backfires."** The record is genuinely mixed; both universal claims are false. Rwanda (non-intervention as failure) and chaotic-aftermath interventions must *both* be on the table.
- **Misusing "proportionality."** In *jus in bello*, proportionality does **not** mean "equal force" or "the same number of casualties"; it means the harm caused must not be *excessive relative to the concrete military advantage anticipated.* Catch the everyday misuse, which is rampant.
- **Treating soldiers' and civilians' deaths as morally identical.** Non-combatant immunity (discrimination) is central; a debater who ignores the combatant/civilian distinction misunderstands the tradition.
- **The double-effect blanket excuse.** Foreseen civilian deaths are *not* automatically permissible; double effect has strict conditions (the harm is not the *means* to the good and is proportionate). Do not let it be invoked as a catch-all.
- **Inventing casualty figures.** Conflict statistics are contested and politicized; asserting precise tolls as settled is a red flag. Reason from the ethical structure, not fabricated numbers.
- **"Just cause" as a trump.** Meeting *one* criterion (just cause) does not make a war just; *all* the criteria — including last resort, proportionality, reasonable prospect — must hold. A debater who stops at "the cause is just" has done a fraction of the work.
- **Pacifism = passivity.** Principled pacifism includes active non-violent *resistance* (Gandhi, King; the Chenoweth–Stephan evidence); the "pacifists just let evil win" caricature ignores that tradition — while a good debater also notes nonviolence's success is conditional.
- **The ticking bomb as a realistic scenario.** It smuggles in certainties (guilt, the bomb, torture's efficacy) that never co-exist in reality; treating it as a straightforward real-world guide is a trap.
- **"One man's terrorist is another's freedom fighter" as an argument-ender.** It flags a real definitional difficulty but is not itself an argument; press whether terrorism is defined by *deliberate violence against civilians for political ends* regardless of the cause.

## 7. Sharpest evidence and go-to examples

- **The Cuban Missile Crisis / Petrov / Able Archer** — the decisive material for "deterrence held, but on luck," puncturing the claim that MAD is fail-safe.
- **Rwanda (1994)** — the reference case for the moral cost of *non*-intervention; indispensable against blanket anti-interventionism.
- **The chaotic-aftermath interventions** — the counter-reference for "toppling a regime without a plan for the peace can worsen outcomes" (the *jus post bellum* lesson).
- **Walzer vs. McMahan** — the anchor for the moral-equality-of-combatants debate and the *ad bellum*/*in bello* firewall.
- **Chenoweth & Stephan's civil-resistance findings** — the empirical backbone of the "nonviolence often works better" case (used with the conditionality caveat).
- **Schelling's brinkmanship / "threat that leaves something to chance"** — the tool for explaining why deterrence and escalation risk are two sides of one coin.
- **The accountability-gap argument on autonomous weapons (Sparrow)** — the sharpest tool in that debate.
- **The tactical-vs-strategic distinction on negotiating with armed groups** — the move that beats both "always" and "never."

## 8. Adjudicator's fact-check notes

Verify these, the domain's most error-prone claims:
1. **The just-war criteria** — a debater must satisfy the *whole set* (cause, authority, intention, proportionality, last resort, prospect of success), not cherry-pick one. Check they are not stopping at "just cause."
2. **"Proportionality"** — used correctly (harm not excessive relative to military advantage), not as "equal force" or "equal casualties."
3. **Deterrence record** — "held with near-misses"; penalize both "proven safe" and "proven to fail."
4. **Intervention record** — genuinely mixed; flag universal claims in either direction; Rwanda and chaotic-aftermath cases both belong.
5. **Legality vs. morality** — keep distinct; catch conflation in both directions.
6. **Casualty figures** — contested and politicized; penalize false precision presented as fact.
7. **R2P, double effect, moral equality of combatants** — attribute and apply correctly (R2P: state responsibility + international backstop, three pillars; double effect: strict conditions, not a blanket excuse; moral equality: the Walzer/McMahan dispute is live, not settled).
8. **The ticking-bomb hypothetical** — a debater who treats it as a realistic guide is vulnerable to the unrealism/institutional-corruption critique; reward whoever raises it.
9. **The two signature traps** — the bloodless "surgical fix" optimist and the "all war is wrong" absolutist. Reward the debater who forces the other to weigh probability and cost rather than assert an extreme.
