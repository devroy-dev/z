# KNOWLEDGE CODEX 06 — Political Philosophy & Ethics

*A deep reference briefing on the moral and political-philosophical frameworks that underpin value debates: the ethical theories and theories of justice in full, the vocabulary, the canonical thinkers and thought-experiments, the live debates with arguments and rebuttals per side, and the myths and traps that recur as fact. This codex marks differently from the empirical ones — reward conceptual rigor, consistency, and survival of the standard objection over data. Built so an adjudicator can check whether a framework is being used correctly and a debater can prepare either side.*

---

## 1. Scope and the core tension

This domain supplies the **frameworks** the other codices borrow: what makes an action right, what a just society owes its members, when the collective may override the individual, and what we owe to those absent from the room (future generations, distant strangers, non-humans). It is the least empirical and most conceptual of the ten — a claim here is judged less by "is it factually true" and more by "is it coherent, consistently applied, and defensible against the strongest objection."

The deep tensions are between **the good and the right** (maximizing welfare vs. respecting constraints and duties), between **liberty and equality**, and between **the individual and the collective.** The signature adjudication trap is the emotionally powerful case with **no underlying principle.** Reward the debater who supplies (or dismantles) the framework beneath the intuition — and penalize *opportunistic framework-switching* (rights when convenient, consequences when convenient) above almost anything else, because consistency is the coin of the realm here.

---

## 2. Core concepts and vocabulary

- **Consequentialism.** Rightness depends only on outcomes; the right act produces the best consequences. **Utilitarianism** is the leading form: maximize aggregate welfare/happiness (Bentham; refined by Mill, who added quality of pleasures). **Act** utilitarianism evaluates each act; **rule** utilitarianism evaluates acts by the rules that, generally followed, maximize welfare.
- **Deontology.** Rightness depends on conformity to duties/rules, not just outcomes; some acts are wrong even if they produce good results. **Kant:** act only on maxims you could will as universal law (the *categorical imperative*), and treat persons always as **ends in themselves, never merely as means** (the humanity formula).
- **Virtue ethics.** Focuses on character rather than acts or outcomes: the right action is what a virtuous person would do; ethics is about cultivating virtues and human flourishing (*eudaimonia*) (Aristotle; the "golden mean" between excess and deficiency; modern revival: Anscombe, MacIntyre's *After Virtue*).
- **The separateness of persons.** The core anti-utilitarian objection (Rawls, Nozick): aggregating welfare across people treats humanity as one super-person and can sacrifice individuals for the sum, ignoring that each person's life is distinct and their own.
- **The demandingness objection.** Consequentialism may require enormous, unrelenting sacrifice (you must *always* maximize the good), which seems to leave no room for personal projects — Bernard Williams's "integrity" objection.
- **Negative vs. positive liberty.** Isaiah Berlin ("Two Concepts of Liberty," 1958): **negative** liberty is freedom *from* interference; **positive** liberty is freedom *to* be one's own master / self-realization. Berlin warned positive liberty can be twisted to justify coercion "for your own good."
- **The harm principle.** Mill (*On Liberty*, 1859): the state may restrict a competent adult's liberty only to prevent harm *to others*, not merely to benefit that person (anti-paternalism).
- **Paternalism.** Overriding a person's choices for their own good. **Soft** paternalism intervenes only where choice is non-voluntary (poor information, incapacity, addiction); **hard** paternalism overrides even informed voluntary choices. **Libertarian paternalism / "nudging"** (Thaler & Sunstein) steers choices while preserving formal freedom.
- **The veil of ignorance / original position.** Rawls's device: principles of justice are those we would choose for society if we did not know our own place in it (class, talents, gender, conception of the good).
- **Rights as trumps.** Rights that override ordinary welfare-maximizing calculation (Dworkin).
- **The non-identity problem.** Derek Parfit's puzzle: policies that affect *which* people come to exist make it hard to say we have "harmed" future people, since different choices produce different individuals — a hard problem for intergenerational ethics.
- **The is/ought gap and the naturalistic fallacy.** Hume's point that one cannot straightforwardly derive an *ought* from an *is*; often invoked (and often *mis*-invoked) in-round.

## 3. Competing frameworks (in full)

**A. Utilitarianism / consequentialism.** **Claim:** maximize aggregate well-being; the right act or policy has the best net consequences. **Strongest case:** impartial (everyone's welfare counts equally, which grounded historic reforms — Bentham on the treatment of animals, prisoners, women), practical (weigh costs and benefits), and reformist (it condemns cruelties defended by tradition). **Objections:** (1) it can justify sacrificing an innocent for the greater good (the *separateness of persons*); (2) it is extremely *demanding*; (3) welfare is hard to measure and compare across people; (4) it can license repugnant conclusions if the numbers align. **Rule utilitarianism** answers some of these by evaluating rules rather than acts, at the cost of either collapsing back toward act utilitarianism or hardening into rule-worship.

**B. Deontology (Kantian).** **Claim:** morality is a matter of duties and universalizable principles; persons have dignity and may not be used *merely* as means, whatever the outcome. **Strongest case:** it protects the individual against being sacrificed for aggregate gain; it grounds inviolable rights; it captures the intuition that some acts (torturing an innocent) are wrong *even if* they would produce good results. **Objections:** (1) rigidity — it seems to forbid lying even to a murderer at the door (Kant's own notorious example); (2) duties can conflict with no clear tie-breaker; (3) it can look coldly indifferent to consequences (*fiat justitia, ruat caelum* — "let justice be done though the heavens fall").

**C. Virtue ethics.** **Claim:** focus on *being* a good person (courage, justice, temperance, practical wisdom / *phronesis*) rather than on a decision-procedure for acts. **Strongest case:** it matches how moral life is actually lived — character, habituation, judgment — and avoids both the rigidity of rules and the coldness of calculation. **Objections:** (1) it under-specifies what to *do* in a hard case ("do what the virtuous person would do" can be unhelpful); (2) virtues can conflict; (3) what counts as a virtue may be culturally relative.

**D. Theories of justice.**
- *Rawlsian justice-as-fairness (A Theory of Justice, 1971).* From the original position behind the veil of ignorance, rational choosers would pick: (1) equal basic liberties for all, and (2) the **difference principle** — social and economic inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged (and attach to positions open under fair equality of opportunity). **Strongest case:** a principled account of *when* inequality is acceptable, grounded in what we would choose if impartial. **Objections:** why maximize the *minimum* (Rawls assumes strong risk-aversion)? Nozick's liberty critique (below); communitarian critiques that the "unencumbered self" behind the veil is a fiction.
- *Nozickian libertarianism (Anarchy, State, and Utopia, 1974).* **Claim:** individuals have inviolable rights (especially self-ownership); a distribution is just if it arose through **just acquisition** and **voluntary transfer**, whatever the resulting pattern. Only a **minimal state** (protection, contract enforcement) is justified; redistributive taxation is "on a par with forced labor." The **Wilt Chamberlain argument:** any *patterned* theory (like Rawls's) is continually upset by voluntary choices, so preserving the pattern requires constant interference with liberty. **Strongest case:** takes individual liberty and voluntary choice seriously; exposes the coercion latent in enforcing distributive patterns. **Objections:** the theory of just acquisition is under-specified, and real historical holdings are often tainted by force and fraud; self-ownership does not obviously extend to unlimited property in external resources; it can license severe deprivation.
- *Luck egalitarianism.* **Claim:** inequalities from *unchosen* bad luck (birth, natural talents) are unjust and should be corrected; inequalities from *choices* are not. **Objection:** it can be harshly moralistic (abandoning the "imprudent") and struggles to draw the choice/luck line.
- *The capabilities approach (Sen, Nussbaum).* **Claim:** justice concerns people's real **freedoms to do and be** what they value (their capabilities), not just resources or welfare — because people convert resources into functioning at different rates (a disabled person needs more to achieve the same mobility). **Strongest case:** captures real freedom better than income metrics; influential in development and human-rights thinking. **Objection:** which capabilities, and who draws up the list?
- *Communitarian critique (MacIntyre, Sandel, Taylor, Walzer).* **Claim:** liberalism's abstract, choosing individual is a fiction; we are constituted by communities, traditions, and shared meanings, and justice must be rooted in them (Walzer's "spheres of justice" — different goods are justly distributed by different principles). **Objection:** risks relativism and can shelter oppressive traditions.

**E. Liberty and paternalism.** The Millian harm principle marks the liberal default (adults may harm only themselves), against which paternalists argue that capacity limits, addiction, imperfect information, and *internalities* (harms to one's own future self) justify some intervention. **Nudging** tries to steer while preserving formal freedom — praised as a middle path, criticized as manipulation and a slippery slope.

**The professional move:** name the framework, apply it *consistently*, defend it against its standard objection, and ideally give a reason to prefer it over the strongest rival. Switching frameworks opportunistically is the cardinal sin.

## 4. Canonical thinkers, works, and thought-experiments

*(Reasoning anchors, not required citations.)*
- **Bentham** (utilitarianism; the "felicific calculus"), **Mill** (*On Liberty*, 1859; the harm principle; higher and lower pleasures; a "utilitarian of rules" on many readings).
- **Kant** (*Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*, 1785; the categorical imperative; humanity as an end).
- **Aristotle** (*Nicomachean Ethics*; virtue, the mean, *eudaimonia*, *phronesis*).
- **Rawls** (*A Theory of Justice*, 1971; veil of ignorance, difference principle) vs. **Nozick** (*Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, 1974; entitlement theory, minimal state, Wilt Chamberlain).
- **Sen & Nussbaum** (the capabilities approach); **Walzer** (spheres of justice).
- **Berlin** ("Two Concepts of Liberty," 1958); **Bernard Williams** (integrity objection to utilitarianism).
- **Parfit** (*Reasons and Persons*, 1984; the non-identity problem; population ethics and the "repugnant conclusion").
- **The trolley problem** (Philippa Foot; Judith Jarvis Thomson) — the classic pump for the consequentialist/deontological divide (divert a trolley to kill one and save five? push a person off a bridge to do the same?). Note its *limits*: intuition-pumps test principles, they do not settle them, and can mislead.
- **Peter Singer's drowning child** — the pump for duties to distant strangers and the demandingness of beneficence.
- **Social-contract lineage:** Hobbes (*Leviathan*, 1651; order over the state of nature), Locke (natural rights, government by consent), Rousseau (the general will).

## 5. The central debates — arguments and rebuttals per side

**Debate 1: "This House believes a just society should prioritize equality of outcome over equality of opportunity."**
- *Pro (outcome):* Formal equality of opportunity is hollow when starting points are radically unequal — background, wealth, and unchosen luck shape outcomes so heavily that only attention to results delivers real fairness (Rawlsian and luck-egalitarian intuitions).
- *Con (opportunity):* Equalizing outcomes erases desert, agency, and incentive; it punishes effort and choice and requires continual coercive interference with liberty (Nozick's Wilt Chamberlain point).
- *Adjudication:* reward whoever **defines the equality metric** (welfare? resources? capabilities? real opportunity?) and defends it against the standard objection (incentive/liberty cost for outcome-egalitarians; "hollow formal equality" for opportunity-egalitarians). Strong debaters often converge on *fair* equality of opportunity plus a floor — reward that when earned.

**Debate 2: "This House believes the state has no right to prevent competent adults from harming only themselves."**
- *Pro (anti-paternalism):* Sovereign adults own their lives; the harm principle bars coercion for one's "own good"; the power to protect people from themselves is the power to impose the majority's values on private choices.
- *Con (paternalism):* "Purely self-regarding" harm is rarer than it looks (third-party and social costs, dependents); addiction, misinformation, and cognitive bias undermine the "competent, informed choice" premise; soft paternalism intervenes only where autonomy is already compromised.
- *Adjudication:* the strong line **locates the precise condition** that licenses intervention (incapacity, non-voluntariness, externalities, internalities) rather than arguing blanket liberty or blanket protection. Penalize a Con that cannot limit its principle (it would justify banning anything risky) and a Pro that pretends no choice has spillovers.

**Debate 3: "This House prefers a Rawlsian society to a libertarian one."**
- *Pro (Rawls):* Behind the veil, rational people would insure against ending up worst-off; a just society improves the position of the least advantaged, not just the aggregate or the lucky.
- *Con (Nozick):* Justice concerns how holdings *arose* (just acquisition and voluntary transfer), not whether they match a pattern; enforcing Rawlsian distribution requires continually overriding free choices and treats people's talents as collective property.
- *Adjudication:* a clean framework clash — reward internal consistency and engagement with the *best* version of the rival (does the Rawlsian answer the liberty objection? does the libertarian answer tainted-acquisition and severe-deprivation?). Do *not* reward whichever society the adjudicator personally prefers.

**Debate 4: "This House believes future generations have rights that can override the preferences of the present."**
- *Pro:* We routinely bind the future (debt, climate, nuclear waste); impartiality across time is as compelling as impartiality across space; discounting future people's interests to near-zero is arbitrary.
- *Con:* The non-identity problem complicates "rights" (our choices determine *who* exists); rights are usually held by actual, identifiable persons; present people have real, weighty claims too.
- *Adjudication:* reward engagement with the **non-identity problem** and with *how* to weigh present against future (discounting, thresholds, a duty to *sustain* rather than a right held by specific future persons). A debater who has never heard of the non-identity problem is under-equipped here.

**Debate 5: "This House believes we have as strong a duty to distant strangers as to those close to us."**
- *Pro (Singer):* If we can prevent something bad without sacrificing anything of comparable moral importance, we ought to — and physical distance is morally irrelevant (the drowning-child logic scaled up).
- *Con:* Special obligations (to family, community, compatriots) are real and constitutive of the moral life; a duty this demanding is self-effacing and ignores the legitimate partiality that gives life meaning (the integrity objection).
- *Adjudication:* weigh **impartial beneficence** against **special obligations and demandingness**; reward whoever engages the strongest version of the other (Singer's rigor vs. the integrity/partiality reply) rather than caricaturing it.

## 6. Common myths and factual traps

- **Framework-label without content.** Saying "utilitarian" or "Kantian" as a badge without deploying the actual machinery (aggregation and its objections; universalizability and ends-in-themselves). Catch the name-drop.
- **Opportunistic framework-switching.** Invoking rights when consequences are inconvenient and consequences when rights are inconvenient — the single biggest tell of a weak debater here. A consistent framework beats a convenient one.
- **"Utilitarianism just means the greatest good for the greatest number."** A common oversimplification hiding the hard parts — aggregation, the separateness of persons, act/rule.
- **Kant "forbids lying even to save a life" — as a knockdown.** Kant did (in)famously argue this about the murderer at the door, but it is a contested *implication*, and modern Kantians have responses; treating it as an obvious reductio is lazy.
- **Treating the trolley problem as settling anything.** Intuition-pumps *test* principles; they don't *prove* them. A round that becomes trolley-problem ping-pong without extracting the underlying principle is shallow.
- **"Rawls proves we should maximize equality."** Rawls's difference principle *permits* inequalities that benefit the worst-off; it is not a mandate for equal outcomes. Misstating it is common.
- **"Libertarianism = selfishness / anarchy."** Nozick's theory is a rigorous rights-based position (self-ownership, just transfer), not "greed"; strawmanning it is an error.
- **Confusing negative and positive liberty** — and missing Berlin's warning that positive liberty can be weaponized to coerce people "toward their real selves."
- **Naturalistic-fallacy abuse.** Not every move from facts to values is fallacious, and the is/ought gap is frequently invoked incorrectly. Use with care.
- **Assuming a shared value is "self-evident."** The domain's job is to *defend* values, not assert them; "obviously X is good" with no argument is exactly what should lose.

## 7. Sharpest evidence and go-to examples

- **The Wilt Chamberlain argument (Nozick)** — the crispest tool for showing that enforcing *any* distributive pattern requires continual interference with liberty.
- **The veil of ignorance (Rawls)** — the go-to device for arguing what impartial choosers would pick and reframing self-interested claims as questions of fairness.
- **The separateness of persons** — the decisive objection whenever a side is aggregating welfare across people.
- **The capabilities approach** — the sharpest move when "resources/income" metrics miss real freedom (disability, conversion factors).
- **The harm principle + soft/hard paternalism distinction** — the toolkit for any liberty-vs-protection debate.
- **The non-identity problem** — essential for any intergenerational or population-ethics motion.
- **Singer's drowning child** — the pump for duties to distant strangers.
- **Nudging / libertarian paternalism** — the middle-ground vocabulary that dissolves false binaries between total liberty and total control.

## 8. Adjudicator's fact-check notes

Because this domain is conceptual, "fact-checking" means checking **whether frameworks are described and applied correctly**:
1. **Attribution** — Rawls (veil of ignorance, difference principle), Nozick (entitlement, minimal state, Wilt Chamberlain), Kant (categorical imperative, ends-in-themselves), Mill (harm principle), Berlin (two liberties), Sen/Nussbaum (capabilities), Walzer (spheres), Parfit (non-identity), Williams (integrity), Singer (beneficence). Misattributing a concept is a real error.
2. **The difference principle** *permits* inequality benefiting the worst-off — not equal outcomes. Flag misstatements.
3. **Consistency** — the primary marker. A debater who switches frameworks opportunistically should lose ground even if each individual point sounds good.
4. **Survival of the standard objection** — reward a framework defended against its known weakness (utilitarianism vs. the separateness of persons; Kant vs. rigidity; Rawls vs. the liberty objection; Nozick vs. tainted acquisition).
5. **Thought-experiments** — reward using them to *extract a principle*; penalize treating them as proofs or getting lost in the hypothetical.
6. **Is/ought and naturalistic fallacy** — often mis-invoked; check the invocation is actually apt.
7. **The signature weak argument** — the emotionally powerful case with no principle underneath. The best debaters supply the missing framework or expose its absence in the opponent.
