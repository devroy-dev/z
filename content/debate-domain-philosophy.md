# KNOWLEDGE CODEX 06 — Political Philosophy & Ethics

*A reference briefing on the moral and political-philosophical frameworks that underpin value debates: the ethical theories and theories of justice in full, the vocabulary, the canonical thinkers and thought-experiments, the live debates with arguments and rebuttals per side, and the myths and traps that recur as fact. This codex marks differently from the empirical ones — reward conceptual rigor, consistency, and survival of the standard objection over data. Built so an adjudicator can check whether a framework is being used correctly and a debater can prep either side.*

---

## 1. Scope and the core tension

This domain supplies the **frameworks** other debates borrow: what makes an action right, what a just society owes its members, when the collective may override the individual, and what we owe to those absent from the room (future generations, distant strangers, non-humans). It is the least empirical and most conceptual of the ten — a claim here is judged less by "is it factually true" and more by "is it coherent, consistently applied, and defensible against the strongest objection."

The deep tensions are between **the good and the right** (maximizing welfare vs. respecting constraints/duties), between **liberty and equality**, and between **the individual and the collective**. The signature adjudication trap is the emotionally powerful case with **no underlying principle** — reward the debater who supplies (or dismantles) the framework beneath the intuition.

---

## 2. Core concepts and vocabulary

- **Consequentialism.** Rightness depends only on outcomes; the right act is the one that produces the best consequences. **Utilitarianism** is the leading form: maximize aggregate welfare/happiness (Bentham; refined by Mill). **Act** utilitarianism evaluates each act; **rule** utilitarianism evaluates acts by the rules that, generally followed, maximize welfare.
- **Deontology.** Rightness depends on conformity to duties/rules, not just outcomes; some acts are wrong even if they produce good results. **Kant:** act only on maxims you could will as universal law (the *categorical imperative*), and treat persons always as **ends in themselves, never merely as means** (the humanity formula).
- **Virtue ethics.** Focuses on character rather than acts or outcomes: the right action is what a virtuous person would do; ethics is about cultivating virtues and human flourishing (*eudaimonia*). (Aristotle; the "golden mean" between excess and deficiency.)
- **The separateness of persons.** The core anti-utilitarian objection (associated with Rawls/Nozick): aggregating welfare across people treats humanity as one super-person and can sacrifice individuals for the sum, ignoring that each person's life is distinct and their own.
- **Negative vs. positive liberty.** Isaiah Berlin ("Two Concepts of Liberty," 1958): **negative** liberty is freedom *from* interference; **positive** liberty is freedom *to* be one's own master / self-realization. Berlin warned that positive liberty can be twisted to justify coercion "for your own good."
- **The harm principle.** Mill (*On Liberty*, 1859): the state may restrict a competent adult's liberty only to prevent harm *to others*, not merely to benefit that person (anti-paternalism).
- **Paternalism.** Overriding a person's choices for their own good. **Soft** paternalism intervenes only where choice is non-voluntary (poor information, incapacity); **hard** paternalism overrides even informed voluntary choices.
- **The veil of ignorance / original position.** Rawls's device: principles of justice are those we'd choose for society if we didn't know our own place in it (class, talents, conceptions of the good).
- **Rights as trumps.** Rights that override ordinary utilitarian calculation (Dworkin).
- **The non-identity problem.** Derek Parfit's puzzle: policies that affect *which* people come to exist make it hard to say we've "harmed" future people, since different choices produce different individuals — a hard problem for intergenerational ethics.

## 3. Competing frameworks (in full)

**A. Utilitarianism / consequentialism.** **Claim:** maximize aggregate well-being; the right act/policy is the one with the best net consequences. **Strongest case:** impartial (everyone counts equally), practical (weigh costs and benefits), and reformist (it condemned cruelties defended by tradition — Bentham on the treatment of animals, prisoners, women). **Objections:** (1) it can justify sacrificing an innocent for the greater good (the *separateness of persons*); (2) it is extremely demanding (you must always maximize); (3) welfare is hard to measure and compare; (4) it can license repugnant conclusions if the numbers line up. **Rule utilitarianism** answers some of these by evaluating rules rather than acts, at the cost of collapsing toward either act utilitarianism or rule-worship.

**B. Deontology (Kantian).** **Claim:** morality is a matter of duties and universalizable principles; persons have dignity and may not be used merely as means, regardless of outcomes. **Strongest case:** it protects the individual against being sacrificed for aggregate gain; it grounds inviolable rights; it captures the intuition that some acts (torturing an innocent) are wrong *even if* they'd produce good results. **Objections:** (1) rigid — seems to forbid lying even to a murderer at the door (Kant's own notorious example); (2) duties can conflict with no clear tie-breaker; (3) it can look coldly indifferent to consequences (fiat justitia, ruat caelum).

**C. Virtue ethics.** **Claim:** focus on being a good person (courage, justice, temperance, practical wisdom) rather than on a decision-procedure for acts. **Strongest case:** matches how moral life is actually lived (character, habituation, judgment); avoids the rigidity of rules and the coldness of calculation. **Objections:** (1) it under-specifies what to *do* in a hard case ("do what the virtuous person would do" can be unhelpful); (2) virtues can conflict; (3) it can be culturally relative about what counts as a virtue.

**D. Theories of justice.**
- *Rawlsian justice-as-fairness (A Theory of Justice, 1971).* From the original position behind the veil of ignorance, rational choosers would pick: (1) equal basic liberties for all, and (2) the **difference principle** — social and economic inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged (and attach to positions open under fair equality of opportunity). **Strongest case:** a principled account of when inequality is acceptable, grounded in what we'd choose if impartial. **Objections:** why maximize the *minimum* (Rawls assumes extreme risk-aversion)? Nozick's liberty critique (below); communitarian critiques that the "unencumbered self" behind the veil is a fiction.
- *Nozickian libertarianism (Anarchy, State, and Utopia, 1974).* **Claim:** individuals have inviolable rights (especially self-ownership); a distribution is just if it arose through just acquisition and voluntary transfer, whatever the pattern. Only a **minimal state** (protection, enforcement of contracts) is justified; redistributive taxation is akin to forced labor. The **Wilt Chamberlain argument:** any "patterned" theory of justice (like Rawls's) will be continually upset by voluntary choices, so preserving the pattern requires constant interference with liberty. **Strongest case:** takes individual liberty and voluntary choice seriously; exposes the coercion latent in enforcing distributive patterns. **Objections:** the theory of *just acquisition* is under-specified and historical holdings are often tainted by force/fraud; self-ownership doesn't obviously extend to unlimited property in external resources; it can license severe deprivation.
- *Luck egalitarianism.* **Claim:** inequalities from *unchosen* bad luck (where you're born, natural talents) are unjust and should be corrected; inequalities from *choices* are not. **Objection:** it can be harshly moralistic (abandoning the "imprudent") and struggles to draw the choice/luck line.
- *The capabilities approach (Sen, Nussbaum).* **Claim:** justice is about people's real **freedoms to do and be** things they value (their capabilities), not just resources or welfare — because people convert resources into functioning at different rates (a disabled person needs more to achieve the same mobility). **Strongest case:** captures real freedom better than income metrics; influential in development and human-rights thinking. **Objection:** which capabilities, and who decides the list?
- *Communitarian critique.* (MacIntyre, Sandel, Taylor, Walzer): liberalism's abstract, choosing individual is a fiction; we are constituted by communities, traditions, and shared meanings, and justice must be rooted in them. **Objection:** risks relativism and can shelter oppressive traditions.

**E. Liberty and paternalism.** The Millian harm principle marks the liberal default (adults may harm only themselves), against which paternalists argue that capacity limits, addiction, imperfect information, and internalities justify some intervention. **Libertarian paternalism / "nudging"** (Thaler & Sunstein) tries to steer choices while preserving formal freedom — praised as a middle path, criticized as manipulation and a slippery slope.

The professional debater's move: **name the framework, apply it consistently, and defend it against its standard objection** — and, ideally, offer a reason to prefer it over the strongest rival. Switching frameworks opportunistically (rights when convenient, consequences when convenient) is the cardinal sin here.

## 4. Canonical thinkers, works, and thought-experiments

*(Reasoning anchors, not required citations.)*
- **Bentham** (utilitarianism; the "felicific calculus"), **Mill** (*On Liberty*, 1859; the harm principle; higher/lower pleasures).
- **Kant** (*Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals*, 1785; the categorical imperative; humanity as an end).
- **Aristotle** (*Nicomachean Ethics*; virtue, the mean, *eudaimonia*).
- **Rawls** (*A Theory of Justice*, 1971; veil of ignorance, difference principle) vs. **Nozick** (*Anarchy, State, and Utopia*, 1974; entitlement theory, minimal state, Wilt Chamberlain).
- **Sen & Nussbaum** (the capabilities approach).
- **Berlin** ("Two Concepts of Liberty," 1958).
- **Parfit** (*Reasons and Persons*, 1984; the non-identity problem; population ethics and the "repugnant conclusion").
- **The trolley problem** (Philippa Foot; Judith Jarvis Thomson): the classic pump for the consequentialist/deontological divide (is it permissible to divert a trolley to kill one and save five? to push a person off a bridge to do the same?). Note its *limits*: intuition-pumps test principles but don't settle them, and can mislead.
- **Social-contract lineage:** Hobbes (*Leviathan*, 1651; order over anarchy), Locke (natural rights, government by consent), Rousseau (the general will).

## 5. The central debates — arguments and rebuttals per side

**Debate 1: "A just society should prioritize equality of outcome over equality of opportunity."**
- *Pro (outcome):* Formal equality of opportunity is hollow when starting points are radically unequal — background, wealth, and unchosen luck shape outcomes so heavily that only attention to results delivers real fairness (Rawlsian and luck-egalitarian intuitions).
- *Con (opportunity):* Equalizing outcomes erases desert, agency, and incentive; it punishes effort and choice, and requires continual coercive interference with liberty (Nozick's Wilt Chamberlain point).
- *Adjudication:* reward whoever **defines the equality metric** (welfare? resources? capabilities? real opportunity?) and defends it against the standard objection (incentive/liberty cost for outcome-egalitarians; "hollow formal equality" for opportunity-egalitarians). The strongest debaters often converge on *fair* equality of opportunity plus a floor — reward recognition of that middle ground when earned.

**Debate 2: "The state has no right to prevent competent adults from harming only themselves."**
- *Pro (anti-paternalism):* Sovereign adults own their lives; the harm principle bars coercion for one's "own good"; the power to protect people from themselves is the power to impose the majority's values on private choices.
- *Con (paternalism):* "Purely self-regarding" harm is rarer than it looks (third-party and social costs, dependents); addiction, misinformation, and cognitive bias undermine the "competent, informed choice" premise; soft paternalism intervenes only where autonomy is already compromised.
- *Adjudication:* the strong line **locates the precise condition** that licenses intervention (incapacity, non-voluntariness, externalities, internalities) rather than arguing for blanket liberty or blanket protection. Penalize a Con that can't limit its principle (it would justify banning anything risky) and a Pro that pretends no choice has spillovers.

**Debate 3: "This House prefers a Rawlsian society to a libertarian one."**
- *Pro (Rawls):* Behind the veil, rational people would insure against ending up worst-off; a just society improves the position of the least advantaged, not just the aggregate or the lucky.
- *Con (Nozick):* Justice is about how holdings arose (just acquisition and voluntary transfer), not about matching a pattern; enforcing Rawlsian distribution requires continually overriding free choices and treats people's talents as collective property.
- *Adjudication:* this is a clean framework clash — reward internal consistency and engagement with the *best* version of the rival (does the Rawlsian answer the liberty objection? does the libertarian answer the tainted-acquisition and severe-deprivation objections?). Do not reward whichever society the adjudicator personally prefers.

**Debate 4: "Future generations have rights that can override the preferences of the present."**
- *Pro:* We routinely bind the future (debt, climate, nuclear waste); impartiality across time is as compelling as impartiality across space; discounting future people's interests to near-zero is arbitrary.
- *Con:* The non-identity problem complicates "rights" (our choices determine *who* exists); rights are usually held by actual, identifiable persons; present people have real, weighty claims too.
- *Adjudication:* reward engagement with the **non-identity problem** and with *how* to weigh present against future (discounting, thresholds, a duty to sustain rather than a right held by specific future persons). A debater who has never heard of the non-identity problem is under-equipped here.

## 6. Common myths and factual traps

- **Framework-label without content.** Saying "utilitarian" or "Kantian" as a badge without deploying the actual machinery (aggregation and its objections; universalizability and ends-in-themselves). Catch the name-drop.
- **Opportunistic framework-switching.** Invoking rights when consequences are inconvenient and consequences when rights are inconvenient. The single biggest tell of a weak debater in this domain; a consistent framework beats a convenient one.
- **"Utilitarianism just means the greatest good for the greatest number."** A common oversimplification; the real theory has to grapple with aggregation, the separateness of persons, and act/rule distinctions. The slogan hides the hard part.
- **Kant "forbids lying even to save a life" — as a knockdown.** Kant did (in)famously argue this about the murderer at the door, but it's a contested implication, and modern Kantians have responses; treating it as an obvious reductio is lazy.
- **Treating the trolley problem as settling anything.** Intuition-pumps *test* principles; they don't *prove* them. A debate that becomes trolley-problem ping-pong without extracting the underlying principle is shallow.
- **"Rawls proves we should maximize equality."** Rawls's difference principle permits *inequalities* that benefit the worst-off; it is not a mandate for equal outcomes. Misstating it is common.
- **"Libertarianism = selfishness / anarchy."** Nozick's theory is a rigorous rights-based position with a real argument (self-ownership, just transfer), not merely "greed"; strawmanning it is an error.
- **Confusing negative and positive liberty** — and missing Berlin's warning that positive liberty can be weaponized to coerce people "toward their real selves."
- **"Naturalistic fallacy" abuse.** Not every move from facts to values is fallacious, and the is/ought gap is often invoked incorrectly. Use with care.
- **Assuming a shared value is "self-evident."** The domain's job is to *defend* values, not assert them; "obviously X is good" with no argument is exactly what should lose.

## 7. Sharpest evidence and go-to examples

- **The Wilt Chamberlain argument (Nozick)** — the crispest tool for showing that enforcing any distributive pattern requires continual interference with liberty.
- **The veil of ignorance (Rawls)** — the go-to device for arguing what impartial choosers would pick, and for reframing self-interested claims as questions of fairness.
- **The separateness of persons** — the decisive objection whenever a side is aggregating welfare across people.
- **The capabilities approach** — the sharpest move when "resources/income" metrics miss real freedom (disability, conversion factors).
- **The harm principle + soft/hard paternalism distinction** — the toolkit for any liberty-vs-protection debate.
- **The non-identity problem** — the essential complication for any intergenerational or population-ethics motion.
- **Soft vs. hard paternalism / "nudging"** — the middle-ground vocabulary that dissolves false binaries between total liberty and total control.

## 8. Adjudicator's fact-check notes

Because this domain is conceptual, "fact-checking" means checking **whether frameworks are described and applied correctly**:
1. **Attribution** — Rawls (veil of ignorance, difference principle), Nozick (entitlement, minimal state, Wilt Chamberlain), Kant (categorical imperative, ends-in-themselves), Mill (harm principle), Berlin (two liberties), Sen/Nussbaum (capabilities), Parfit (non-identity). Misattributing a concept is a real error.
2. **The difference principle** permits inequality benefiting the worst-off — not equal outcomes. Flag misstatements.
3. **Consistency** — the primary marker. A debater who switches frameworks opportunistically should lose ground even if each individual point sounds good.
4. **Survival of the standard objection** — reward a framework defended against its known weakness (utilitarianism vs. the separateness of persons; Kant vs. rigidity; Rawls vs. the liberty objection; Nozick vs. tainted acquisition).
5. **Thought-experiments** — reward using them to *extract a principle*, penalize treating them as proofs or getting lost in the hypothetical.
6. **The signature weak argument** — the emotionally powerful case with no principle underneath. The best debaters supply the missing framework or expose its absence in the opponent.
