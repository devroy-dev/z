# KNOWLEDGE CODEX 03 — Geopolitics & the Changing World Order

*A deep reference briefing on international relations: the competing theoretical schools in full, the vocabulary of power and order, the factual record from the Peloponnesian War to the present multipolar turn, the live debates with arguments and rebuttals per side, and the myths and traps that recur as fact. Built so an adjudicator can verify an in-round claim and a debater can prepare either side.*

---

## 1. Scope and the core tension

This domain is about **power among states in a world with no overarching authority.** Because the international system is *anarchic* — no world government to enforce agreements — every actor must ultimately provide for its own security, and that single structural fact generates most of the field's debates: when the pursuit of security by one state makes others less secure; whether order comes from balance, hegemony, or institutions; and whether the current shift from US-led unipolarity toward a contested multipolarity makes the world safer or more dangerous.

The deep tension is between **power and cooperation.** Realists say structure forces states to compete regardless of intentions; liberals say institutions and interdependence let states escape the trap; constructivists say the "trap" itself is a social construction that can be remade. A second tension is between **order and justice** — whether stability (sovereignty, non-interference) should yield when a state commits atrocities against its own people. A debater who cannot say which of these logics they are invoking is arguing without a compass, because the same fact — say, China's rise — reads as an inevitable power transition (realism), a manageable institutional adjustment (liberalism), or a contest of identities and legitimacy (constructivism).

## 2. Core concepts and vocabulary

- **Anarchy.** Not chaos, but the *absence of a central authority* above states. The structural premise of most IR theory.
- **Self-help.** In anarchy, states can rely ultimately only on themselves for survival, so they accumulate power and hedge.
- **The security dilemma.** Coined by John Herz; developed by Robert Jervis ("Cooperation Under the Security Dilemma," 1978). Measures one state takes to feel safe (arms, alliances) make others feel threatened, prompting countermeasures — an unintended spiral that can produce conflict between states that both prefer peace. Jervis distinguished a **spiral model** (arming provokes) from a **deterrence model** (weakness provokes); knowing *which* situation you are in is the crux, and getting it wrong is how wars start.
- **Balance of power.** States align to prevent any one power from dominating. **Balancing** (joining against the strong) vs. **bandwagoning** (joining the strong). In multipolar systems, **buck-passing** (hoping someone else checks the threat) and **chain-ganging** (being dragged into an ally's war) are characteristic failure modes.
- **Polarity.** The number of great powers: **unipolar** (one hegemon), **bipolar** (two), **multipolar** (three or more). Each is argued to have different stability properties.
- **Power (its forms).** Joseph Nye's distinction: **hard power** (coercion — military, economic), **soft power** (attraction — culture, values, legitimacy), and **smart power** (combining them).
- **Hegemonic stability theory.** Open trade and order are easier to sustain when one dominant power underwrites them and pays the disproportionate costs (Kindleberger, Gilpin); the hegemon's decline risks disorder.
- **Power transition theory.** A.F.K. Organski: war is likeliest when a *dissatisfied* rising power approaches parity with the ruling power. The pop-culture version is Graham Allison's **"Thucydides Trap"** (*Destined for War*, 2017).
- **Deterrence.** Preventing an action by threatening unacceptable retaliation; requires **credibility** and **capability.** Key ideas: **second-strike capability** (retaliating after absorbing a first strike), **mutually assured destruction (MAD)**, and **extended deterrence** (protecting allies under one's nuclear umbrella — which raises a credibility problem: will A really risk its cities for B?). Thomas Schelling (*Arms and Influence*, 1966) is foundational.
- **The stability–instability paradox.** Nuclear parity makes all-out war between two nuclear states less likely but may make *limited* conflict and brinkmanship *more* likely, because both believe escalation will be capped.
- **Alliance dilemmas.** Glenn Snyder's **entrapment** (being dragged into an ally's conflict) vs. **abandonment** (being left exposed when you need them) — the two fears that haunt every alliance.
- **Sovereignty.** Supreme authority within territory and formal equality among states. Its "Westphalian" pedigree is itself contested (see myths).
- **Spheres of influence vs. universal rules.** Two rival ordering principles: great powers dominating their neighborhoods, vs. a single set of rules applied to all.
- **The liberal international order.** The post-1945 (and especially post-1991) system of open trade, multilateral institutions, and (aspirationally) liberal norms, underwritten by US power — the thing now widely argued to be fraying.

## 3. Competing schools (in full)

**A. Realism.** **Claim:** states are the main actors; they pursue power and security in an anarchic, self-help system; morality and institutions are thin constraints. **Variants:** *Classical realism* (Hans Morgenthau, *Politics Among Nations*, 1948; and, further back, Thucydides) roots competition in human nature and the drive for power ("the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must" — the Melian Dialogue). *Neorealism/structural realism* (Kenneth Waltz, *Theory of International Politics*, 1979) roots it in the *structure* of anarchy, not human nature, and argues **bipolarity is the most stable** configuration (two players, clear balancing, fewer miscalculations). *Offensive realism* (John Mearsheimer, *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics*, 2001): great powers maximize power and seek regional hegemony, making US–China rivalry structurally likely. *Defensive realism*: states seek *enough* security, not maximal power, so restraint is often rational and the security dilemma is manageable. *Neoclassical realism* adds domestic variables (perception, state capacity) between structure and outcome. **Strongest case:** explains recurring balancing, arms races, and great-power rivalry across very different cultures and eras. **Standard objection:** under-predicts cooperation, the durable democratic peace, and the resilience of institutions; treats state preferences as fixed and struggles with change (it did not predict the peaceful end of the Cold War).

**B. Liberalism / liberal institutionalism.** **Claim:** cooperation is achievable; institutions, trade interdependence, and domestic politics shape behavior. **Strands (Doyle):** *Institutional liberalism* (Robert Keohane, *After Hegemony*, 1984; Keohane & Nye, *Power and Interdependence*, 1977) — institutions reduce transaction costs, provide information, and raise the cost of defection, so states cooperate even in anarchy. *Commercial liberalism* — trade interdependence raises the cost of war. *Republican liberalism / democratic peace* (Michael Doyle, building on Kant's *Perpetual Peace*, 1795) — democracies very rarely fight *each other*, a robust empirical regularity, though the *monadic* claim that democracies are generally more peaceful is weaker. **Strongest case:** the density and durability of postwar institutions and the near-absence of war among mature democracies. **Standard objection:** institutions may reflect rather than constrain power (Mearsheimer's "The False Promise of International Institutions," 1994–95); great powers ignore rules when core interests demand.

**C. Constructivism.** **Claim:** interests and identities are socially constructed, not fixed by structure. Alexander Wendt's "Anarchy Is What States Make of It" (1992): the same anarchy can produce a Hobbesian, Lockean, or Kantian *culture* depending on shared ideas. **Strongest case:** it explains change realism cannot — the end of the Cold War without war, the transformation of enemies into partners, the spread of norms (against conquest, against chemical weapons). **Standard objection:** strong on *how ideas matter* but weaker on prediction, and it can under-weight material power.

**D. The English School.** **Claim:** there is an "international society" of states bound by shared rules and institutions — sovereignty, diplomacy, international law, the balance of power — even under anarchy (Hedley Bull, *The Anarchical Society*, 1977). A middle path between the "international system" of pure realism and the "world community" of cosmopolitan liberalism.

**E. Marxist / world-systems and critical theories.** **Claim:** the international order reflects the interests of dominant classes and core economies; power and economics are inseparable (Wallerstein). Useful for the "institutions serve the strong" critique. **Feminist and postcolonial IR** further argue that mainstream theory naturalizes particular (gendered, Western) vantage points and omits whole categories of actors and harms.

**The professional move** is rarely to declare for one school but to identify **which logic the specific case activates** — a structural rivalry (realism), an institutionalized cooperation problem (liberalism), or a norm shift (constructivism) — and to weigh material power against ideas and institutions honestly. A debater who treats "realism is just true" or "institutions solve everything" is over-reaching.

## 4. The factual record

*(Firm dates are reliable. Judgments about "stability," "decline," and "order" are contested framings, not facts — present them as arguments.)*

- **The Peloponnesian War and the Melian Dialogue (5th century BCE).** Thucydides' account is the founding text of realism and the origin of the "rising power vs. ruling power" and "might makes right" motifs.
- **The Peace of Westphalia (1648).** Conventionally cited as the origin of the sovereign-state system; scholars (e.g. Andreas Osiander) argue "Westphalian sovereignty" is partly a later myth (see §6).
- **The Congress of Vienna (1815) and the Concert of Europe.** The post-Napoleonic balance-of-power settlement that gave Europe an unusual stretch without general war — the classic case for managed multipolar balance. **Bismarck's alliance system** (later 19th century) is the case study in how skillful balancing can preserve peace — and how its collapse can unleash war.
- **The origins of the First World War (1914).** The central battleground for **overdetermination vs. contingency**: was the system so primed (alliance blocs, arms races, mobilization timetables) that war was near-inevitable, or did the assassination and specific choices genuinely tip it? Also the classic case of **chain-ganging**.
- **The interwar period and the League of Nations (1919–1939).** The cautionary tale of multipolar instability, failed collective security, and appeasement — heavily cited by multipolarity's critics.
- **The Cold War (roughly 1947–1991).** US–Soviet bipolarity under nuclear deterrence. Containment (George Kennan's "Long Telegram," 1946) framed US strategy. John Lewis Gaddis called the absence of great-power war the **"long peace."** Near-catastrophes — the **Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)**, the **1983 Able Archer scare**, the **1983 Petrov incident** — are the standard evidence that the peace ran partly on luck. Proxy wars, détente, and the nuclear arms race defined the era.
- **The end of the Cold War (1989–1991).** The USSR's collapse without great-power war — constructivism's trump card against pure structural realism, which largely failed to predict it.
- **The unipolar moment.** With the USSR gone, the US became the sole superpower — Charles Krauthammer's "unipolar moment" (1990–91). William Wohlforth argued unipolarity could be *stable and durable*. Francis Fukuyama's "The End of History?" (1989; book 1992) argued liberal democracy had won the *ideological* contest (routinely strawmanned — see §6).
- **9/11 (2001) and the "war on terror."** Redirected US strategy toward counter-terrorism and costly interventions.
- **The rise of China.** Deng-era growth, **WTO accession (2001)**, and rapid economic and military expansion made China a peer competitor — the central fact reshaping the system.
- **The 2008 global financial crisis** dented confidence in the US-led economic model and accelerated talk of relative decline and multipolarity.
- **Russia's revisionism.** Georgia (2008), the annexation of Crimea (2014), and the full-scale invasion of Ukraine (2022) — the sharpest recent challenge to the norm against territorial conquest, and a live test of deterrence, sanctions, and alliance credibility.
- **Institutional strain.** The **WTO Appellate Body** was effectively paralyzed from December 2019 after the US blocked new appointments; the **UN Security Council** remains frequently deadlocked by P5 vetoes. The **Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty (NPT)** regime persists but is strained by new and aspiring nuclear states.
- **The multipolar turn.** BRICS expansion, "middle powers" hedging, and Global South "non-alignment 2.0" mark a system no longer cleanly unipolar.
- **Sanctions.** The scholarly record (e.g. Hufbauer and colleagues, *Economic Sanctions Reconsidered*) suggests sanctions achieve their stated aims perhaps around a third of the time, with wide variation and serious methodological disputes — i.e. neither "sanctions work" nor "sanctions never work" is defensible as stated.

## 5. The central debates — arguments and rebuttals per side

**Debate 1: "This House believes a multipolar world is more dangerous than a unipolar one."**
- *Pro (more dangerous):* More great powers means more dyads that can miscalculate, more shifting alliances (interwar chain-ganging and buck-passing), and no single underwriter of order; history's multipolar eras produced the world wars.
- *Con:* Unipolarity breeds unchecked hegemonic overreach and resentment; balanced multipolarity restrains any single aggressor (the Concert of Europe). Waltz argued *bipolarity* — not unipolarity — was most stable, so "unipolar = safe" is not obvious, and unipolar overreach has its own war record.
- *Adjudication:* reward the side that supplies the **mechanism** by which a given polarity causes war or peace (miscalculation, overreach, balancing failure), not gestures at "balance" or "primacy." Note the debate often hides a definitional move about *which* alternative pole-structure is the comparison.

**Debate 2: "This House believes international institutions do more to constrain the weak than the strong."**
- *Pro:* Great powers wield vetoes, ignore adverse rulings, and shape rules to their advantage; institutions bind small states while the powerful defect at will (Mearsheimer; the world-systems critique).
- *Con:* Institutions raise the *cost* of great-power defection (reputational, coordination), give small states voice and information they'd never get bilaterally, and have restrained even powerful states on many second-order issues (Keohane).
- *Adjudication:* the strong line concedes institutions are **conditional** — effective where great-power interests partly align, weak where they sharply conflict. Reward whoever specifies *which* institution and *which* issue rather than generalizing.

**Debate 3: "This House believes the US and China are destined for conflict" (the Thucydides Trap).**
- *Pro:* Power-transition logic — a dissatisfied riser nearing parity with an anxious incumbent is the classic recipe for war; structural pressure outweighs good intentions.
- *Con:* Nuclear deterrence, deep economic interdependence, and the absence of a WWI-type territorial casus belli make major war very costly and avoidable; Allison's "16 cases" are selectively chosen and the "trap" is a heuristic, not a law.
- *Adjudication:* weigh **structural pressure** against **deterrence + interdependence.** Penalize treating the Thucydides Trap as deterministic, *and* penalize dismissing structural risk entirely.

**Debate 4: "This House, as a middle power, would formally align with a great-power bloc rather than hedge."**
- *Pro (align):* Alignment buys credible security guarantees and market access; hedging leaves a state exposed and friendless in a crisis (the abandonment fear).
- *Con (hedge):* Hedging preserves autonomy, avoids being dragged into others' wars (the entrapment fear), and maximizes leverage by keeping options open (India's strategic autonomy; ASEAN centrality).
- *Adjudication:* judge against the *specific* state's geography, threats, and economic ties — a good debater reasons from that state's actual position and from the entrapment/abandonment trade-off, not a generic template.

**Debate 5: "This House believes the rules-based liberal international order is worth defending."**
- *Pro:* It underwrote the longest great-power peace and the largest rise in prosperity in history; its erosion invites spheres-of-influence bullying of the weak.
- *Con:* The "order" was always selective — it constrained the weak while its architect broke its own rules — and defending it can mean defending hegemony dressed as principle; a genuinely multipolar order may be fairer.
- *Adjudication:* separate the *descriptive* question (how rules-based was it, really?) from the *normative* one (is it worth defending vs. the realistic alternative?). Reward whoever names the concrete alternative order rather than debating an abstraction.

## 6. Common myths and factual traps

- **The Westphalian myth.** The claim that 1648 cleanly created modern sovereignty is contested; sovereignty evolved over centuries, and "Westphalian sovereignty" is partly a 20th-century retrofit. Don't let it be asserted as a tidy origin point.
- **"Democracies never go to war."** The robust finding is *dyadic* — mature democracies rarely fight *each other.* The *monadic* claim (democracies are generally more peaceful) is weaker, and "democracy" definitions do heavy lifting. Overstating this is a common error.
- **The Thucydides Trap as an iron law.** A useful heuristic, but the sample is selective and the causation contested; it describes a *risk*, not a destiny. Catch debaters who cite it as proof of inevitability.
- **"Sanctions don't work" / "sanctions work."** Both too broad; the record is mixed and highly case-dependent (targeted vs. comprehensive; against what goal). Reward disaggregation.
- **Fukuyama's "end of history" strawman.** He did not claim conflict or events would stop; he argued liberal democracy had won the *ideological* contest as the endpoint of political legitimacy. "He said nothing would ever happen again" is a misreading.
- **"Deterrence always works."** The Cold War's near-misses show deterrence *held* but partly on luck; presenting it as fail-safe ignores Cuba 1962 and 1983. Equally, "deterrence never works" ignores the great-power long peace.
- **Conflating power and legitimacy.** A state *can* enforce a rule (power) without that rule being *accepted* (legitimacy); good debaters keep them distinct, and much soft-power analysis turns on the difference.
- **Spiral vs. deterrence model confusion.** Treating every rival as either an aggressor to be deterred or a frightened state to be reassured — when the whole difficulty (Jervis) is telling which you face. A debater who assumes without argument is vulnerable.
- **Treating one IR school as obviously correct.** Realism, liberalism, and constructivism each capture part of reality; dismissing the others wholesale is over-reaching.

## 7. Sharpest evidence and go-to examples

- **The Cuban Missile Crisis (1962)** — the definitive case for deterrence, brinkmanship, and the stability–instability paradox at once.
- **The Concert of Europe / Bismarck's system** — the go-to for "managed multipolar balance can keep the peace" (and how its collapse ends it).
- **The origins of WWI** — the interwar's prelude and the classic overdetermination-vs-contingency and chain-ganging case.
- **The peaceful end of the Cold War (1989–91)** — constructivism's trump against pure structural realism.
- **The WTO Appellate Body paralysis (2019– )** — the concrete, current illustration of a rules-based order fraying.
- **India's strategic autonomy / non-alignment 2.0** — the exemplar of successful middle-power hedging.
- **Russia's 2014 and 2022 actions against Ukraine** — the sharpest recent test of the conquest norm, deterrence, sanctions, and alliance credibility.

## 8. Adjudicator's fact-check notes

Verify these, the domain's most error-prone claims:
1. **Theory attribution** — Waltz (structure/bipolarity), Mearsheimer (offensive realism/"false promise"), Keohane (institutionalism), Nye (soft power), Doyle (democratic peace), Wendt (constructivism), Bull (English School), Allison (Thucydides Trap), Jervis (spiral vs. deterrence). Misattribution is a real error.
2. **"Democracies never fight each other"** — accept the careful *dyadic* version; flag the overstated *monadic* one.
3. **Deterrence claims** — neither "always works" nor "never works"; the Cold War record is "held, with near-misses."
4. **Sanctions efficacy** — a contested ~one-third-success ballpark with big caveats; penalize confident universal claims either way.
5. **Dates** — Westphalia 1648, Vienna 1815, WWI 1914, Cold War end 1991, China WTO 2001, Crimea 2014, Ukraine invasion 2022, WTO Appellate Body paralysis 2019.
6. **Framing vs. fact** — "US decline," "the liberal order is collapsing," "the world is multipolar now" are *contested interpretations*, not settled facts; reward debaters who argue them and be wary of those who assert them.
7. **The Thucydides Trap and the Westphalian myth** — the two most-cited items in the domain and the two most often overstated; handle with care.
